# Decision Making

## Decision Quality Framework

### The Two-by-Two of Decisions
*(Jeff Bezos / Amazon leadership principles)*
- **Reversible + low stakes**: delegate or decide fast — don't waste cognitive load here
- **Irreversible + high stakes**: slow down, gather data, seek input, sleep on it
- **The CEO trap**: applying the same deliberation to everything (paralysis) or the same speed to everything (irreversible mistakes)
- **Coaching prompt**: "Is this a one-way door or a two-way door? If it's two-way, what are you waiting for?"

### What Would Need to Be True?
Instead of asking "is this a good idea?" ask "what would need to be true for this to be a good idea?" This:
- Separates facts from assumptions
- Creates falsifiable conditions you can test
- Prevents confirmation bias from dominating

### Deciding vs Optimising
CEOs often delay decisions because they're looking for the optimal answer when a good answer made now and executed well beats an optimal answer made later. The cost of indecision is rarely zero.
- Signs of over-optimising: the same decision is discussed in multiple meetings without resolution; "we need more data" is recurring; options keep getting added rather than narrowed
- Rule of thumb: if you've gathered input from the right 3–4 people and still can't decide, you're not missing data — you're avoiding the trade-off

### Pre-Mortem
Before a big decision, spend 10 minutes on: "Assume this fails. It's 12 months from now and it went badly. What happened?" This:
- Surfaces risks that optimism suppresses
- Generates specific contingency thinking
- Doesn't prevent you from deciding — it makes the decision more robust

### Cognitive Biases to Watch
- **Sunk cost**: "we've invested too much to stop now" — past investment is irrelevant to future decisions
- **Availability bias**: over-weighting the most recent or vivid data
- **Overconfidence**: especially in CEO-level roles where feedback loops are slow and people tell you what you want to hear
- **Group think**: in rooms where challenging the CEO feels risky, decisions look unanimous when they're not

## Decision Hygiene
- Write it down: capture decisions with context and rationale (prevents revisiting, creates institutional memory)
- Assign one owner: decisions made by a group often end up owned by no one
- Time-box deliberation: agree upfront how long a decision will be open before it's forced closed
- Communicate the "why": people execute better on decisions they understand, even if they disagree
