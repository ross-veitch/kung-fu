# Executive Presence

The ability to command a room, earn credibility quickly, and communicate in a way that makes people want to follow you — without performing or pretending.

---

## What executive presence actually is

Not appearance. Not volume. Not aggression.

Executive presence is the combination of **credibility** (do you know what you're talking about?), **intention** (are you actually trying to help, or just trying to look good?), and **composure** (can you stay grounded when things get hard?).

The shorthand that works: *"Do people feel more capable or less capable after talking to you?"* That's the measure.

---

## The three dimensions

### 1. Composure under pressure

High-pressure situations — board scrutiny, investor pushback, a direct report melting down — reveal who people actually are. The leader who gets defensive, overexplains, or shuts down is communicating that they can't handle scrutiny. The leader who stays curious and grounded is communicating that they can.

**Physiological foundation**: Composure is mostly a nervous system skill. Slow exhalation (4-count in, 8-count out) activates the parasympathetic response within seconds. This is not metaphorical — it is a physiological interrupt.

**Cognitive reframe**: Treat the challenge as information, not as an attack. "What is this person actually worried about?" shifts you from defensive mode to curious mode. Curiosity and defensiveness cannot coexist.

**The pause**: Saying nothing for 2–3 seconds before responding is one of the highest-leverage executive presence moves. It signals confidence (you don't need to fill silence), improves the quality of the response, and is almost universally read as authority by the room.

---

### 2. Communication that commands attention

**Clarity over comprehensiveness**: Most leaders speak too much. The underlying fear is that if you leave something out, people will think you don't know it. The opposite is true — people who can say it in three sentences know it better than people who need twelve. The ability to compress signals mastery.

**Bottom line first**: The most common executive communication failure is leading with context before conclusion. Boards, investors, and senior leaders want the answer, then the reasoning — not the journey to the answer. Start with what you want them to know, then support it.

**Questions that open, not close**: The best executive communicators are often the people asking the most precise questions, not the people with the most answers. "What would have to be true for this to work?" is a more powerful contribution to a strategy discussion than another point in the same direction.

**Silence as tool**: The willingness to sit with silence — after asking a question, after making a point — is rare and powerful. Most people rush to fill silence. Don't. The person who is comfortable in silence controls the pace of the conversation.

---

### 3. Presence (as in: being actually here)

The most underrated executive presence skill is genuine attention. Most people in a room are 60% present and 40% composing their next comment or checking their mental agenda. The person who is genuinely, completely attentive — tracking body language, noticing what's not being said, hearing the emotional content under the words — is read as having exceptional presence.

**Practical discipline**: Before entering a high-stakes conversation, spend 60 seconds with one simple intention: "I am going to notice what is actually happening in this room." Not plan. Not perform. Notice.

**Eye contact that lands**: Not staring, not scanning. Making contact with one person for 3–5 seconds while making a point, then moving to the next person. This makes each individual feel spoken to directly.

## Common failure modes

| Failure mode | What it signals | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| Overexplaining | Anxiety about being misunderstood; low trust in the audience | Cut by 50% — if they need more, they'll ask |
| Defensive response to challenge | Threat response; confusing self-worth with position | Pause, reframe as information: "What's the concern underneath this?" |
| Vocal fry / uptalk | Uncertainty, seeking approval | Statements end with a falling tone; practise recording and reviewing |
| Filling every silence | Discomfort; fear silence will be read as having nothing to say | Practise sitting in silence during low-stakes conversations first |
| Hedging everything | Risk aversion; fear of being wrong | Have a view. Be willing to be wrong. Credibility comes from having a perspective, not from being always right. |
| Physical shrinking (posture, movement) | Subservience, anxiety | Take up appropriate space; feet planted, shoulders back — this is not performance, it changes your internal state first |

## High-leverage coaching prompts

**After a difficult conversation:**
> "What were you trying to protect by doing/saying that?"
> "What would you do differently if you weren't worried about how it looked?"

**Before a high-stakes presentation:**
> "What do you want them to feel when they leave the room? Not think — feel."
> "What's the one thing you want them to remember?"

**On communication style:**
> "What did you not say that you actually thought?"
> "Where were you managing impressions instead of communicating?"
