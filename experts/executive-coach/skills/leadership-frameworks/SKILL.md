# Leadership Frameworks

Mental models and frameworks for understanding leadership at scale.

## Founder Mode vs Manager Mode
*(Paul Graham / Ben Horowitz framing)*
- **Founder mode**: high-context, skip-level involvement, pattern-matching from firsthand knowledge — essential at early stages and during crises
- **Manager mode**: managing through a hierarchy, trusting direct reports to own their domains — necessary as the org scales and can't all be held in one head
- The failure mode: staying in founder mode when the org needs manager mode (bottleneck, micromanagement, team can't grow). And the reverse: switching to manager mode too early, losing touch with product and culture.
- **Coaching prompt**: "What mode are you in right now? What's the evidence? What would the org tell you if it could?"

## Leadership Stage Transitions
*(Based on Kegan adult development + Mochary)*
Leaders typically get stuck at these transitions:
1. **Operator → Manager**: learning to do through others rather than directly
2. **Manager → Executive**: managing managers, not just individual contributors; setting vision rather than running plays
3. **Executive → CEO**: the whole org, board, investors, external narrative, culture as strategy — accountable for everything, in control of almost nothing
- Each transition requires letting go of what made you successful before
- **Coaching prompt**: "What skill that got you here might be limiting you now?"

## The Drama Triangle → Empowerment Triangle
*(Karpman / Conscious Leadership)*
- Drama roles: Victim ("this is happening to me"), Villain ("it's their fault"), Hero ("I'll save everyone")
- Empowerment roles: Creator ("what do I want?"), Challenger ("how can I help them grow?"), Coach ("here's support without rescue")
- High-performing executives still slip into drama — often as Hero (doing others' work) or Victim (powerless against external forces)
- **Coaching prompt**: "Which role are you playing in this situation right now?"

## The Mochary Method Core Habits
*(Matt Mochary — practical CEO operating system)*
- **Emotion logging**: name the emotion before making a decision or sending a message while activated
- **Weekly 1:1s with direct reports**: structured — what went well, what to improve, what do you need from me
- **Topgrading**: honest assessment of whether each person on the team is an A, B, or C player — and acting accordingly
- **Decision capture**: write it down when the decision is made (context, decision, rationale) — prevents revisiting and builds institutional memory
- **Feedback culture**: make it safe and expected at all levels

## Immunity to Change
*(Kegan & Lahey)*
The gap between what leaders say they want to do and what they actually do is not a gap in willpower or knowledge — it's often a hidden competing commitment. The structure:
1. Improvement goal (what I say I want)
2. Behaviours that work against it (what I actually do)
3. Hidden competing commitment (what I'm implicitly protecting)
4. Big assumption (the belief that makes the competing commitment feel necessary)
- **Coaching prompt**: "You said you'd [X] and you didn't. What were you protecting by not doing it?"
