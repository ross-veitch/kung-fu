# Team Dynamics

## Building a Leadership Team That Works

The CEO's most leveraged act is not a product decision or a strategy call — it's building the right leadership team. A weak leadership team compounds every problem; a strong one multiplies every opportunity.

### The A/B/C Framework
*(Topgrading — Bradford Smart)*
- **A players**: would enthusiastically rehire; proactively raise the bar around them; rare
- **B players**: solid contributors but not raising the ceiling; often occupying roles that need an A
- **C players**: costing more in management overhead, team morale, and missed outcomes than they contribute
- CEOs routinely wait too long to act on C players (and sometimes B players in critical roles). The cost: team credibility, retention of A players, and the CEO's own energy.
- **Coaching prompt**: "If you were starting over and could hire anyone for each role, who would you re-hire without hesitation?"

### The Five Dysfunctions
*(Patrick Lencioni)*
Teams fail predictably, in this order:
1. Absence of trust → no vulnerability, no real conversation
2. Fear of conflict → artificial harmony, decisions revisited in the hallway
3. Lack of commitment → ambiguous decisions, no buy-in
4. Avoidance of accountability → standards drift, peers don't challenge each other
5. Inattention to results → individual agendas displace team outcomes

- Most executive teams operate at dysfunction 2 or 3 and mistake politeness for health
- Fix 1 first — everything else is downstream of psychological safety between the CEO and direct reports

### Feedback Culture
The quality of feedback flowing in an org is a proxy for its health. Signs of a weak feedback culture:
- People find out problems are serious only when someone quits or something breaks
- Leaders only receive positive feedback (or silence)
- Performance reviews surface nothing surprising

Building a feedback culture:
1. Model it — the CEO gives AND asks for direct feedback publicly
2. Make it normal — build feedback into 1:1 rhythms, not just annual reviews
3. Reward the messenger — never punish someone for honest bad news

## Org Design Principles
- **Form follows function**: structure the org around the work, not around people's comfort or tenure
- **Spans and layers**: too wide = CEO becomes a bottleneck; too tall = too many layers slow decisions and signal mistrust
- **Single-threaded ownership**: each major outcome has one owner who is accountable — not a committee
- **Avoid the "brilliant jerk" trap**: no amount of individual output is worth the systemic cost of someone who corrodes trust, safety, and team culture
