# /future-deck — Futures-Facing Presentation Structure

**Purpose**: Structure a futures-facing presentation — for a board, an investor, a leadership team, or an external audience. This is the architecture of the argument, not the visual design.

**When to use**: Board strategy sessions, investor presentations with a long-range thesis, executive offsite future-of-the-business sessions, external conference talks.

---

## The core narrative challenge

Most futures presentations fail because they:
1. Are too abstract (structural forces without implications for *this* company)
2. Are too predictive (presents one future as inevitable; kills engagement when audience disagrees)
3. Are too safe (hedges everything; leaves no call to action)
4. Are too long (the audience was with you for 10 minutes and you lost them at minute 25)

A great futures presentation is *specific, provocative, and actionable*. It should make the audience feel something — not just understand something.

---

## Narrative arc options

### Arc A: The Structural Shift Deck
*For: board strategy sessions, investor presentations*

**Opening**: The world has changed structurally, and here's evidence most people aren't taking seriously yet.
**Rising action**: Map the structural forces and show their second-order effects.
**Conflict**: Our current strategy was built for a world that's changing. Here's where we're exposed.
**Resolution**: Here's what we do about it — specific, timed, resourced.
**Call to action**: Decision(s) needed from this audience.

---

### Arc B: The Three Futures Deck
*For: strategy workshops, scenario planning sessions*

**Opening**: We don't know which future is coming. But we can map the possibility space.
**Three scenarios**: Three vivid, named futures — each specific and internally consistent.
**Implications per scenario**: What does each world require of us?
**Robust strategy**: What's the strategy that performs reasonably well across all three?
**Bets and options**: Where should we commit; where should we keep options open?

---

### Arc C: The Opportunity Deck
*For: new initiative pitches, investment theses, partner presentations*

**Opening**: A structural shift creates a window — and here's why now is the time.
**The shift**: Deep evidence that something structural is changing.
**The window**: Why this moment is different from 3 years ago and from 3 years from now.
**The play**: What specifically we're doing to capture this opportunity.
**The downside**: Honest risk cases and how we've designed against them.
**The ask**: Capital / partnership / approval needed.

---

## Slide architecture (Arc A — most common)

```
1. Opening provocation (1 slide)
   - One stark, specific statement that reframes how the audience should see the next 10 minutes

2. What's changing structurally (2–3 slides)
   - 3 structural forces, each with specific evidence
   - Not "AI is growing" but "inference cost fell 100× in 24 months and is still falling — this is a cost curve event, not a product event"

3. Second-order effects (1–2 slides)
   - What changes *because* of the structural shift that most aren't tracking
   - Use design fiction or specific examples to make it real

4. Our exposure (1–2 slides)
   - Where does the structural change intersect our current strategy?
   - What risks are we carrying that we haven't explicitly acknowledged?
   - What opportunities does this create that we're not positioned for?

5. Strategic response (2–3 slides)
   - What we're doing (or proposing to do) differently
   - Timeline and resource requirements
   - What success looks like and when

6. Signals we're watching (1 slide)
   - 3–5 specific leading indicators that will tell us if the thesis is materialising
   - Shows the board we have epistemic humility and a monitoring process

7. Decision(s) needed (1 slide)
   - Specific. Time-bound. Clear ask.
```

---

## Presentation quality rules

**Density over volume**: 10 dense slides beat 30 thin slides every time. Each slide should earn its place.

**Specificity over generality**: "AI inference costs fell 100× in 24 months and will fall another 50× in the next 36 months" beats "AI is getting cheaper and more powerful."

**Show the work, don't just assert**: the structural forces need evidence. Not anecdote — structural data. Cost curves, adoption curves, regulatory filings, demographic data.

**Name the uncertainty**: telling the board what you don't know is more credible than pretending you do. A futures presentation that acknowledges uncertainty and shows how you've designed against it is more trustworthy than one that presents a single inevitable future.

**The call to action is the point**: the entire presentation exists to earn the right to make a specific ask. Don't bury it or soften it.

## Output format

```
PRESENTATION TITLE: [something specific, not "The Future of X"]
AUDIENCE: [board / investors / leadership team / external]
ARC: [A: Structural Shift / B: Three Futures / C: Opportunity]
LENGTH: [target slide count]

NARRATIVE OUTLINE:
  Slide 1: [Opening provocation — specific text]
  Slides 2–3: [Structural forces — with specific evidence sources]
  Slides 4–5: [Second-order effects / scenario implications]
  Slides 6–7: [Our exposure / strategic response]
  Slide 8: [Signals to monitor]
  Slide 9: [Decision ask]

SPEAKER NOTES (key points per section):
  [Opening]: [what the presenter should say that isn't on the slide]
  [Structural forces]: [key verbal nuances]
  [Ask]: [exact wording of the ask]

LIKELY PUSHBACK AND RESPONSES:
  "[Objection]" → [response]
```
