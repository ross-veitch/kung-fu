# /design-fiction — Future Scenario Storytelling

**Purpose**: Write a vivid scene from inside a future scenario to make it emotionally real for an audience. Design fiction bypasses intellectual defence mechanisms ("that won't happen") by showing rather than arguing. When people experience a scenario, they engage with it differently than when they analyse it.

**When to use**: Strategy workshops, executive presentations, investor communications, futures-facing board decks, team alignment around a long-range thesis.

**Method base**: Dunne & Raby's design fiction / diegetic prototype approach — using plausible fictional artefacts and scenes to inhabit a possible future rather than describe it.

---

## What makes design fiction work

The best design fiction:
- **Is specific**: not "people use AI to book travel" but "Amara opens her phone at 7am. Her travel agent — an AI called Marco that she's used for 3 years — has already rebooked her flight after the algorithm detected a 73% chance of a departure delay."
- **Has texture**: the world has brands, prices, small frictions, familiar problems solved in unfamiliar ways
- **Shows winners and losers**: good fiction shows who benefits and who doesn't — it's not utopian or dystopian, just specific
- **Includes the artefacts**: what does the interface look like? What does the notification say? What does the receipt show?
- **Reveals the assumption**: after the scene, the structural assumption it makes explicit is the real payload

## Format options

### Option A: The Day-in-the-Life Scene
Follow a specific person through one day at the target horizon. Show how their behaviour has changed — what's frictionless that wasn't, what new anxieties they have, what they take for granted.

Good for: consumer product strategy, marketplace dynamics, behaviour shifts

### Option B: The News Article
Write a tech journalism piece from the target year. "Company X has acquired Company Y in a deal that analysts say marks the consolidation of the [sector] market, which has been transformed by..." The news article form forces specificity about what has already happened.

Good for: competitive landscape evolution, M&A strategy, regulatory environment

### Option C: The Product Screenshot / Interface Description
Describe an interface in the target year. What does the app screen look like? What does it say? What choices does it offer? What does it not offer (because the problem is solved differently)?

Good for: product strategy, UX vision, demonstrating AI-mediated experiences

### Option D: The Overheard Conversation
Two people in the target year discussing something casually that reveals how the world works. A conversation at a conference, a customer call, a board room discussion.

Good for: team strategy alignment, revealing strategic implications obliquely

---

## Phase 1: Set the parameters

- **Scenario**: which future are we inhabiting? (Use /scenarios output, or define a specific world)
- **Horizon**: what year is this set in?
- **Central character**: who are we following? (customer, executive, new entrant, regulator)
- **Central question**: what strategic assumption does the scene need to illustrate?

---

## Phase 2: Write the scene (300–600 words)

Ground it in:
- Specific year and place
- Named brands (use real or plausible-sounding fictional ones)
- Specific numbers (prices, speeds, conversion rates)
- Small authentic frictions (not everything is perfect)
- The contrast with today (what has changed vs what is surprisingly the same)

---

## Phase 3: The strategic reveal

After the scene, one paragraph:
- What assumption does this scenario make?
- If this scenario is plausible, what should we do differently today?
- What is the earliest signal that this world is approaching?

---

## Output format

```
SCENARIO: [named world from /scenarios, or defined here]
HORIZON: [year]
FORMAT: [Day-in-the-Life / News Article / Interface / Conversation]

---

[SCENE TEXT — 300–600 words]

---

STRATEGIC REVEAL:
  Assumption: [what this world assumes is true]
  Plausibility: [why this isn't science fiction]
  If this is coming: [what to do differently starting now]
  Earliest signal: [how we'd know this world is approaching]
```
