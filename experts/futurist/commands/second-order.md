# /second-order — Second & Third-Order Effects Analysis

**Purpose**: Trace the causal chain beyond the obvious first-order effect of a trend, technology shift, policy change, or strategic move. Most strategic errors come from stopping at first-order.

**When to use**: Evaluating a trend, deciding on a strategic move, stress-testing a forecast, or challenging a consensus view.

---

## The discipline

**First-order thinking**: the direct, obvious consequence of a change. Usually what everyone discusses.

**Second-order thinking**: what changes *because* the first-order effect happened? Who responds, and how? What new constraints or opportunities emerge?

**Third-order thinking**: what changes because of the second-order effects? Where do the chains from multiple second-order effects collide?

**The error**: most strategic planning stops at first-order. The dangerous surprises live at second and third.

---

## The causal chain method

For the input trend/change:

**Step 1 — First-order effects (direct)**
What directly and immediately changes? Be exhaustive here — list 5–8 first-order effects, not just the obvious one.

**Step 2 — Who responds, and how?**
For each major first-order effect:
- Which actors (companies, workers, governments, consumers) are most affected?
- What is their rational response? What is their *actual* likely response (accounting for cognitive bias, political constraints, economic incentives)?
- How does their response create new dynamics?

**Step 3 — Second-order effects**
Map the key second-order effects: what changes *because* actors responded to the first-order effects?

**Step 4 — Third-order effects**
For the 2–3 most important second-order effects: what do they in turn cause?

**Step 5 — Collision mapping**
Where do chains from different second-order effects intersect? These intersections are often where the most non-obvious dynamics emerge.

---

## Example pattern (AI gets cheaper)

| Order | Effect |
|---|---|
| First | AI gets 10× cheaper; companies use more AI; more tasks automated |
| Second | Demand for knowledge workers softens; wage premiums compress; junior roles disappear faster than senior |
| Second | Companies that automate fastest gain cost advantage → competitive pressure to automate across sector |
| Second | AI inference providers (chips, cloud) face enormous demand growth |
| Third | Education sector faces demand collapse for degrees whose value was knowledge transmission → forces reorientation toward judgment/creativity |
| Third | Chip supply constraints tighten → geopolitical premium on chip manufacturing capacity |
| Third | Competitive pressure on companies to automate creates talent crisis for engineers who can build AI systems (scarcity at the top; abundance at the middle) |

## Output format

```
INPUT: [trend / change / event]

FIRST-ORDER EFFECTS:
  1. [effect]
  2. [effect]
  ...

SECOND-ORDER EFFECTS:
  [First-order effect X] →
    Actor response: [who responds, how]
    Second-order: [what this creates]
  
  [repeat per major first-order effect]

THIRD-ORDER EFFECTS (top 2–3 chains):
  [Second-order Y] → [Third-order consequence]

COLLISION POINTS:
  [Where chains from different second-order effects create unexpected dynamics]

STRATEGIC IMPLICATIONS:
  [What this means for the specific company / sector in question]

WHAT MOST PEOPLE ARE MISSING:
  [The non-obvious insight that only emerges at second or third order]

12–24 MONTH ACTIONS:
  [What to do based on the second/third-order view]
```
