# Geopolitics & Regulation

## The geopolitical lens for technology strategy

Geopolitics is not background noise for technology companies — it is an increasingly direct strategic input. Export controls, data localisation requirements, digital sovereignty mandates, and great power competition over technology standards have moved from edge-case compliance issues to core strategic risks.

**The shift that matters**: until ~2015, technology generally operated above geopolitics — global platforms, global talent, global capital, treated the internet as borderless by default. That assumption is broken. The question for every technology company is now: which side of the fragmentation are you on, and what does that mean for your market access, your supply chain, and your competitive position?

## US-China technology competition

### The structural dynamic

**What's happening**: The US-China relationship has shifted from "competitive but interdependent" to "competitive and decoupling-in-strategic-domains." The domains that are decoupling fastest:

1. **Semiconductors** — The BIS export controls (October 2022, extended 2023) restrict China's access to advanced chips (>18nm logic) and the equipment to make them. TSMC, ASML, Lam Research, NVIDIA all constrained. China is investing massively in domestic chip capability (SMIC, Huawei HiSilicon) but is 2–5 years behind and may have an architectural ceiling.

2. **AI systems** — NVIDIA H100/A100/H20 export restricted. China developing domestic alternatives (Huawei Ascend, Biren). Gap is real; China's AI development is not blocked, but it's more expensive and constrained.

3. **Software platforms** — TikTok ban pressure in the US, Google/Meta blocked in China for over a decade. Platform stacks are bifurcating.

4. **5G infrastructure** — Huawei effectively banned in US, UK, Australia, much of EU. Replaced by Ericsson, Nokia, Samsung. China's 5G buildout is most advanced in the world (300M+ base stations).

### What's not decoupling

- Most consumer goods manufacturing (Apple assembles in China, is not leaving fast)
- Commodity and agricultural trade
- Most pharmaceutical supply chains
- Financial markets (though this is increasingly at risk)

### The most important second-order effect

**China's forced self-sufficiency is accelerating Chinese capability in strategic areas.** Being cut off from TSMC and ASML has not stopped China — it has forced a massive government-backed investment in domestic alternatives. In 10 years, China may have a domestic semiconductor industry that does not need TSMC. The decoupling policy may have created a stronger rival.

### For travel companies

**MENA / Wego specifically**: The decoupling creates opportunities in the non-aligned space. China's outbound tourism is a structural growth story (120M Chinese tourists pre-COVID, $250B in spend). UAE and Saudi Arabia are actively cultivating Chinese tourism and investment. A travel platform well-positioned in both MENA and Chinese traveller corridors is in a structurally advantaged position as bifurcation proceeds.

## MENA geopolitical dynamics

### The Abraham Accords and regional normalisation

**What changed**: The Abraham Accords (2020) normalised UAE-Israel and Bahrain-Israel relations, with broader Arab world recognition of Israel proceeding in stages. The attempted Saudi-Israel normalisation was disrupted by October 7, 2023, but the structural logic — shared concern about Iran, shared interest in economic development — has not changed.

**For travel**: Israel-UAE and Israel-Bahrain direct air routes opened. Normalisation creates new travel corridors that did not exist before 2020. Saudi normalisation (if and when it proceeds) would be the largest opening — Saudi tourism from Israel, Jewish heritage tourism in Saudi Arabia.

### Iran

**The structural tension**: Iran and Saudi Arabia are in structural competition for regional dominance. The China-brokered Saudi-Iran diplomatic normalisation (March 2023) is a significant development — but it is diplomatic, not strategic. The fundamental competition over regional influence, proxy conflicts (Yemen, Lebanon, Iraq, Syria), and nuclear capability continues.

**For travel**: Iran is a significant potential travel market (90M population, deep historical/cultural assets) that is largely inaccessible to Western businesses due to sanctions. Watch for: sanctions relief as a key signal for market opening.

### Gulf Vision strategies

Saudi Vision 2030, UAE Centennial 2071, Qatar National Vision 2030 are genuine strategic restructuring efforts driven by the recognition that the oil era is time-limited. Tourism is central to all of them:

- Saudi: 100M tourists/year by 2030 (from ~15M in 2019). NEOM, AlUla, Red Sea Project, Diriyah are flagship investments.
- UAE: Tourism already 11% of GDP; target is 25M visitors by 2031. Dubai EXPO legacy, Expo City ongoing.
- Qatar: 6M visitors/year target post-World Cup.

**Strategic implication for travel**: The Gulf states are building supply (hotels, attractions, airlines, airports) at scale. They are also building demand-side incentives (visa liberalisation, marketing). This is a structural tailwind for the region's tourism, not a cycle.

## Regulatory wave dynamics

### How regulatory waves work

Technology regulation follows a predictable pattern:
1. **Technology emerges** in a regulatory vacuum; permissive by default
2. **Harms accumulate** (or are perceived to accumulate); political pressure builds
3. **Regulatory response** — typically overcorrects in response to political moment
4. **Implementation friction** — enforcement capability lags; industry lobbying moderates
5. **Normalisation** — regulation becomes standard operating environment; incumbents use compliance costs as moats

**The error**: predicting that regulation will be implemented as written, at the speed stated. Reality: regulatory implementation is consistently slower and more negotiated than the headline.

### AI regulation (2024 state)

**EU AI Act** (in force August 2024):
- Risk-based tiered approach: unacceptable risk (prohibited), high risk (registration + conformity assessment), limited risk (transparency obligations), minimal risk (no obligations)
- High-risk applications: biometric surveillance, critical infrastructure, employment decisions, law enforcement, credit scoring, education
- General purpose AI (GPAI) models: transparency requirements, copyright compliance, capability evaluations for frontier models
- Timeline: most provisions apply 2025–2026

**US approach**:
- Executive Order on AI (October 2023): agencies to develop sector-specific AI guidance; safety evaluations for frontier models; export controls
- No comprehensive federal AI legislation as of 2024; sector-specific guidance evolving
- More permissive than EU by design; regulatory arbitrage opportunity for US-based AI development

**China approach**:
- Generative AI Regulations (August 2023): registration requirement for generative AI services; content restrictions; training data requirements
- Typically enforced more strictly on foreign companies than domestic

**The emerging standard**: safety evaluations for frontier AI models (similar to financial stress tests) are the likely global convergence point. What varies: which models are "frontier," who does the evaluation, what the consequences of failure are.

### Data localisation & digital sovereignty

**The trend**: Countries increasingly requiring that data on their citizens be stored within their borders. This is not reversing; it is accelerating.

**Affected businesses**: any company with significant personal data on users (travel booking history, payment data, identity) in affected markets.

**Key markets and requirements**:
- EU (GDPR): not strict localisation but transfer restrictions (Schrems II); data must have equivalent protection outside EU
- China: Personal Information Protection Law (PIPL) + Critical Information Infrastructure regulations — strict localisation for most personal data
- Russia: Federal Law 242-FZ — Russian citizens' data must be stored in Russia
- India: Digital Personal Data Protection Act (2023) — some localisation; cross-border transfer framework still being finalised
- Saudi Arabia: PDPL — similar to GDPR; cross-border transfer with adequacy

**Practical implication for Wego/WegoPro**: As data volume in MENA grows, need to understand which markets have localisation requirements affecting booking and travel data. UAE and Saudi are the priority markets.

## Regulation as competitive moat

**The counterintuitive point**: for established players, regulation can be a competitive advantage. Compliance costs are fixed costs — they scale better with size. Regulation creates barriers to new entrants. Incumbents who participate in regulatory design can shape it to their advantage.

**Active risk management approach**:
- Monitor the regulatory trajectory early (bills, consultation papers, agency guidance) — don't wait for enforcement
- Engage in consultations where possible — your input shapes the output
- Build compliance infrastructure early — don't be the last to comply when the wave breaks
- Watch for regulatory arbitrage windows — regions with more permissive environments for AI/data can be used for development before deployment elsewhere

- **MENA**: Saudi-Israel normalisation progress; Iran nuclear deal developments; oil price volatility driving fiscal pressure on Vision strategies
- **AI regulation**: First major GPAI enforcement actions under EU AI Act; US federal AI legislation introduction; China expanding AI regulation scope
- **Data sovereignty**: New market localisation requirements; cross-border data transfer enforcement actions; Schrems III litigation
