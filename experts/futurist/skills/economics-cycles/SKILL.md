# Economics & Cycles

## Why cycles matter for foresight

Most near-term economic analysis treats the current conditions as close to permanent. Cycle awareness is the corrective: it provides a framework for understanding where we are in longer-wave dynamics, which conditions are structural (here to stay) vs cyclical (will reverse), and what typically follows the current phase.

**Critical discipline**: cycles are descriptive and probabilistic, not deterministic. They give you base rates and analogous periods — not predictions. The value is: "this has a precedent, and here's what happened last time; how similar is the current situation?"

## Kondratiev waves (K-waves)

**What they are**: 40–60 year economic supercycles driven by technology clusters, first described by Nikolai Kondratiev in the 1920s.

**The historical pattern**:

| Wave | Period | Core technology cluster | Phase peaks/troughs |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1st | 1780s–1840s | Textile machinery, water power | British industrial revolution |
| 2nd | 1840s–1890s | Steam power, railways | Great railway expansion; crash of 1873 |
| 3rd | 1890s–1940s | Electricity, chemicals, steel | Electrification; WWI/II; Great Depression |
| 4th | 1940s–1990s | Oil, petrochemicals, automobiles, mass production | Post-WW2 boom; stagflation 1970s; 1980s recovery |
| 5th | 1990s–2040s? | IT, internet, telecommunications | Dotcom boom/bust; 2008 GFC; current AI build-out |

**Current position in Wave 5**: 
- Installation phase (1990–2000): speculative frenzy in internet/telecom; dotcom crash 1999–2001
- Post-crash depression/irruption (2000–2009): consolidation; surviving tech companies build foundations; 2008 GFC
- Golden age deployment (2009–now): tech giants reach maturity; internet becomes infrastructure; AI begins installation phase
- The next turning point: AI is potentially a Wave 6 trigger or the late-stage component of Wave 5

**Carlota Perez's refinement** (*Technological Revolutions and Financial Capital*, 2002):

Perez identified that within each K-wave, there is a consistent internal structure:
1. **Big Bang / Installation**: New technology emerges; financial capital floods in; speculative frenzy
2. **Turning Point (Collapse)**: Financial bubble bursts; crash
3. **Golden Age / Deployment**: Technology becomes infrastructure; productive capital deploys at scale; broadly shared prosperity
4. **Maturity**: Technology matures; returns on capital decline; financial capital seeks the next opportunity

**Application to AI**:
- We are in the *Installation phase* of AI — financial capital flooding in, speculative valuations, "everyone is an AI company"
- History suggests: a correction is coming (not necessarily catastrophic, but the current valuations assume a deployment at scale that hasn't happened yet)
- After the correction: the companies that survive and build real AI infrastructure will power the Golden Age deployment
- Implication: invest for the deployment phase, not the installation phase

## Ray Dalio's debt super-cycle

**The framework** (*Principles for Dealing with the Changing World Order*, 2021):

Dalio identifies a long-term debt cycle of ~75–100 years, driven by the expansion and contraction of debt:

1. **New world order / currency reserve system establishment**: Emerging from major conflict or economic crisis; new reserve currency; new institutions
2. **Rise**: Debt relatively low; productivity growth; strong institutions; trade expansion
3. **Excess / Decadence**: Debt accumulation accelerates; wealth inequality widens; institutional quality degrades; currency debasement begins
4. **Decline and conflict**: Internal conflicts (class/interest group) and external conflicts (between rising and declining powers); war or major disruption; reset

**Where we are (2024 Dalio analysis)**:
- US is in "Excess/Decadence" phase: debt-to-GDP at levels not seen since WW2; wealth inequality at 100-year highs; institutional trust declining; rising power (China) challenging reserve currency status
- But the timeline for "Decline and conflict" is measured in decades, not years
- Key signals: if the dollar loses reserve currency status, or if US debt market stress emerges, the transition accelerates

**For strategy**: Not a 5-year operational concern, but a 15–25 year structural concern. Companies that are globally diversified (revenue, assets, talent) are better positioned in a declining unipolar order than those deeply US-dependent.

## Business cycle (standard)

**The standard framework**: Expansion → Peak → Contraction → Trough → Recovery

**Key relationships**:
- Early cycle: credit expansion, investment recovery, employment growth, low inflation
- Mid cycle: credit normalisation, productivity-led growth, earnings expansion
- Late cycle: capacity constraints, inflationary pressure, credit tightening, wage growth
- Recession: demand destruction, credit contraction, unemployment rising
- Recovery: usually led by rate cuts + fiscal stimulus + inventory rebuild

**The 2024–2026 context**:
- The 2020–2022 stimulus cycle (COVID response) created an unusual late-cycle pattern: persistent inflation, aggressive rate hikes, labour market unusual resilience
- "Soft landing" is possible but not guaranteed: history of aggressive rate hike cycles ending in recession (13/14 post-WW2 US tightening cycles ended in recession)
- AI investment wave creating a secondary capex cycle running independently of the consumer/housing cycle
- MENA economies: oil-linked; current oil price range ($70–90) is broadly neutral; fiscal break-evens vary by country (Saudi ~$80, UAE ~$65, Qatar ~$45)

## Productivity waves

**The productivity puzzle**: economic theory predicts that technological improvement should show up in productivity statistics. It often doesn't — at least not immediately.

**Solow paradox (1987)**: "You can see the computer age everywhere except in the productivity statistics." Said when personal computers were already widespread. Productivity surge came 8–12 years later (mid-1990s).

**Explanation (David, Brynjolfsson)**: Technology diffusion requires complementary changes — business process restructuring, workforce retraining, infrastructure buildout — that take years to implement. The productivity shows up after the complementary investment, not when the technology is adopted.

**AI implication**: If the Solow paradox pattern holds, widespread AI productivity gains would show up in macro statistics 2028–2035, not now. The productivity revolution is real; it's just early. This is important for calibrating expectations.

## Capital cycle theory

**The mechanism** (Capital Returns by Marathon Asset Management):

Capital flows into attractive industries → capacity builds → supply increases → returns decline → capital exits → capacity reduces → returns improve → capital re-enters.

**The timing**: capital allocation decisions take 3–7 years to build new capacity. The capital cycle operates on a multi-year lag.

**Current capital cycle dynamics**:

| Sector | Cycle position | Implication |
|---|---|---|
| Data centres / AI infrastructure | Heavy investment phase | Capacity surplus risk 2026–2028 if AI demand doesn't scale as expected |
| Semiconductor fab | Investment phase (CHIPS Act, TSMC expansion) | Supply normalisation 2025–2026 |
| Oil & gas E&P | Underinvestment since 2020 | Supply constraint risk if demand doesn't fall fast enough; oil price support |
| Commercial real estate (office) | Structural change + cycle bust | Long slow recovery; office overhang persists |
| Travel infrastructure | Post-COVID recovery investment | Airport/hotel capacity additions 2024–2028; potential oversupply in some markets |

## Applying cycle frameworks

**The synthesis approach**:
1. **Identify the relevant cycle timescale** — Is the question about the next recession (business cycle), the AI deployment wave (K-wave), or long-range structural position (Dalio)?
2. **Locate yourself on the cycle** — Not precise timing, but rough position (early/mid/late/turning)
3. **Derive the base case trajectory** — What typically happens next from here?
4. **Identify the variance** — What could accelerate or arrest this trajectory?
5. **Connect to strategic implications** — What does this mean for capital allocation, timing, and optionality?

**The overconfidence trap**: Cycle frameworks can create false precision. "We are in late-cycle so a recession is imminent" ignores that late-cycle can extend for years. Use cycles for directionality and probability, not timing.
