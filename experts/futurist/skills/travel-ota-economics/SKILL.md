## The OTA Stack

### How metasearch works (and why it's structurally fragile)

Metasearch is a price comparison and aggregation layer. The OTA pays a cost-per-click to the metasearch engine (Google Flights, Kayak, Skyscanner) and hopes the conversion rate makes the unit economics work. The margin structure:

```
Supplier price → OTA margin → CPC to metasearch → Customer
```

The challenge: metasearch is inherently commoditising. If the OTA's differentiation is price discovery, and price discovery becomes a free feature in Google or an AI assistant, the OTA's core value proposition disappears. The structural fragility of metasearch is real — it is a layer that exists because of friction, and friction is being reduced by AI.

**Wego's specific position**: MENA-native metasearch with local brand trust, Arabic-language UX, and MENA-specific payment integrations (Mada, local wallets). The moat is not price discovery — it's local trust + payment convenience + MENA supplier relationships. This is more durable than pure price comparison, but must be actively extended.

### GDS dependency and NDC disruption

**Global Distribution Systems (GDS)**: Amadeus, Travelport, Sabre. The legacy airline distribution network. OTAs pay GDS booking fees; airlines pay GDS incentive fees. Both sides pay for the privilege. The GDS intermediary exists because the technology was built in the 1960s–70s and the airline industry never replaced it.

**NDC (New Distribution Capability)**: IATA standard for direct airline-OTA connectivity. Allows airlines to offer richer content (bundled fares, ancillaries, dynamic pricing) directly without GDS markup. Airlines want NDC to take margin back from GDS. OTAs that rely on GDS aggregation face cost and content disadvantage against airlines' own direct channels.

**The structural shift**: airlines are pushing NDC aggressively (Lufthansa Group, British Airways/Iberia, American Airlines). GDS-dependent OTAs face:
1. Rising GDS booking fees (as GDS volume contracts, fixed costs spread over fewer bookings)
2. Content gaps (NDC-only fares not available through GDS)
3. Long-term disintermediation if airlines successfully pull customers direct

**MENA context**: Gulf carriers (EK, WY, FZ, XY) are more aggressive than legacy Western carriers on direct distribution. Emirates was one of the first to pull content from major GDS providers in disputes. For Wego, the NDC transition is existential-level: NDC direct connect capabilities are a strategic necessity, not a nice-to-have.

### OTA economics: the margin structure

**Flights**: thin margins. Airlines are powerful suppliers; OTA take rates on flights are 2–4%. Volume-driven business. The OTA earns on booking fees, credit card cashback, and ancillary upsells.

**Hotels**: better margins. Hotel take rates 10–25% depending on chain, market, and booking channel. Booking.com and Expedia built multi-billion businesses on hotel economics. This is where OTA profitability lives.

**Key metric: revenue per session**. Composite of conversion rate × take rate × average booking value. MENA average booking values are relatively high (long-haul travel, Gulf carrier premium pricing), which is a structural advantage for MENA OTAs.

**CAC dynamics**: Google has systematically increased CPC prices as it has captured more metasearch intent itself (Google Flights, Google Hotels). This has compressed OTA marketing efficiency over 2018–2024. The structural trend: organic and brand-driven traffic becomes more valuable as paid acquisition costs rise.

## MENA Travel Market Dynamics

### Demand drivers (structural, 10+ year)

**Vision 2030 (Saudi Arabia)**: KSA government targets 150M tourist arrivals by 2030 (vs 79M in 2019). Domestic tourism development (NEOM, The Line, Red Sea Project, AlUla). Saudi outbound travel normalising post-conservative-era restrictions. Saudi women travelling independently since 2018. Young population (median age 27) with rising disposable income and travel appetite.

**UAE Tourism 2030**: targeting 40M tourists/year to Dubai alone. Expo 2020 legacy infrastructure. Abu Dhabi cultural investments (Louvre, Guggenheim, Ferrari World expansion). DIFC as the regional financial hub attracting business travel.

**Demographic dividend**: MENA has one of the youngest populations globally. The generation entering peak travel years (25–40) is digitally native, mobile-first, and travel-hungry. This is a structural tailwind for online travel for 15–20 years.

**Remittance corridor travel**: large expatriate populations in Gulf states travelling home to South Asia (India, Pakistan, Bangladesh, Philippines) and Arab world. This is high-frequency, price-sensitive travel — Wego's core user base.

### Supply constraints (watch closely)

**Airport capacity**: Riyadh King Salman International (planned, 120M capacity by 2030), Dubai's Al Maktoum Airport expansion. Capacity is a genuine constraint on the Vision 2030 travel ambitions — infrastructure delivery matters.

**Airline capacity**: Gulf carriers are order-cycle leaders. Emirates' A380/777 fleet and 777X order. Saudi National Carrier (Riyadh Air, launched 2023) adding capacity. FlyNAS, Air Arabia Saudi expanding LCC. The Gulf is adding capacity faster than almost any other region — but primarily at the premium end.

**Visa normalisation**: Saudi eVisa (launched 2019) was transformative. UAE and Qatar visa-on-arrival for 160+ nationalities. Egypt's e-Visa. The administrative barriers to MENA travel are falling — this is a structural demand enabler.

### Competitive landscape

**Global OTAs in MENA**:
- Booking.com: strong in hotels globally; MENA penetration limited by brand, language, and payment friction
- Expedia: weaker MENA presence; Vrbo (vacation rental) not well-suited to MENA market
- Agoda: Southeast Asia strength; limited MENA presence
- None of the global OTAs have cracked MENA consumer trust in the way Wego has

**MENA-native OTAs**:
- **Almosafer/Seera Group**: Saudi-headquartered, backed by PIF (Saudi Public Investment Fund) — deep funding, Vision 2030 alignment, government relationships. The most credible competitive threat to Wego in KSA.
- **flyin.com**: Saudi OTA, flights + hotels, competes directly with Wego
- **Cleartrip (Flipkart-owned)**: India-first with MENA presence; strong in Indian-expat demographic
- **Tajawal**: Abu Dhabi-based, mid-market, UAE focus

**Airlines direct**: EK (Emirates.com), SQ (Singapore Airlines — less MENA but important for Wego's SIN base), WY (Omanair), XY (flynas), Air Arabia. All investing in direct booking; loyalty programs are the pull mechanism.

## WegoPro: B2B Corporate Travel

**Market structure**: MENA corporate travel management is highly fragmented. Most large MENA corporations use either:
1. Legacy TMCs (BCD Travel, CWT, Amex GBT) with poor digital experience
2. Manual booking processes through in-house travel managers
3. Informal processes (employee books on consumer OTA, expenses it)

**Competitive set**:
- **SAP Concur**: dominant enterprise; high implementation cost; poor mobile UX; aging platform
- **Navan (formerly TripActions)**: Silicon Valley SME/mid-market; US-heavy; limited MENA inventory/language
- **TravelPerk**: European SME focus; MENA presence minimal
- **Serko/Cytric**: airline-adjacent TMC tools

**WegoPro's structural advantage**: MENA-native inventory (Gulf carrier NDC relationships, Arabic UX, local payment methods), combined with modern SaaS tooling. The gap in the market is a modern, MENA-native corporate travel platform. This is the genuine opportunity.

**Key B2B metrics**: seat under management (SUM), average booking value (ABV), corporate account retention, policy compliance rate.

## WegoBeds: B2B Accommodation Supply

**The bedbank model**: WegoBeds aggregates hotel inventory at net rates and resells to OTAs, TMCs, and other distributors. The margin is the markup between net rate and selling rate.

**Market structure**: bedbank consolidation happening globally (Hotelbeds/GTA/Tourico merger, Jumbo/MTS acquisition trail). The business is volume + relationships — large aggregators have better net rates because of volume commitments.

**WegoBeds' position**: MENA-specialist supplier relationships (regional properties not well-covered by global bedbanks) + WegoPro captive demand (cross-sell). The logic: own the supply and the demand, improve margin at both ends.

## AI Disruption to OTA: The Core Structural Question

**The threat**: AI agents (GPT-4o, Claude, Gemini) with travel tools can execute end-to-end travel booking via natural language. If users shift from "search metasearch → browse → click → book" to "tell agent: book me cheapest EK flight to Riyadh next Thursday", the metasearch discovery layer is bypassed entirely.

**Timeline estimate**:
- 2024–2026: AI travel assistants emerge but remain supplementary (humans still verify and book manually)
- 2026–2028: AI agents achieve booking execution capability (Booking.com Agent Mode, Google Gemini Travel, OpenAI travel plugin ecosystem). Early adopters shift behaviour.
- 2028–2031: Agent-mediated booking becomes a significant minority of transactions for tech-forward demographics. Metasearch click-through model under structural pressure.
- 2031+: Agent-native distribution likely dominant for complex/multi-leg itineraries; simple searches still via direct interfaces.

**The strategic bet for OTAs**: don't be the search box; be the agent. Or: be the inventory layer that agents access (become the API, not the UI). Wego's AI strategy question is whether it builds its own agent capability or positions Wego as the best-connected inventory/execution layer for third-party agents.

**MENA-specific dynamics**: MENA users are mobile-first, WhatsApp-native, and may adopt conversational AI travel booking faster than Western markets where browser-based search is more entrenched. WA Business API + travel agent = potentially compelling MENA-native AI travel product.
