# Decks & Presentations (Futures-Facing)

## The core challenge

Futures presentations have a specific communication problem: the audience's default mode is scepticism ("this is speculative") or dismissal ("this is too far out to be actionable"). The job of the presentation is to overcome both without sacrificing intellectual honesty.

The solution is not to be less rigorous — it's to be more specific, more grounded, and more translatable to near-term action than the audience expects.

---

## Narrative architecture

### The Minto Pyramid (inverted for futures)

Standard Minto: conclusion first, evidence second. This works for decision memos.

Futures presentations often work better with a modified structure: **provocation first** (to disrupt the audience's current model) → **evidence of structural shift** → **implications** → **actions**. The provocation earns the right to be taken seriously before making the ask.

### Three narrative arcs (see also /future-deck command)

**Arc A — The Structural Shift**: Something fundamental has changed. Here's what most people haven't taken seriously yet. Here's what it means for us. Here's what we need to do.

*Best for*: board strategy sessions, senior leadership offsite, investor updates with a long-range thesis.

**Arc B — The Scenario Space**: The future is genuinely uncertain. Here are the three worlds that could emerge. Here's what each world requires of us. Here's the strategy that makes us robust across them.

*Best for*: strategy planning workshops, teams that need to build shared mental models, decisions with high irreversibility.

**Arc C — The Opportunity Window**: A structural shift is creating a window that won't stay open. Here's the evidence. Here's the play. Here's why now.

*Best for*: investment pitches, new initiative proposals, partnership pitches.

## Slide design principles (futures-specific)

### Specificity over generality
Every claim needs a number, a named company, a specific geography, or a named mechanism. Vague futures claims kill credibility.

❌ "AI is getting cheaper and more powerful."
✅ "GPT-4 class inference cost fell 100× between January 2023 and December 2024 — and the cost curve shows no sign of flattening. This is faster than any previous compute cost decline, including the original Moore's Law drop in the 1970s."

### Evidence hierarchy for futures claims

Rank claims by strength of evidence:
1. **Physical laws / mathematical certainty** (e.g., cost curves based on fundamental physics) — strongest
2. **Historical pattern** (e.g., every major technology paradigm has followed this S-curve shape)
3. **Empirical data trend** (e.g., data centre energy demand has grown 30% annually for 5 years)
4. **Multiple independent sources converging** (e.g., Gartner, Accenture, and McKinsey all project X)
5. **Single expert or report** — weakest for futures claims; cite but don't rely on

### The "this is not science fiction" test

Every futures claim should pass: "Could a reasonable, sceptical senior executive accept that this is plausible based on what's already happened?" If not, you've moved from structural foresight to speculation. Speculation is fine in a workshop; it's deadly in a board presentation.

## Scenario visualisation approaches

### The 2×2 matrix
Classic. Works for two clearly defined axes. Simple to explain and remember.

*When to use*: when you have two genuinely independent critical uncertainties that are both intuitively understandable.
*Failure mode*: axes that are actually correlated; too-abstract axis labels; scenarios that aren't genuinely distinct.

### The cone of plausibility
Visualises the range of possible futures expanding from "now" over time. Shows: predetermined elements (narrow band, high certainty), probable futures (middle band), possible futures (wide band), implausible futures (outside the cone).

*When to use*: when you want to show the *degree* of uncertainty rather than distinct scenarios; when the audience needs to understand that you're not predicting, you're mapping.

### The journey narrative
Show a timeline from now to the horizon, marked with key decision points, turning points, and signals. Shows: when forks in the road occur; what events would confirm which path is being taken.

*When to use*: when sequence matters — the story is about how you get from here to there, not just about the end state.

## The signals dashboard slide

Every futures presentation should include a signals monitoring slide. It:
- Shows the audience you have epistemic humility (you know you might be wrong)
- Demonstrates operational discipline (you have a process for tracking the thesis)
- Gives the audience a way to evaluate the thesis over time

Structure:
| Signal | What it would confirm | What it would disconfirm | Current reading |
|---|---|---|---|
| [metric/indicator] | [scenario A] | [scenario B] | [current state] |

Aim for 3–6 signals maximum. Each should be observable in the next 6–18 months.

## Common failures in futures presentations

**The PowerPoint disease**: 50 slides of context before getting to the implication. The audience needed the implication at slide 3.

**The prediction trap**: presenting one future as inevitable kills engagement. The audience finds one thing they disagree with and dismisses the whole analysis. Scenario framing ("here are three possible worlds") is more durable.

**The abstraction trap**: structural forces described in the abstract, never connected to *this company's* specific exposure. The audience leaves thinking "interesting, but what do I do?"

**The no-ask problem**: a futures presentation with no call to action is a research report, not a strategy presentation. The final slide must ask for something specific and time-bound.

**The consultant tell**: phrases like "leveraging synergies," "transformation journey," "ecosystem play." Use plain language. The ideas are complex enough without wrapping them in jargon.

## Practical tooling

**Diagrams**: use simple 2×2 matrices, timeline visualisations, and cone-of-plausibility diagrams. Avoid complex flowcharts — they confuse rather than clarify.

**Data visualisation**: cost curve charts (log scale), adoption S-curves, capability benchmark comparisons. These are the most credible visuals for technology futures claims.

**Design fiction inserts**: one or two fictional artefacts (a news headline, a product screenshot from the future, a customer quote from 2030) can make scenarios viscerally real in a way that abstractions cannot.

**Platform**: Google Slides for internal strategy work (collaborative, version-controlled, easy to iterate). PowerPoint for external/investor presentations where formatting control matters. Gamma for rapid generation of structured decks that need AI-native design.
