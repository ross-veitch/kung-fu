# Provocative Assumption Testing

## The core discipline

Every strategy is built on a stack of assumptions. Most are invisible — not because they're unimportant, but because they feel like facts. The job of provocative assumption testing is to surface those invisible load-bearing assumptions and stress-test them before reality does.

**The strategic danger of invisible assumptions**:
- They can't be monitored if they're not named
- They can't be contingency-planned against if they're not acknowledged
- When they fail, the failure appears to come from "nowhere"
- They tend to cluster around whatever was true in the environment when the strategy was designed

---

## Techniques

### 1. The assumption stack

Build a complete inventory of what the strategy assumes. Three layers:

**Stated assumptions** (in the plan): these are the starting point, not the destination. Most planning documents state the obvious assumptions and leave the dangerous ones unstated.

**Implied assumptions** (inferred from choices): if the strategy invests heavily in paid search, it assumes Google search maintains dominance in discovery. If it's building a large sales force, it assumes sales-led growth will persist. If it's premium-pricing, it assumes customers will pay the premium. These are rarely written down.

**Background assumptions** (so embedded they feel like reality): 
- "Customers care most about price"
- "Our market is growing"  
- "The technology will continue improving"
- "Regulation will not fundamentally change our model"
- "Our key competitors will compete the same way they do today"

Background assumptions are the most dangerous. Run them like a checklist.

---

### 2. The inversion test

For each significant assumption, reverse it and ask: *what would have to be true for the opposite of this assumption to be correct?*

Purpose: not to predict the reversal, but to understand the conditions under which your assumption fails — and assess how plausible those conditions are.

**Inversion examples**:

| Assumption | Inversion | Conditions that would make inversion true |
|---|---|---|
| "AI will keep getting cheaper" | AI costs plateau or rise | Energy constraint; regulatory cap on compute; architectural ceiling |
| "Business travel recovers to pre-2020 levels" | Business travel permanently contracts | Video conferencing quality improves further; carbon taxes; policy shift |
| "Google search dominates discovery" | Discovery fragments across AI agents | AI agents become preferred interface; Google loses distribution monopoly |

---

### 3. The six-inversion framework

Six structured provocations to apply to any assumption set:

**The Reversal**: Take the dominant trend and reverse it.
*"What if [thing growing/increasing] reverses/declines/collapses?"*

**The Floor**: What if the physical or economic limit arrives earlier than modeled?
*"What if [resource/cost/infrastructure] hits a hard constraint in 3 years, not 10?"*

**The Preference shift**: What if customers stop caring about what they currently optimise for?
*"What if [current customer priority] becomes irrelevant to the next customer cohort?"*

**The Power shift**: What if margin and power moves to a different layer of the value chain?
*"What if [current high-margin layer] commoditizes and value concentrates in [different layer]?"*

**The Technology flip**: What if the enabling technology becomes free and ubiquitous?
*"What if [current differentiating technology] becomes infrastructure available to all competitors?"*

**The Regulation shock**: What if the regulatory environment becomes hostile to the current model?
*"What if the EU/US/China decides [specific regulation]? What does the model look like in 24 months?"*

---

### 4. The pre-mortem (assumption failure mode)

A pre-mortem is a specific technique for assumption testing applied to the future:

1. Set a horizon (e.g., "It is 2029")
2. State: "The strategy has failed. Not a small miss — a real failure."
3. Ask: which of our assumptions failed? What did we believe that turned out to be wrong?
4. Generate multiple failure stories (divergent thinking)
5. Identify the most common sources of failure across the stories (convergent analysis)
6. Build monitoring and contingency plans for the most likely assumption failures

The pre-mortem works because it changes the cognitive frame from "this will probably work" (planning optimism) to "this has already failed — what happened?" (diagnostic mode). The diagnostic mode is far better at generating specific failure scenarios.

---

### 5. The red team

When assumption testing is a group exercise, red team discipline is critical:

- **Assign a red team role**: one or more people are explicitly tasked with finding the flaws. Without explicit assignment, everyone defaults to defending the plan.
- **Separate generation from evaluation**: first generate all the ways the assumption could be wrong (no criticism allowed). Then evaluate the list.
- **Protect the red team from social pressure**: the person who says "I think we're wrong about X" needs to be rewarded, not punished. Culture determines whether assumption testing actually happens.

---

## Output: assumption risk register

The practical output of assumption testing is an assumption risk register:

| Assumption | Type (stated/implied/background) | Centrality (1–5) | Confidence (1–5) | If wrong: | Monitor: |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| [assumption] | [type] | [1–5] | [1–5] | [consequence if fails] | [indicator to watch] |

Prioritise: Centrality > 3 AND Confidence < 3. These are the load-bearing assumptions you don't have strong evidence for — the highest risk assumptions in the stack.
