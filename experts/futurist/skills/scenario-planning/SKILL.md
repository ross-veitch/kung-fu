# Scenario Planning

## What scenario planning is and isn't

**What it is**: A method for structuring uncertainty into a manageable set of coherent futures, then deriving strategic implications from each. The goal is not to predict which future will occur — it's to be prepared for a range of futures and to identify strategies that are robust across them.

**What it isn't**: 
- A forecast (scenario planning does not give you a probability-weighted expected value)
- A risk register (scenarios are holistic futures, not individual risks)
- Strategy itself (scenarios are inputs to strategy; they don't produce strategy directly)

**When to use it**: When facing a major decision under high uncertainty; when the strategic question involves forces outside your control (geopolitics, technology paradigm shifts, regulatory change); when you need to build organisational alignment around uncertain futures.

**The Shell origin**: Scenario planning as a formal discipline was developed by Shell in the 1970s. Shell's scenarios predicted the 1973 oil crisis, allowing Shell to respond faster than competitors. The method has since been adopted by governments, military planners, and large corporations.

## The 2×2 scenario matrix method

The most common and communication-effective scenario planning approach.

### Step 1: Define the focal question

Be specific. Not "what will the world look like?" but "what will the competitive landscape of online travel distribution look like in 2030, and what does that mean for Wego's strategy?"

A good focal question:
- Is decision-relevant (the scenarios will influence an actual decision)
- Is uncertain (if you already know the answer, you don't need scenarios)
- Is bounded (5–15 year horizon; specific sector/geography)

### Step 2: Identify driving forces (STEEPV scan)

Systematically identify forces that could influence the focal question:

| Category | Questions to ask |
|---|---|
| **S**ocial | Demographics? Consumer behaviour shifts? Cultural changes? |
| **T**echnological | Technology trajectories? Capability thresholds? Disruption potential? |
| **E**conomic | Business cycle? Capital flows? Cost curve changes? |
| **E**nvironmental | Climate change? Resource constraints? Regulatory response? |
| **P**olitical | Geopolitics? Regulation? Policy shifts? |
| **V**alues | Ethical shifts? Stakeholder priorities? Purpose expectations? |

Generate a long list (20–30 factors), then cluster and prioritise.

### Step 3: Identify critical uncertainties

From the driving forces, identify the 2–3 that are:
1. **Highly impactful** — significant effect on the focal question if they resolve one way vs another
2. **Highly uncertain** — genuinely could go either way; you cannot determine which outcome is more likely

Distinguish from **predetermined elements** — forces that will happen regardless (e.g., global smartphone penetration will continue increasing; the baby boomer retirement wave is locked in). Predetermined elements are inputs that all scenarios share; they are not axes.

**The 2-axis selection**: Choose the 2 most impactful and most uncertain driving forces. These become the axes of your 2×2 matrix.

**Common errors in axis selection**:
- Choosing predetermined elements as axes (they resolve the same way in all scenarios)
- Choosing axes that are correlated (AI capability AND automation rate — these move together; they're not independent axes)
- Choosing axes that are really the same thing (economic growth AND consumer spending)
- Choosing axes that don't generate meaningfully different strategic implications when they vary

**Good axis pairs (travel/OTA context)**:

*Example A*:
- Axis 1: AI agents as trip planners (Minimal adoption ← → Ubiquitous, autonomous)
- Axis 2: OTA competitive structure (Consolidated to 2–3 global giants ← → Fragmented / regional players)

*Example B*:
- Axis 1: Climate policy impact on aviation (Manageable / gradual ← → Disruptive / demand-destroying)
- Axis 2: Middle East geopolitical stability (Improved / normalisation ← → Escalation / fragmentation)

### Step 4: Build the 4 scenarios

Each quadrant of the 2×2 becomes a named scenario. **Give each scenario an evocative name** — not "Scenario A" but something memorable that captures the essence of that world.

**Scenario naming principles**:
- Names should evoke the scenario, not describe it technically
- Good names: "The AI Concierge World," "The Great Fragmentation," "The Silent Plateau"
- Bad names: "High AI + Consolidated OTAs," "Scenario 2B"

**Scenario narrative structure**:
1. **Opening image** (2–3 sentences describing the world in the scenario's horizon year)
2. **Key forces** that drove this world into being
3. **Competitive landscape** in this world
4. **What happened to the company** (or industry) in this world
5. **Strategic implications** — what you need to have done by [year] to thrive in this world

### Step 5: Wind tunnel testing

Test your existing strategy (or strategic options) against each scenario:

| Strategy / option | Scenario 1 | Scenario 2 | Scenario 3 | Scenario 4 |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Invest heavily in UI/UX | ✅ Thrives | ⚠️ Vulnerable | ❌ Irrelevant | ✅ Thrives |
| Build direct supplier API relationships | ✅ Thrives | ✅ Thrives | ✅ Thrives | ⚠️ Partial |
| Geographic expansion to new MENA markets | ✅ Thrives | ✅ Thrives | ⚠️ Delayed | ❌ Risky |

**A robust strategy** performs reasonably well across all scenarios — it doesn't require a specific scenario to succeed.

**An optionality-preserving strategy** keeps doors open across scenarios — it invests in capabilities that are valuable in multiple scenarios rather than betting on one.

**A hedged portfolio**: some investments that are robust + some that are scenario-specific bets + monitoring triggers to shift allocation as uncertainty resolves.

### Step 6: Identify triggers and signposts

For each axis of uncertainty, identify observable signals that indicate which direction the axis is resolving:

**Example (AI agent adoption axis)**:
- 🚦 Signal: OpenAI, Google, or Anthropic announces dedicated travel agent product with booking capability
- 🚦 Signal: Booking.com or Expedia reports agent-originated bookings > 5% of total
- 🚦 Signal: Major airline or hotel chain opens direct AI-agent API with preferential pricing

**Review cadence**: Assign someone to monitor these signals quarterly. When a predetermined trigger fires, review whether the scenario portfolio allocation should change.

## Cone of plausibility

Alternative to the 2×2 for communicating futures to executive audiences.

**The concept**: At the current moment, the range of plausible futures is wide. As time extends further, the range widens further. Certain futures are within the cone (plausible given current trajectories); others are outside (implausible without extraordinary events).

**How to draw it**:
1. Current state on the left
2. Time horizon on the right
3. Three zones:
   - **Business as usual** (straight ahead): extrapolation of current trends
   - **Plausible futures** (the wide cone): where structural forces could plausibly take you
   - **Outside the cone**: technically possible but requiring extraordinary/unlikely conditions

**When to use**: For executive communication when you need to convey uncertainty without the precision of a 2×2. "Here are the plausible futures; here's where the business-as-usual trajectory leads; here are the specific choices that move us toward or away from desired futures."

## Common scenario planning errors

1. **Scenarios that converge** — if your 4 scenarios all look basically the same, your axes weren't sufficiently differentiated or uncertain. Good scenarios should feel like genuinely different worlds.

2. **Good scenario + 3 bad scenarios** — unconsciously making one scenario "the preferred future" and treating the others as dystopias. All scenarios should be plausible; none should be clearly preferred.

3. **No translation to action** — scenarios that are intellectually interesting but don't produce actionable strategic implications. Every scenario must end with: what do we need to do today?

4. **False precision** — 2×2 matrices suggest crisp boundaries; real futures blend axes. Scenarios are thinking tools, not predictions.

5. **One-and-done** — scenarios built for a strategy offsite and then filed away. Scenarios need living trigger signals and periodic review.

## Quick scenario method (for time-constrained situations)

When you need scenarios in 30–60 minutes rather than a multi-day workshop:

1. State the focal question (5 min)
2. List 10 driving forces; mark the 2 most uncertain and impactful (10 min)
3. Name 4 scenarios from the 2×2 (5 min)
4. Write 3 sentences describing each scenario world (10 min)
5. Wind tunnel test: for each scenario, name the one thing you'd urgently do if you knew it was coming (10 min)
6. Identify 2–3 signals to watch per scenario (10 min)

**Output**: a coherent scenario set that is discussion-ready for executive decision-making, not polished enough for public communication, but functional for internal strategic use.
