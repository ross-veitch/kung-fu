# Biology & Convergence

## Why biology matters for a futurist

Biology is undergoing a transformation analogous to what happened to computing in the 1990s: it is becoming programmable, predictable, and cheap. The convergence of AI with biology (AlphaFold, protein design, drug discovery automation) is accelerating this transformation by an order of magnitude. Within the 5–15 year futurist horizon, biology-driven structural shifts will rival AI in their breadth of impact.

The key framing: **biology is becoming an engineering discipline.** CRISPR allows editing of genomes with precision. Synthetic biology allows design of organisms from scratch. AI allows prediction of protein structure, drug-target interaction, and clinical outcomes that previously required years of wet-lab work. These are not incremental improvements — they are paradigm shifts.

## The genomics cost curve

**DNA sequencing** has followed an exponential cost-reduction curve faster than Moore's law:

| Year | Cost per human genome | Milestone |
|---|---|---|
| 2001 | ~$100M | Human Genome Project completion |
| 2007 | ~$1M | First next-generation sequencing platforms |
| 2014 | ~$1,000 | Illumina HiSeq X Ten; clinical sequencing becomes viable |
| 2020 | ~$600 | Routine clinical use; direct-to-consumer (23andMe, AncestryDNA) |
| 2024 | ~$200–400 | Approaching commodity; some platforms targeting $100 |
| 2030 (projection) | ~$10–50 | Whole-genome sequencing as routine health screening |

**Second-order effects of cheap sequencing**:
- Cancer early detection via liquid biopsy (circulating tumor DNA) becomes standard-of-care
- Personalised medicine: drug dosing, drug selection, risk stratification based on individual genetics
- Preventive health: genomic risk scores for complex diseases (cardiovascular, Alzheimer's) enable decades-early intervention
- Agricultural genomics: precision breeding, disease-resistant crops, reduced pesticide use
- Forensics and ancestry: already transformed; continued expansion

## CRISPR and genome editing

**What CRISPR is**: A molecular tool (adapted from bacterial immune systems) that allows precise editing of DNA at specified locations. Think find-and-replace for the genome.

**Current state (2024)**:
- First CRISPR-based human therapeutic approved (December 2023): Casgevy for sickle cell disease and beta-thalassemia (Vertex/CRISPR Therapeutics)
- Several others in late-stage clinical trials: transthyretin amyloidosis (Intellia), haemophilia, acute myeloid leukaemia
- Ex-vivo editing (cells edited outside the body, returned) is easier and already clinical
- In-vivo editing (editing cells inside the body) is the harder frontier; early successes in liver targeting; others coming

**The structural question**: can CRISPR become a platform for treating common diseases (not just rare inherited conditions)?

**Barriers**:
- Delivery: getting the editing machinery inside the right cells in the body at therapeutic doses (LNPs work for liver; other tissues harder)
- Off-target effects: unintended edits; not yet fully solved; regulatory bar is high
- Manufacturing: cell therapies are expensive to manufacture at scale; process innovation required
- Pricing and access: Casgevy is priced at $2.2M/patient; not sustainable for common disease; requires different economic model

**Timeline**: Ex-vivo therapies for additional rare diseases: 2024–2028. In-vivo therapies for common diseases: 2027–2035. Platform commoditization: 2030+.

## AI × biology convergence

This is the most powerful current convergence. AI is not just being applied to biology — it is restructuring what is possible.

### AlphaFold and the protein structure revolution

**What happened**: AlphaFold 2 (DeepMind, 2020) solved a 50-year grand challenge — predicting the 3D structure of proteins from amino acid sequence — with near-experimental accuracy. AlphaFold 3 (2024) extends this to DNA, RNA, and protein-ligand interactions.

**Why it matters**:
- Protein structure is function: understanding how a protein folds reveals how it works, where drugs can bind, what mutations cause disease
- Drug discovery previously required crystal structures (years of wet-lab work). AlphaFold predicts in hours.
- The AlphaFold database now contains predicted structures for ~200M proteins (essentially all known proteins)

**Second-order effect**: This is not just faster drug discovery — it enables drug discovery targets that were previously inaccessible ("undruggable" proteins). The tractable target space has expanded enormously.

### AI-accelerated drug discovery

**Traditional pipeline**: Target identification → hit discovery → lead optimisation → preclinical → clinical (Phase I/II/III) → approval. Total time: 10–15 years. Total cost: ~$2B per approved drug.

**Where AI is compressing the pipeline**:
1. **Target identification**: AI mining of genomics, proteomics, and literature to identify novel targets. Was years; now months.
2. **Molecule design**: Generative AI designing novel molecules with desired properties (binding affinity, selectivity, ADMET — absorption, distribution, metabolism, excretion, toxicity). Was months of chemistry iteration; now days.
3. **Virtual screening**: Predicting which of millions of candidate molecules will bind to a target. AI replaces physical compound library screening.
4. **Toxicity prediction**: Early in silico prediction of toxicity and off-target effects, reducing late-stage failures.

**Early evidence of impact**:
- Insilico Medicine: AI-designed drug in Phase II clinical trials (first ever AI-designed molecule to reach this stage, 2023)
- Recursion Pharmaceuticals: AI-first drug discovery at scale; multiple programmes in clinical trials
- Isomorphic Labs (Google DeepMind spinout): full-stack AI drug discovery; partnered with Eli Lilly and Novartis

**What hasn't been compressed**: Clinical trials are still slow, expensive, and necessary. Phase III trials involving 10,000+ patients cannot be AI-ised. The bottleneck is shifting from discovery to clinical validation.

**Structural implication**: Drug discovery is commoditising at the early stage. The moat is moving to clinical development expertise, regulatory relationships, and patient access. The integrated pharma model (discovery + development + manufacturing + distribution) is being challenged by AI-native discovery firms.

## Longevity science and the longevity economy

### The science

**Where longevity science is** (2024):
- **Hallmarks of ageing** (López-Otín et al., 2013, updated 2023): 12 identified biological mechanisms of ageing including genomic instability, telomere attrition, epigenetic alterations, senescent cell accumulation, mitochondrial dysfunction, chronic inflammation ("inflammageing"), and loss of proteostasis
- **Senolytics**: drugs that selectively clear senescent cells (zombie cells that contribute to inflammation and tissue dysfunction). Dasatinib + quercetin is the most-studied combination; Unity Biotechnology leading clinical programmes
- **NAD+ precursors** (NMN, NR): declining NAD+ is a hallmark of ageing; precursor supplementation shows animal evidence; human evidence mixed. Mainstream supplement adoption ahead of clinical evidence — a pattern in longevity.
- **Rapamycin**: mTOR inhibitor; the most consistently life-extending intervention in model organisms (mice, worms, flies). Off-label use in longevity medicine growing; clinical trials underway
- **GLP-1 agonists**: Semaglutide (Ozempic/Wegovy) primary application is diabetes/obesity, but emerging evidence of broader effects: reduced cardiovascular events, potential reduced Alzheimer's risk, anti-inflammatory effects. Could be the first genuinely "multi-dimensional" health drug.

**The honest assessment**: we can extend healthy lifespan in model organisms reliably. Human translation is much harder. The expected value of longevity interventions for humans (except the basics: exercise, sleep, not smoking, healthy weight) is still speculative. But the research infrastructure and investment are at a level that meaningful progress in the next decade is likely.

### The longevity economy

**The structural force**: even without dramatic lifespan extension, the existing demographic shift (ageing population staying healthier longer) is creating massive structural demand.

**Market dimensions**:
- Preventive medicine / executive health: comprehensive health screening, biomarker tracking, personalised protocols. Growing rapidly.
- Longevity clinics: Function Health, Fountain Life, Lifeforce, Human Longevity Inc. — premium concierge medicine with extensive diagnostics
- Supplement market: $180B globally; longevity supplements fastest growing segment
- Fitness / rehabilitation: driven by older cohort wanting to maintain physical function
- Travel intersection: medical tourism, wellness travel, "longevity retreats" — an emerging category where MENA (Dubai especially) is positioning aggressively

**For travel specifically**: "Wellness tourism" is a $650B market globally (Global Wellness Institute, 2023); fastest growing segment. Dubai's ambition to be a global wellness hub is a deliberate policy choice. This creates a structural alignment between Wego's MENA positioning and a growing sector.

## Synthetic biology

**What it is**: Engineering biology from the ground up — designing and building biological systems (organisms, metabolic pathways, cellular machinery) rather than modifying existing ones.

**Current applications**:
- **Biofuels and materials**: engineered microorganisms producing fuels, chemicals, and materials from biological feedstocks (replacing petrochemicals). Ginkgo Bioworks, Zymergen.
- **Food production**: precision fermentation producing animal proteins without animals (dairy proteins, egg whites). Perfect Day, Every Company.
- **Biomanufacturing**: producing pharmaceutical compounds via engineered organisms (more efficient than traditional synthesis).
- **Agriculture**: nitrogen-fixing microbiomes, pest-resistant crops, reduced-fertiliser farming.

**The platform thesis**: synthetic biology is a general-purpose technology, like computing. The infrastructure being built now (biological foundries, DNA synthesis, organism engineering tools) is the equivalent of the 1990s semiconductor infrastructure. The applications emerge over decades as the tools get cheaper and better.

**Timeline**: Platform commoditisation 2028–2035; broad industrial application 2030–2040; consumer-visible applications earlier (food, personal care).
