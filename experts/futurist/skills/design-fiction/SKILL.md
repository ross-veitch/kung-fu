# Design Fiction

## What design fiction is

**Design fiction** (coined by Julian Bleecker, developed by Bruce Sterling, practiced by IDEO, Superflux, Near Future Laboratory) is the use of fictional artefacts to make futures concrete and experienceable. Instead of describing a future abstractly ("in 2030, AI agents will autonomously book travel"), design fiction makes that future legible by presenting an artefact that would exist within it — a screenshot of an AI agent's conversation, a fake product manual, a newspaper front page from that world, a scene from inside the future.

**Why it works**: Abstract scenarios engage the analytical mind. Design fiction engages the visceral, emotional mind. When you read a fictional scene from inside a future, you stop debating whether the future is plausible and start asking: "How do I feel about this? What does this mean for us? Do we want this world?"

**What design fiction is not**: Science fiction entertainment. The goal is not narrative quality but strategic utility — to make a future real enough to generate a genuine response and surface hidden assumptions.

## Diegetic prototypes

A **diegetic prototype** is an artefact that would exist in the world of the fiction — not a prototype of the real product, but a prototype of what the product or experience would be *within* the fictional future.

**Examples**:
- A screenshot of a mobile app UI from 2030 (even if the app doesn't exist)
- A fake product packaging for a synthetic food company in a world where lab meat has won
- A terms-of-service document from an AI travel agent that books on your behalf
- A hotel loyalty program FAQ explaining how AI-agent bookings earn points
- A HR policy document for hiring autonomous AI employees

**Why diegetic prototypes are powerful**:
- They force specificity: you can't write a fake UI without deciding what the product actually does
- They surface contradictions: writing a fake ToS for an AI agent exposes all the edge cases (who's liable if the agent books wrong? Can the agent override your preferences?)
- They generate conversation: people react to artefacts more directly than to abstractions
- They're memorable: a well-constructed design fiction is retained long after a strategic deck is forgotten

## Core writing techniques

### Write from inside the future, not about it

**Bad (describing the future)**:
> "By 2030, AI agents will be booking most travel. Travellers will interact with agents via natural language. OTAs will shift to API-first models."

**Good (writing from inside the future)**:
> *Thursday, March 14 2030, 8:47 AM*
> 
> Layla opened her phone to find three messages from Atlas, her travel agent. Atlas had spotted a 40% fare drop on Emirates DXB→LHR for the dates her calendar showed she had a board meeting in London. It had pre-staged two options — one business class with a two-night hotel near Canary Wharf, one economy plus a day hotel for a nap before the evening meeting — and attached a single-tap confirmation button to each.
> 
> She tapped the first option without opening her email.
> 
> Atlas had already forwarded the confirmation to her PA, updated her Google Calendar, and added the hotel's gym timetable to her morning briefing. Her Wego loyalty points were auto-applied. She'd vaguely remember booking this flight somewhere around her third coffee.

The second version makes the future *real*. It surfaces specific questions: what is Atlas? Who built it? How did it get access to her calendar? What happened to the OTA she used to book on?

### Use the mundane, not the spectacular

The most effective design fiction is boring. Not: "In 2030, the first human-AI hybrid CEO was appointed." But: "The quarterly travel expense report arrived at 6 AM, automatically compiled by her company's AI travel agent and cross-referenced against policy. Two bookings had been flagged for review — one a 15% above-policy hotel rate (justified by proximity to the venue), one an upgrade to business class on a red-eye (Atlas had noted her calendar showed an 8 AM meeting the next morning and applied her stated preference)."

The mundane fiction is more strategically useful because it forces specifics:
- Who is making these judgments? The AI or the human?
- What are the approval flows?
- How are policies encoded?
- What happens when the AI is wrong?

### Authentic artefact formats

Choose formats that would genuinely exist in the future world:

| Format | When to use |
|---|---|
| News article / headline | Announcing that something has happened; most effective for shocking or surprising futures |
| Product review (e.g. App Store review) | Consumer reaction to a new product or experience |
| Internal memo / policy document | Corporate or institutional response to a structural change |
| Customer service conversation | Revealing how a system handles edge cases; surfaces failure modes |
| Job posting | What skills the future economy needs; what kinds of companies exist |
| Social media post / thread | Authentic public reaction; ambient cultural signals |
| Mobile app screenshot | Making a digital product concrete |
| User journey / task flow | Showing a process end-to-end in the future world |

## Design fiction as strategic tool

### Surfacing hidden assumptions

Write a piece of design fiction set in your feared future (the one your current strategy implicitly assumes won't happen). Read it in a room with your leadership team.

**What happens**: People who have been abstractly nodding at "the AI disruption scenario" suddenly have a concrete reaction. "We'd never let an AI make booking decisions without human approval" — wait, why not? What is the actual assumption there? Is it about trust, liability, customer preference, or competitive dynamics?

The fiction surfaces the assumption. Then you can examine it.

**Exercise**: Write a design fiction where your company's biggest existing product is irrelevant. Make it as credible as possible. Read it in a leadership offsite. The discomfort is the signal.

### Wind-tunnel testing via fiction

Write design fiction for each of your scenario quadrants. Make the artefacts as authentic as possible (real product names, real company names). Circulate to the leadership team with a single question: "Does this world feel possible? What would you do if you knew this was coming?"

This is the visceral complement to the analytical wind-tunnel test in scenario planning. The analytical version tells you which strategies are robust; the fiction tells you whether the team *feels* the urgency.

### Executive communication

Futures-oriented presentations often fail because the audience is disengaged — they've seen too many "disruption" decks. Design fiction as a communication device re-engages:

1. **Open with a scene**: two paragraphs of design fiction that drops the audience into the world you're about to describe analytically
2. **Use diegetic artefacts as slides**: a fake screenshot, a fake product review, a fake news headline — interspersed with analytical slides
3. **Close with an artefact from the desired future**: what does success look like, made concrete

The combination of fiction (visceral) + analysis (rational) + strategic implication (actionable) is more effective than any of the three alone.

## The Wego-relevant futures canon

Specific design fiction scenarios that are strategically relevant for a MENA travel company:

### "The Atlas Booking"
*Scene*: A Dubai business executive delegates a complex multi-city trip (Dubai → Singapore → Tokyo → Dubai, 10 days, three sets of meetings, family extension onto the end) to an AI agent. The agent proposes, negotiates, adapts, and books — in real time, over 8 minutes. The human's role is to review and approve. Surfaces: does Wego have an agent API? What is the booking experience when no human is directly engaged?

### "The Visa-Free Corridor"
*Scene*: It's 2028, and the Saudi-Israel normalisation has been complete for two years. A Tel Aviv family is booking their first Umrah + Saudi tourism experience, guided through a Saudi tourism platform that Wego powers. Surfaces: is Wego positioned for the corridors that don't exist yet?

### "The Empty Business Class"
*Scene*: It's 2031, and corporate travel policy has been AI-optimised. WegoPro's AI travel manager has cut Wego Corporate Client's business travel costs by 40% without reducing trips — by automatically identifying when business class is necessary (red-eye before 8 AM meeting) vs unnecessary (3-hour daytime flight). Surfaces: does WegoPro have the analytical capabilities to deliver this? Who owns the travel policy logic?

### "The Bleisure Booking"
*Scene*: It's 2027, and Wego's AI layer has identified that 34% of the DXB-BKK route is booked Friday-Sunday with Monday extensions — a bleisure pattern. Wego has built a "bleisure mode" that automatically surfaces weekend activities, hotel extensions, and restaurant recommendations aligned with the traveller's revealed preferences. A traveller books what appears to be a standard work trip and wakes up to a fully personalised Friday-evening itinerary. Surfaces: is Wego a booking engine or an experience layer?

## Quick design fiction prompt format

When asked to produce design fiction, structure the output as:

```
**Artefact type**: [news article / product review / memo / scene / etc.]
**Scenario**: [which scenario world this comes from]
**Year**: [specific year]
**Artefact**:

[The actual fictional content]

---
**What this surfaces**:
- [Assumption 1 made explicit by the fiction]
- [Assumption 2]
- [Strategic question this artefact raises]
```
