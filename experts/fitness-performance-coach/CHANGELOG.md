### Key findings

**1. HIIT elicits broad molecular + hormonal adaptations in trained athletes (2025/2026)**
An 8-week HIIT intervention in national-level flatwater kayak athletes showed significant increases in IGF-1R, MMP-4, MMP-9, TNF-α, and serum testosterone, alongside meaningful improvements at the second ventilatory threshold and in 200m/1000m time trials. *Takeaway: HIIT drives coordinated anabolic, remodeling, and inflammatory signaling simultaneously — not just cardiovascular adaptation.*

**2. Same-session vs. alternated weekly RT+HIIT — no significant difference in outcomes (2025)**
A study comparing RT+HIIT within the same session vs. alternated weekly found no significant group differences in body composition, VO₂max, or muscular fitness after 8 weeks. Both protocols improved body fat %, skeletal muscle mass, and max power. *Takeaway: Scheduling flexibility matters more than sequencing dogma for recreational athletes — removes a key programming constraint.*

**3. HRV bibliometrics 2010–2025: fatigue/readiness prediction is the dominant emerging trend**
A comprehensive bibliometric analysis of 1,660 HRV sports science papers found that HRV applications are shifting toward multi-source integration and ML-powered fatigue/readiness prediction. External load intensity indicators (high-intensity jumps/accelerations) proved more sensitive to fatigue than accumulated volume. *Takeaway: HRV + external load intensity (not just volume) is the emerging gold standard for training readiness monitoring.*

**4. Aerobic vs. resistance training have opposing effects on PTX3 (inflammation marker)**
Meta-analysis: aerobic training significantly *increased* PTX3 (SMD=0.71), resistance training significantly *decreased* it (SMD=-0.69), HIIT had no effect. Effect was age-moderated — significant elevation in over-50s only for aerobic exercise. *Takeaway: Modality choice has distinct inflammatory signatures; for executives managing chronic low-grade inflammation, resistance training has an edge over pure aerobic.*
