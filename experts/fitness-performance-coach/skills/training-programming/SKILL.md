# Training Programming

## The hierarchy of physical adaptation

Not all training produces the same adaptations. Programming intelligently means knowing what you're developing and at what timescale:

| Adaptation | Primary stimulus | Timescale |
|------------|-----------------|-----------|
| VO2 max | Zone 4–5 intervals | 6–12 weeks |
| Mitochondrial density | Zone 2 (MAF pace) | 12+ weeks |
| Muscular strength | Compound lifting, 3–5 rep range | 6–8 weeks per block |
| Muscular hypertrophy | Volume work, 6–12 rep range | 8–12 weeks |
| Movement skill | Skill practice, technical work | Ongoing |
| Recovery capacity | Deload, sleep, nutrition | Days–weeks |

The two training qualities with the highest ROI for longevity and general performance: **VO2 max** (strongest predictor of all-cause mortality) and **strength** (grip strength, leg press power predict independent function into old age).

## Zone training framework

Heart rate zones (% of max heart rate). Individual variation exists — these are starting points:

| Zone | % HRmax | Feel | Purpose |
|------|---------|------|---------|
| Zone 1 | < 60% | Very easy, can hold full conversation | Recovery, warm-up |
| Zone 2 | 60–70% | Comfortable, can converse in sentences | Aerobic base, fat oxidation, mitochondrial density |
| Zone 3 | 70–80% | Moderate effort, short sentences | "No man's land" — not easy enough to build base, not hard enough to build top end |
| Zone 4 | 80–90% | Hard, phrases only | Lactate threshold, race pace work |
| Zone 5 | > 90% | Maximum, cannot speak | VO2 max intervals |

**The Zone 3 trap**: most amateur athletes spend too much time in Zone 3. The evidence-backed approach (used by elite Norwegian endurance athletes) is polarised: ~80% Zone 1–2, ~20% Zone 4–5. Minimise Zone 3.

## VO2 max development protocol (Norwegian 4×4)

The most evidence-backed HIIT protocol for VO2 max gains:
- **4 intervals of 4 minutes** at 90–95% HRmax
- **3 minutes active recovery** at ~60% HRmax between intervals
- **Frequency**: 2× per week maximum (requires full recovery between sessions)
- **Warm-up**: 10 minutes at Zone 2
- **Total session**: ~40–45 minutes
- **Expected gains**: +3–5 ml/kg/min VO2 max in 8–10 weeks

Important: VO2 max intervals require high recovery. Do not run them on consecutive days or after high-volume strength sessions.

## Periodisation principles

**Periodisation** = systematic variation of training load over time to drive adaptation while avoiding staleness and injury.

**Macrocycle**: full training plan, typically 12–24 weeks
**Mesocycle**: training block, typically 4–6 weeks with a specific focus
**Microcycle**: individual week of training

Within each mesocycle: **progressive overload** (increase load/volume/intensity week-over-week) followed by a **deload week** (reduce volume by 40–50%) to allow supercompensation.

Simple periodisation model for concurrent training (strength + endurance):
- Week 1: Base volume, moderate intensity
- Week 2: +5–10% volume
- Week 3: +5–10% volume again
- Week 4: Deload (60% of week 3 volume)

## Concurrent training management

When training both strength and endurance, manage interference effects:
- **Same-day sequencing**: strength before endurance. Endurance before strength impairs strength output and potentially blunts hypertrophy response.
- **Separation**: 6+ hours between strength and high-intensity endurance sessions on the same day
- **Frequency balance**: 2–3 strength sessions per week + 3–4 endurance sessions covers most goals without accumulated fatigue

## Readiness-based adjustments

Biometric readiness (HRV, sleep score, resting HR trend) should gate training intensity:
- **High readiness** (HRV above 7-day average, good sleep): execute planned session or slightly exceed
- **Moderate readiness** (near average): execute planned session, don't push beyond
- **Low readiness** (HRV depressed, poor sleep, elevated RHR): drop to Zone 1–2 only, reduce strength load by 20–30%, consider full rest
