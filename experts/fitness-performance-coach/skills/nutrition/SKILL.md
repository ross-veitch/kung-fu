# Performance Nutrition

## The nutrition hierarchy

Like recovery, nutrition impact is tiered. Optimise in order:

1. **Total calories** — eating enough to support training and recovery
2. **Protein** — sufficient for muscle protein synthesis (MPS)
3. **Carbohydrates** — the primary fuel for high-intensity work
4. **Fats** — hormonal health, fat-soluble vitamins, satiety
5. **Nutrient timing** — when you eat relative to training
6. **Supplements** — small additional benefit once 1–5 are optimised

Most people skip to 6 while ignoring 1–2. Don't.

## Protein requirements

**For recreational athletes and performance-focused individuals**:
- Minimum for muscle preservation: 1.4g/kg bodyweight/day
- Optimal for hypertrophy and recomposition: **1.6–2.2g/kg/day**
- Upper end (during calorie deficit or heavy training): 2.4g/kg/day

**Distribution matters**: 4–5 meals of 30–40g protein each produces better MPS than the same total consumed in 1–2 sittings. This is because MPS is maximised at ~0.4g/kg per meal — eating 100g at once does not produce 3× the response.

**Timing**: Protein within 2 hours post-training is beneficial but not as critical as once believed. Total daily intake matters more than the exact timing window.

**Sources**: Complete proteins (meat, fish, eggs, dairy, soy) provide all essential amino acids. Leucine content is the key trigger for MPS — 2.5–3g leucine per meal is the threshold to maximise MPS response.

## Carbohydrates and energy systems

Carbohydrates are the primary fuel for glycolytic (high-intensity) work. Fat fuels Zone 1–2 aerobic work. Both are always in use; the ratio shifts with intensity.

**Fuelling high-intensity work**:
- Zone 4–5 intervals and resistance training are glycogen-dependent
- Inadequate carbohydrate impairs performance at these intensities and impairs recovery
- For athletes training > 1 hour/day at moderate-high intensity: 5–7g CHO/kg/day

**Carbohydrate periodisation**:
- **Train high, compete high**: adequate carbohydrate for intense sessions and competitions
- **Train low, sleep low**: strategically depleted glycogen states for Zone 2 sessions to enhance fat oxidation adaptation (used by elite endurance athletes; not required for general fitness)
- For most non-elite athletes: simply ensuring adequate total intake is sufficient; periodisation adds marginal benefit

**Intra-training fuelling**:
- Sessions < 60 minutes: glycogen stores are typically sufficient; no intra-workout carbs needed
- Sessions 60–90 minutes at moderate intensity: optional fuelling; may help with energy and focus
- Sessions > 90 minutes or multi-day events: 30–60g CHO/hour; up to 90g/hour with glucose:fructose mix (2:1 ratio — utilises both intestinal transporters)

## Energy availability and RED-S

**Low Energy Availability (LEA)** occurs when caloric intake is insufficient to support both training and basic physiological functions. LEA causes:
- Hormonal suppression (testosterone in males, oestrogen in females)
- Bone density reduction
- Immune suppression
- Impaired recovery and adaptation
- Cognitive impairment

**Relative Energy Deficiency in Sport (RED-S)** is the clinical syndrome. Common in endurance athletes, weight-category sports, and aesthetics-focused training.

**Minimum energy availability**: ~30 kcal/kg fat-free mass/day to maintain basic physiological function. Optimal performance: 45 kcal/kg FFM/day.

**Signs of LEA**: Unexplained fatigue, persistent illness, stress fractures, hormonal disruption, mood decline, poor adaptation to training. **Escalation trigger**: suspected RED-S warrants referral to a sports physician and registered dietitian.

## Evidence-based supplements

Ranked by strength of evidence for performance:

| Supplement | Evidence | Application | Dose |
|-----------|----------|-------------|------|
| Creatine monohydrate | ★★★★★ | Strength, power, muscle mass, cognition | 3–5g/day (no loading needed) |
| Caffeine | ★★★★★ | Endurance, strength, alertness | 3–6mg/kg, 45–60min pre-training |
| Beta-alanine | ★★★★ | Buffering lactate in 1–4 min efforts | 3.2–6.4g/day (split doses; causes paresthesia) |
| Sodium bicarbonate | ★★★ | Buffering in 1–7 min efforts | 0.3g/kg, 60–90min pre-event (GI risk) |
| Citrulline malate | ★★★ | Endurance, reduced fatigue | 6–8g, 60min pre-training |
| Omega-3 (EPA/DHA) | ★★★ | Inflammation, joint health, MPS | 2–4g EPA+DHA/day |
| Vitamin D3 + K2 | ★★★ | Bone, immune, hormone (if deficient) | 2000–4000 IU D3 + 100mcg K2 |
| Magnesium glycinate | ★★★ | Sleep quality, cramp prevention | 200–400mg before bed |
| Beetroot / nitrates | ★★★ | Endurance performance, VO2 economy | 500mg nitrate (~500ml beetroot juice), 2.5hrs pre-event |

**What to avoid**: Most pre-workout products contain proprietary blends with underdosed actives and overhyped ingredients. Caffeine + creatine + beta-alanine achieves more than 99% of what a $60 pre-workout promises.

## Body composition

**Cutting (fat loss) while preserving muscle**:
- Calorie deficit: 300–500 kcal/day below TDEE; > 500 kcal increases muscle loss risk
- Protein: increase to 2.0–2.4g/kg during deficit to offset elevated catabolism
- Resistance training: must be maintained during a cut to preserve lean mass
- Rate: 0.5–1% bodyweight/week is sustainable; faster losses increasingly compromise muscle

**Bulking (muscle gain)**:
- Calorie surplus: 200–400 kcal/day above TDEE; larger surplus → more fat gain, not more muscle
- Protein: 1.6–2.2g/kg
- Rate: 0.25–0.5% bodyweight/week is the realistic maximum for trained athletes (newbies can do more)

**Recomposition** (simultaneous fat loss and muscle gain):
- Possible at lower training levels and for beginners; very slow for trained athletes
- Requires precise calorie balance, adequate protein, and consistent training
- More realistic approach than most "recomp" content suggests
