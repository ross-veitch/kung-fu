# Recovery and Adaptation

## The recovery hierarchy

Recovery is not optional. It is 50% of the training stimulus. Rank recovery modalities by impact:

1. **Sleep** (by far the most important — non-negotiable foundation)
2. **Nutrition** (protein, carbohydrates around training, total calorie adequacy)
3. **Stress management** (psychological stress impairs physical recovery via cortisol)
4. **Deload / periodisation** (planned recovery built into the programme)
5. **Active recovery** (Zone 1 movement, walking, light mobility work)
6. **Heat stress** (sauna — meaningful evidence base; Rhonda Patrick protocols)
7. **Cold stress** (ice bath, cold shower — timing and application matter)
8. **Other modalities** (massage, compression, foam rolling — benefit modest)

Optimising tier 7 while ignoring tier 1 is the most common mistake in recovery practice.

## Sleep

**Why it matters**: Sleep is when growth hormone is released, when muscle protein synthesis peaks, when cortisol resets, and when the glymphatic system clears metabolic waste (including amyloid precursors). A single night of < 6 hours impairs reaction time, mood, immune function, and anabolic hormone status measurably.

**Targets**:
- Duration: 7–9 hours for most adults; athletes training > 10 hours/week may need 9–10
- Consistency: same bed and wake time ± 30 minutes is more important than total duration
- Quality: minimize wake time after sleep onset (WASO); prioritise slow-wave (deep) and REM sleep

**Sleep architecture** (average per 8-hour night):
- Light sleep (N1/N2): ~55%
- Deep sleep (N3/SWS): ~15–20% — peaks in first half of night; impaired by alcohol
- REM sleep: ~20–25% — peaks in second half of night; impaired by disrupted sleep timing

**Sleep hygiene essentials**:
- Temperature: 18–20°C room temperature; cooling body drives sleep onset
- Light: blackout curtains or eye mask; blue light avoidance 1–2 hours before bed
- Alcohol: impairs sleep architecture, suppresses REM — even moderate amounts
- Exercise timing: intense exercise within 2 hours of bed can delay sleep onset for some
- Caffeine half-life is 5–6 hours — noon caffeine cutoff for most people

**Tracking sleep quality** (wearables):
- Oura Ring and WHOOP have the best validated sleep staging via PPG
- Deep sleep % and REM % are more informative than total duration
- Heart rate during sleep: lower is better; should approach resting HR
- HRV during sleep: trends up during recovery; down during illness or overtraining

## HRV interpretation

**What HRV measures**: Heart Rate Variability — beat-to-beat variation in heart rate. Higher HRV generally = better autonomic nervous system (ANS) recovery. Lower HRV = higher sympathetic tone (stress, fatigue, illness).

**The right way to read HRV**:
- **Never use a single morning value in isolation** — always use the 7-day rolling average as the baseline
- **The direction of the trend matters more than the absolute number** — HRV is highly individual; a "low" number for one athlete is normal for another
- **Morning orthostatic protocol** (most accurate): 5 minutes lying still, then stand — HRV drops on standing; a larger-than-usual drop = low readiness

**GREEN/YELLOW/RED readiness classification**:

| Status | Criteria | Training recommendation |
|--------|----------|------------------------|
| 🟢 GREEN | HRV ≥ 7-day average; sleep score > 70; subjective feel 7+/10 | Execute planned session; may push slightly |
| 🟡 YELLOW | HRV within 1 SD below average; sleep 50–70; subjective feel 5–6/10 | Execute planned session; do not exceed target intensity |
| 🔴 RED | HRV > 1 SD below average; or sleep < 50; or subjective feel ≤ 4/10 | Zone 1–2 only, or full rest; address cause |
| 🚨 ESCALATE | HRV depressed > 3 consecutive days; or elevated RHR > 5bpm × 3+ days | Stop training; assess for illness/overtraining; may need medical review |

**What drives HRV down**:
- Poor sleep (strongest correlate)
- Alcohol (dramatic suppressor — even 1–2 drinks)
- Illness (often shows HRV drop 1–2 days before symptoms)
- Psychological stress
- Training load exceeding recovery capacity
- Travel, especially across time zones

## Deload mechanics

**What a deload achieves**: Supercompensation — the body adapts to training stimulus during recovery. Without a deload, accumulated fatigue masks fitness gains. A planned deload every 3–4 weeks allows fitness to "surface."

**Standard deload protocol**:
- **Volume**: Reduce by 40–50% (halve the total sets or mileage)
- **Intensity**: Maintain at ~85–90% of normal (keep training quality; just do less)
- **Frequency**: Can reduce by 1 session if desired
- **Duration**: Typically 5–7 days

**Signs a deload is needed sooner than scheduled**:
- Performance declining despite adequate sleep and nutrition
- Persistent HRV suppression (3+ days below normal)
- Elevated RHR (3+ days above normal)
- Persistent muscle soreness that doesn't resolve between sessions
- Mood and motivation decline (often the earliest indicator)

## Heat stress (sauna)

**Evidence base**: Robust. Multiple RCTs and large epidemiological studies (Laukkanen et al., Finland). Sauna use 4–7×/week associated with 40% reduction in all-cause mortality vs. 1×/week.

**Performance benefits**:
- Plasma volume expansion (improves endurance; similar to altitude adaptation)
- Heat shock protein upregulation (cytoprotective)
- GH spike (sauna immediately post-training amplifies GH response)
- Cardiovascular conditioning effect independent of exercise

**Protocol for performance**:
- Temperature: 80–100°C (traditional Finnish sauna) or 50–60°C (infrared — lower evidence base)
- Duration: 15–20 minutes per session
- Frequency: 4+ sessions/week for maximum benefit; 2–3×/week for moderate benefit
- Timing: post-training is optimal (GH synergy); not immediately before sleep (core temp elevation can delay sleep onset)
- Hydration: replace fluid losses (approximately 500ml per 15-minute session)

## Cold stress (ice bath / cold shower)

**Important caveat on timing**: Cold water immersion (CWI) post-resistance training blunts the hypertrophic adaptation by suppressing the inflammatory signalling cascade required for muscle protein synthesis. Use with care around strength sessions.

**Where cold is beneficial**:
- After endurance training (reduces delayed onset muscle soreness; speeds return to training)
- On recovery days (vagal nerve stimulation, mood, alertness)
- Not immediately after strength training if hypertrophy is a goal

**Protocol**:
- Temperature: 10–15°C (effective range; colder is not necessarily better)
- Duration: 5–10 minutes; onset of benefits occurs by ~3 minutes
- Cold shower alternative: less evidence but accessible; 2–3 minutes at coldest tap temperature
