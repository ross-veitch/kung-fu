# Strength Training

Strength training for the performance-focused adult: programming paradigms, concurrent training management, progressive overload mechanics, and the principles that separate sustainable long-term development from short-term thrashing.

---

## Programming paradigms

### 5/3/1 (Wendler)

The most widely adopted intermediate/advanced strength programme. Built around four main lifts (squat, deadlift, bench press, overhead press), cycled across 4-week waves with defined percentage-based loading.

**Structure**: 3 weeks of increasing intensity (65–75%, 70–80%, 75–85% of training max) + 1 deload week at 40–60%. Training max is set at ~90% of true 1RM to ensure all reps are completable with good technique.

**Variants**: *Beyond 5/3/1* (joker sets, first-set-last FSL backdowns); *5/3/1 for Beginners* (3× weekly, 3 main lifts per session); *Building the Monolith* (volume-focused mass phase). Choose based on training age and goal.

**Best for**: Intermediate to advanced lifters who want a simple, durable strength programme that can run alongside endurance training without excessive fatigue.

### GZCLP (Tier-Based Training)

A tier-based system developed by Cody Lefever. Divides movements into T1 (heavy compound 5×3+, main strength work), T2 (moderate compound volume 3×10, hypertrophy/back-off), and T3 (isolation work 3×15+). Progress T1 by weight, T2 by weight, T3 by reps.

**Best for**: Beginners and early intermediates who want more volume than Starting Strength/StrongLifts but less complexity than 5/3/1.

### Reactive Training Systems (RTS) / Autoregulation

RPE-based training where load is prescribed relative to perceived effort, not fixed percentages. RPE 10 = max effort; RPE 8 = 2 reps left in the tank. Allows load to fluctuate day-to-day with actual readiness.

**Key concept**: *e1RM back-calculation* — if you hit RPE 8 at 100kg for 5 reps, the system estimates your daily 1RM and adjusts future sets accordingly.

**Best for**: Experienced lifters with good RPE calibration; athletes with high variability in daily readiness (e.g., concurrent endurance athletes).

### Conjugate / Concurrent (Westside)

Originally a powerlifting system (Louie Simmons) built around rotating max effort and dynamic effort days across upper/lower splits. Technically complex; less applicable to endurance-concurrent athletes due to high CNS demand frequency.

**What to borrow**: the *dynamic effort* concept (sub-maximal loads moved with maximal intent — 60–70% for speed/power development) is useful for athletes who want to maintain power qualities without heavy loading.

### Block Periodisation for Strength

Strength-focused mesocycles structured as discrete blocks:
- **Accumulation** (3–4 weeks): moderate intensity, high volume; build work capacity
- **Intensification** (3–4 weeks): higher intensity, moderate volume; build strength expression
- **Realisation/Peaking** (1–2 weeks): low volume, high intensity; peak performance

Useful when building into a specific event (race, competition, testing day). Allows deliberate periodisation of strength alongside an endurance calendar.

## Concurrent training: strength + endurance

The *interference effect* (Hickson, 1980) established that concurrent training can compromise strength and hypertrophy adaptations when volumes are high. Subsequent research (Wilson et al. 2012 meta-analysis) quantified this: hypertrophy is most affected, strength to a lesser degree, power/force development most severely.

### Sequencing rules

**Same-day sessions:**
- Strength before endurance: strength is a higher-CNS-demand activity; doing it first preserves quality
- Exception: if endurance session is low-intensity (Zone 1–2), order matters less
- Minimum gap between same-day sessions: 6–8 hours if possible; 3+ hours at minimum
- Never: high-intensity intervals followed immediately by heavy strength work

**Weekly sequencing:**
- Separate high-intensity endurance from heavy strength by ≥48 hours
- Lower-body strength and high-intensity running share muscle groups — treat them as competing stressors
- Upper-body strength days are relatively compatible with running sessions same-day

### Minimising interference

1. **Prioritise** — decide which quality is primary for the current phase (strength or endurance) and programme accordingly. The secondary quality goes into maintenance mode.
2. **Reduce strength frequency** during endurance-heavy blocks: 2× per week strength is typically sufficient for maintenance; 3× for active development
3. **Prefer lower-body strength exercises with low eccentric damage** (trap bar deadlift, leg press) during heavy running phases — eccentric loading from squats + downhill running accumulates rapidly
4. **Monitor CTL/ATL** (Training Peaks) — when ATL/acute load spikes, reduce strength volume before reducing running quality

## Progressive overload principles

The cardinal rule of adaptation: the training stimulus must exceed current capacity, followed by adequate recovery. Four primary overload mechanisms:

| Mechanism | How applied | Example |
|-----------|-------------|---------|
| **Load** | Add weight | +2.5kg each session (novice), monthly (advanced) |
| **Volume** | Add sets or reps | Move from 3×5 to 4×5 before adding weight |
| **Density** | Reduce rest periods | Same work in less time |
| **Specificity** | More challenging variation | Progress goblet squat → front squat → back squat |

**Novice progression**: linear (add weight each session) — works for 3–6 months
**Intermediate**: weekly linear or wave periodisation
**Advanced**: monthly progress targets; undulating periodisation

### Deload mechanics for strength athletes

**When**: every 4–8 weeks (programme-dependent), or reactively (if HRV/readiness trend drops, or accumulated fatigue is evident)

**Types**:
- **Volume deload**: same weight, 40–50% of normal sets/reps
- **Intensity deload**: same volume, 60–70% of normal weights
- **Full deload**: very light technique work, active recovery emphasis

Post-deload supercompensation typically peaks at 3–5 days after the deload ends — plan heavy sessions accordingly.

## Strength for endurance athletes: specific application

Endurance athletes benefit from strength training primarily through:
1. **Running economy** — stronger hips, glutes, and calves reduce energy cost per stride
2. **Injury resilience** — stronger connective tissue and supporting musculature reduces overuse injury risk
3. **Power at lactate threshold** — particularly relevant for 5K–half marathon distances

### Priority exercises for runners

| Exercise | Why it matters |
|----------|---------------|
| Single-leg RDL | Hip hinge strength, glute/hamstring, addresses left-right asymmetries |
| Bulgarian split squat | Unilateral leg strength without spinal loading; mimics running mechanics |
| Trap bar deadlift | Full posterior chain; lower eccentric DOMS than barbell; power transfer |
| Nordic hamstring curl | Eccentric hamstring strength; strong evidence for reducing hamstring strain |
| Calf raises (single-leg, slow eccentric) | Achilles tendon loading; high Achilles injury risk in runners |
| Hip thrust / glute bridge | Glute activation; key for propulsion |

### Programming during race build

**Base phase** (high aerobic volume): 2–3× strength per week, moderate intensity (RPE 6–7), prioritise movement quality
**Build phase** (adding intensity): 2× strength per week, maintain strength without adding fatigue
**Peak/taper** (2–3 weeks pre-race): 1× light strength per week; movement maintenance only; no new stimulus

## Common technique faults

### Squat
- Butt wink (lumbar flexion at bottom): usually limited hip mobility or excessive depth for current mobility; address with mobility work and box squat
- Knee cave (valgus collapse): weak glutes/abductors; cue: "spread the floor" / drive knees out
- Good morning squat (hips shoot up first): quad weakness causing bar to shift onto back extensors; box squat, pause squat

### Deadlift  
- Rounded lower back: typically too much weight, or poor bracing; cue: "chest up, proud chest" before initiating pull
- Bar drifting forward: bar should stay in contact with legs throughout; cue: "push the floor away" not "pull the bar up"
- Hyperextension at lockout: excessive lumbar extension at top; cue: "tall and tight" — glutes squeezed, not leaning back

### Overhead press
- Excessive lumbar arch: weak core bracing; cue: "ribs down" before pressing
- Bar path drifting forward: drive bar back over the ears, not forward
- Early arm lockout without shoulder elevation: finish the press by shrugging at the top
