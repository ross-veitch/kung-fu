# /recovery-protocol — Design a recovery protocol based on biometric state

**Purpose**: Prescribe a structured recovery intervention when biometric data signals fatigue, overtraining, or reduced readiness.

**When to use**: When HRV is suppressed, sleep quality is degraded, resting HR is elevated, or subjective readiness is low for ≥3 consecutive days.

---

## Phase 1: Assess the state

Recovery protocols are not one-size-fits-all. The intervention depends on the type and severity of fatigue.

### **Biometric inputs**
Review the last 7 days of data:

**HRV (heart rate variability)**
- 7-day rolling average vs baseline
- Day-to-day volatility (stable vs erratic)
- Absolute value compared to personal norm

**Resting heart rate**
- Elevated ≥5 bpm above baseline → sympathetic dominance (fight-or-flight)
- Stable or slightly below baseline → parasympathetic recovery happening

**Sleep metrics**
- Total sleep time (TST): <7h consistently → sleep debt accumulating
- Deep sleep %: <15% → insufficient physical recovery
- REM sleep %: <20% → insufficient cognitive/emotional recovery
- Sleep efficiency: <85% → poor sleep quality (frequent waking)

**Readiness score** (Oura, WHOOP, Garmin)
- Green (≥70): proceed with planned training
- Yellow (50–69): modify intensity or volume
- Red (<50): mandatory recovery day

**Subjective feel**
Never ignore how the athlete feels. If biometrics say "green" but subjective energy is low, trust the human signal.

### **Classify the fatigue type**

| Fatigue type | Signals | Root cause |
|--------------|---------|------------|
| **Acute (short-term)** | HRV down 1–2 days, bounces back quickly | Single hard session, poor sleep night, travel |
| **Accumulated (moderate)** | HRV down 3–5 days, resting HR elevated, sleep disrupted | Training block without adequate recovery, life stress |
| **Overreaching (severe)** | HRV down >7 days, persistent fatigue, performance plateau or decline | Chronic underrecovery, excessive volume or intensity |
| **Non-training stress** | HRV down, but training load is low | Work stress, illness, relationship issues, poor nutrition |

Each requires a different response.

## Phase 2: Prescribe the protocol

### **For acute fatigue (1–2 days)**
**Intervention**: Active recovery day
- **Training**: Low-intensity only (Zone 1–2, conversational pace, <60 min)
- **Sleep**: Prioritize 8+ hours; consider earlier bedtime
- **Nutrition**: Normal intake; ensure adequate carbs and protein
- **Stress**: Reduce non-training stressors where possible (decline optional meetings, defer non-urgent work)

**Expected recovery**: 24–48 hours. Resume normal training when HRV returns to baseline.

### **For accumulated fatigue (3–5 days)**
**Intervention**: Structured deload
- **Training**: Cut volume by 50%, maintain intensity only on key sessions
  - If 5 sessions/week normally → reduce to 2–3
  - Keep 1–2 high-quality sessions (e.g., threshold intervals, heavy lifts) but reduce duration
  - Everything else is Zone 1–2 active recovery or complete rest
- **Sleep**: Non-negotiable 8+ hours; consider a nap (20–30 min) if feasible
- **Nutrition**: Maintain protein (1.6–2.2 g/kg/day); increase carbs slightly to support glycogen replenishment
- **Modalities**: Add heat (sauna 15–20 min post-training), cold (ice bath or cold shower, 3–5 min), or contrast (alternating hot/cold)
- **Duration**: 5–7 days

**Expected recovery**: HRV should normalize within 5–7 days. If not, escalate to overreaching protocol.

### **For overreaching (>7 days of suppressed HRV)**
**Intervention**: Full recovery week
- **Training**: Complete rest for 3–4 days, then very light active recovery only (walking, easy swimming, yoga)
  - No high-intensity
  - No heavy lifting
  - No long endurance sessions
- **Sleep**: Aggressive sleep prioritization (8.5–9h/night); sleep debt must be cleared
- **Nutrition**: Maintain caloric intake (do not cut calories during recovery); emphasize anti-inflammatory foods (omega-3s, colorful vegetables)
- **Modalities**: Daily sauna (20 min, 170–180°F), contrast therapy, massage or soft tissue work
- **Mental reset**: This is not "lost training" — this is the training. Adaptation happens during recovery, not during stress.

**Expected recovery**: 7–14 days. If HRV does not normalize after 14 days of full recovery, consider:
- Medical evaluation (illness, hormonal imbalance, chronic inflammation)
- Training programme audit (volume/intensity may be structurally unsustainable)
- Life stress audit (work, relationships, finances — training stress is only one input)

### **For non-training stress**
**Intervention**: Maintain light training, address the root stressor
- **Training**: Keep sessions short and easy (Zone 1–2, 30–45 min max)
  - Training can be therapeutic (releases endorphins, provides structure)
  - But do not add training stress on top of life stress
- **Sleep**: Highest priority — sleep debt amplifies stress response
- **Stress management**: Meditation, breathwork (box breathing, 4-7-8), journaling, therapy if appropriate
- **Nutrition**: Avoid relying on caffeine or alcohol to manage stress — both disrupt sleep and HRV

**Expected recovery**: Variable. HRV will normalize when the external stressor resolves or the athlete adapts to it.

## Phase 3: Implement recovery modalities

These are evidence-backed interventions that accelerate recovery. Use them strategically, not as daily rituals.

### **Heat stress (sauna)**
**Protocol**: 15–20 min at 170–180°F (77–82°C), post-training or before bed
**Mechanism**: Increases heat shock proteins, improves cardiovascular function, promotes deep sleep
**Frequency**: 3–4×/week during recovery phase
**Contraindications**: Dehydration, cardiovascular conditions (consult physician)

**Evidence**: Rhonda Patrick's research on sauna + longevity; Finnish sauna studies (reduced all-cause mortality)

### **Cold exposure (ice bath, cold shower)**
**Protocol**: 3–5 min at 50–59°F (10–15°C), ≥4h post-strength training (do not interfere with hypertrophy adaptation)
**Mechanism**: Reduces inflammation, activates brown fat, increases norepinephrine (alertness + focus)
**Frequency**: 2–3×/week during recovery phase
**Timing**: Morning preferred (stimulating); avoid before bed

**Evidence**: Huberman Lab cold exposure protocols; Søberg et al. on metabolic benefits

### **Contrast therapy (alternating hot/cold)**
**Protocol**: 3 rounds of (hot 3 min → cold 1 min), end on cold
**Mechanism**: Vasodilation/vasoconstriction pumps metabolic waste from tissues
**Frequency**: 1–2×/week during deload
**Best for**: Localized soreness, joint inflammation

### **Compression (boots, sleeves)**
**Protocol**: 20–30 min post-training, moderate pressure
**Mechanism**: Enhances venous return, reduces edema
**Frequency**: Daily during recovery phase if available
**Evidence**: Weak but positive; primarily subjective benefit (feels good → reduces stress → aids recovery)

### **Massage / soft tissue work**
**Protocol**: 30–60 min deep tissue or sports massage, or 10–15 min daily foam rolling + lacrosse ball
**Mechanism**: Reduces muscle tension, improves tissue quality, psychological relaxation
**Frequency**: 1×/week professional massage during recovery; daily self-myofascial release
**Evidence**: Moderate for soreness reduction; strong for perceived recovery

### **Nutrition timing**
**Post-workout (within 30–60 min):**
- 20–40g protein (whey, casein, or whole food)
- 0.5–1g/kg carbohydrates (if glycogen-depleting session)

**Throughout the day:**
- 1.6–2.2g/kg protein (spread across 4–5 meals)
- Omega-3s: 2–3g EPA+DHA daily (anti-inflammatory)
- Creatine: 5g daily (supports ATP regeneration, cognitive function)
- Magnesium: 400–500mg before bed (supports sleep, muscle relaxation)

### **Sleep optimization**
**Non-negotiable recovery nights:**
- Bedroom temp: 65–68°F (18–20°C)
- No screens 1h before bed (blue light suppresses melatonin)
- No alcohol (disrupts REM and deep sleep)
- No caffeine after 2pm (half-life 5–6 hours)
- Consider magnesium glycinate (400mg) or L-theanine (200mg) if sleep-onset difficulty

## Phase 4: Monitor response

Recovery is not linear. Track these metrics daily:

**Morning routine (5 min):**
1. Resting HR (immediately upon waking, before getting out of bed)
2. HRV (via Oura, WHOOP, Garmin, or HRV4Training app)
3. Subjective readiness (1–10 scale: energy, mood, motivation, soreness)

**Decision tree:**
- **HRV improving + subjective feel improving** → Protocol is working, continue
- **HRV flat but subjective feel improving** → Give it 2 more days
- **HRV improving but subjective feel poor** → Non-training stressor may be present, address it
- **Both declining** → Escalate (extend rest, consider medical evaluation)

**Return to training:**
Resume normal training only when:
- HRV is within 10% of baseline for 2 consecutive days
- Resting HR is back to normal
- Sleep is consistently ≥7h with good efficiency
- Subjective energy is ≥7/10

Do not rush. One extra recovery day is always better than returning too early and relapsing into deeper fatigue.

---

## Phase 5: Root cause analysis

Recovery protocols treat the symptom. Preventing recurrence requires understanding the cause.

**After recovery, ask:**
1. **Was training load appropriate?** (Volume, intensity, frequency)
   - If overreaching, reduce volume by 10–20% in next block
2. **Was periodisation structure sound?** (Hard weeks followed by deload weeks)
   - 3:1 ratio (3 weeks progression, 1 week deload) is standard
3. **Was sleep consistently sufficient?** (≥7h most nights)
   - If not, training volume must be reduced until sleep is fixed
4. **Were non-training stressors managed?** (Work, relationships, travel)
   - If chronic, training must be adjusted to fit total life stress load
5. **Was nutrition adequate?** (Protein, carbs, micronutrients)
   - Underfueling + high training load = fatigue spiral

Recovery protocols are powerful. But the best recovery protocol is not needing one — which comes from intelligent programming, honest self-assessment, and respecting the body's signals.
