# Deal Mechanics

## M&A Process

### Sellside (selling a company)
**Goal**: Maximise valuation, create competitive tension, maintain confidentiality.

1. **Preparation** (4-8 weeks) — CIM, financial model, data room; resolve legal issues; identify buyer universe (strategic vs financial)
2. **Marketing** (4-6 weeks) — teaser → NDA → CIM distribution
3. **Management presentations** (2-4 weeks) — shortlisted buyers (typically 3-6)
4. **LOI** (1-2 weeks) — IOIs → negotiate LOI with top 1-2 bidders; includes valuation, structure, exclusivity (30-60 days)
5. **Due diligence** (4-8 weeks) — financial, commercial, legal, operational DD; valuation adjustments if issues surface
6. **Definitive agreement** (4-6 weeks) — SPA negotiation; board/shareholder approval
7. **Close** (2-4 weeks) — regulatory approvals; wire funds

**Total timeline**: 5-9 months

### Buyside (acquiring a company)
Same phases in reverse — sourcing/screening → preliminary valuation → management meetings → LOI → DD → SPA → close. Key difference: buyer is validating strategic fit and stress-testing assumptions rather than managing a competitive process.

## Deal Structures

### Asset vs Stock Purchase

| Aspect | Asset Purchase | Stock Purchase |
|---|---|---|
| What's acquired | Specific assets + assumed liabilities | All equity (inherits ALL liabilities) |
| Tax treatment | Buyer gets step-up in basis | No step-up (less tax-efficient for buyer) |
| Contracts | Must be re-assigned | Transfer automatically |
| Seller preference | Dislikes (double tax for C-corps) | Prefers (cleaner, one tax level) |
| Buyer preference | Prefers (less liability, better tax) | Dislikes (inherits unknowns) |

Asset deals: distressed companies or significant liabilities. Stock deals: clean companies with known liabilities.

### Earnouts
Portion of purchase price contingent on future performance. Bridges valuation gaps — buyer thinks $50M, seller thinks $70M → agree $60M base + $10M earnout if targets hit.

**Risks for seller**: buyer controls the business post-close and can starve investment. Mitigations: seller retains board seat during earnout period; independent auditor verifies achievement.

### Locked-Box vs Completion Accounts
- **Locked-box**: price fixed on historical balance sheet date; no post-close adjustment. Seller preference.
- **Completion accounts**: price estimated at signing, adjusted post-close based on actual working capital/debt/cash (typically 60-90 days). Buyer preference. Disputes are common.

## Due Diligence Workstreams

**Financial (QofE — Quality of Earnings)**: verify EBITDA is accurate, recurring, sustainable. Watch for: revenue pulled forward, deferred CapEx, working capital manipulation, related-party transactions. Outcome: adjusted EBITDA (often 10-20% lower than reported).

**Commercial**: validate market position and growth assumptions via customer interviews and competitive analysis. Red flags: customer concentration (top 5 = 70% of revenue), high churn, commoditised product.

**Legal**: contracts, IP ownership, litigation, compliance. Red flags: change-of-control clauses in key customer contracts, licensed vs owned IP, ongoing material litigation.

**Operational/Tech**: tech stack scalability, key person risk, process documentation. Red flags: single-threaded systems, tech debt requiring $5M+ to modernise, recent VP turnover.

## Key Transaction Documents

### LOI (Letter of Intent)
Usually non-binding (except exclusivity + confidentiality). Covers: purchase price, structure, payment terms (cash/earnout/rollover), exclusivity period, conditions, break-up fee.

### SPA (Share Purchase Agreement)
The definitive legal agreement. Key sections:

1. **Purchase price & payment mechanism** — base price, adjustments, earnout terms, escrow
2. **Reps & warranties** — seller reps that financials are accurate, no undisclosed liabilities, contracts are valid; buyer reps that it has financing and authority
3. **Indemnification** — if reps are breached, seller indemnifies buyer; includes basket (minimum threshold), cap (maximum exposure, typically 10-20% of price), survival period (12-24 months)
4. **Conditions precedent** — regulatory approval, financing, no material adverse change
5. **Covenants** — what seller must do/not do between signing and close
6. **Closing mechanics** — wire transfer, share transfer, resignations

### Reps & Warranties Insurance (RWI)
Policy covering rep breaches. Reduces escrow requirements, speeds close. Cost: 3-6% of policy limit. Increasingly standard in deals >$50M.

## What Kills Deals

1. Valuation gap too wide post-DD
2. QofE reveals overstated EBITDA
3. Customer concentration — top customers won't commit to stay
4. Key person dependency — founder is irreplaceable but won't stay
5. Regulatory issues — antitrust approval required
6. Financing falls through
7. Material adverse change during process

**Prevention**: set realistic valuation expectations early; resolve legal/financial issues before going to market; diversify customer base; lock in key employees with retention bonuses.
