# Deal Mechanics

## M&A Process Overview

### Sellside Process (Selling a Company)

**Goal**: Maximize valuation, create competitive tension, maintain confidentiality.

#### Phase 1: Preparation (4-8 weeks)
- Assemble sell-side materials: CIM (Confidential Information Memorandum), financial model, data room
- Identify buyer universe (strategic vs financial buyers)
- Clean up: resolve outstanding legal issues, refresh cap table, get financials audit-ready

#### Phase 2: Marketing (4-6 weeks)
- Send teaser (1-page anonymous overview) to buyer list
- Interested buyers sign NDA
- Distribute CIM (detailed company overview, financials, growth story)

#### Phase 3: Management Presentations (2-4 weeks)
- Shortlisted buyers (typically 3-6) meet management
- Q&A, site visits, deeper diligence

#### Phase 4: LOI (Letter of Intent) (1-2 weeks)
- Request IOIs (Indications of Interest) from buyers
- Negotiate LOI with top 1-2 buyers
- **LOI includes**: valuation, structure, key terms, exclusivity period (30-60 days), break-up fee

#### Phase 5: Due Diligence (4-8 weeks)
- Buyer conducts full DD (financial, commercial, legal, operational)
- Seller responds to DD requests, resolves issues
- Valuation adjustments if DD uncovers problems

#### Phase 6: Definitive Agreement (4-6 weeks)
- Negotiate SPA (Share Purchase Agreement)
- Finalize reps & warranties, indemnities, escrows, earnouts
- Board approval, shareholder approval (if required)

#### Phase 7: Close (2-4 weeks)
- Regulatory approvals (if required)
- Final conditions precedent satisfied
- Wire funds, transfer ownership

**Total timeline**: 5-9 months (varies by deal complexity)

---

### Buyside Process (Acquiring a Company)

**Goal**: Find the right target, validate strategic fit, negotiate favorable terms, integrate successfully.

#### Phase 1: Sourcing & Screening (ongoing)
- Build target list (strategic fit, valuation range, availability)
- Reach out to targets (direct or via intermediaries)
- Screen for fit (size, geography, product/market overlap)

#### Phase 2: Preliminary Valuation & Offer (2-4 weeks)
- Receive CIM, analyze financials
- Build valuation model (DCF, comps)
- Submit IOI (non-binding offer)

#### Phase 3: Management Meetings & Deep Dive (2-4 weeks)
- Meet management, tour facilities
- Validate key assumptions (growth, margins, customer retention)
- Refine valuation

#### Phase 4: LOI Negotiation (1-2 weeks)
- Submit binding LOI (or non-binding with binding exclusivity)
- Negotiate key terms (valuation, structure, conditions, exclusivity)

#### Phase 5: Due Diligence (6-12 weeks)
- Financial DD (quality of earnings, working capital, debt/cash)
- Commercial DD (customer concentration, competitive position, market size)
- Legal DD (contracts, IP, litigation, compliance)
- Operational/Tech DD (systems, processes, org structure)
- **DD flags = valuation adjustment or walk away**

#### Phase 6: SPA Negotiation & Close (6-10 weeks)
- Negotiate final terms (see SPA section below)
- Board/shareholder approval
- Regulatory filings (if required: FTC, EU Commission, etc.)
- Close

**Total timeline**: 4-8 months

## Deal Structures

### Asset Purchase vs Stock Purchase

| Aspect | Asset Purchase | Stock Purchase |
|---|---|---|
| **What's acquired** | Specific assets + assumed liabilities | All equity (shares/stock) |
| **Liabilities** | Buyer picks which liabilities to assume | Buyer inherits ALL liabilities (known + unknown) |
| **Tax treatment** | Buyer gets step-up in basis (depreciate assets) | No step-up (less tax-efficient for buyer) |
| **Contracts** | Must be re-assigned (customer/vendor consent required) | Automatically transfer (no re-assignment) |
| **Seller preference** | Dislikes (double taxation for C-corps in US) | Prefers (cleaner, one level of tax) |
| **Buyer preference** | Prefers (less liability risk, better tax treatment) | Dislikes (inherits all liabilities) |

**When used**:
- **Asset deal**: Distressed companies, target has significant liabilities, buyer wants to cherry-pick assets
- **Stock deal**: Clean companies, all liabilities are known/acceptable, seller has leverage

### Earnouts

**What**: Portion of purchase price contingent on future performance (revenue, EBITDA, user growth, etc.)

**Why**: Bridges valuation gap (buyer thinks company is worth $50M, seller thinks $70M → agree on $60M with $10M earnout if targets hit)

**Structure**:
- Base consideration: $50M at close
- Earnout: $10M if Year 1 EBITDA exceeds $15M

**Earnout period**: Typically 1-3 years (longer = more risk for seller)

**Earnout metrics**: Revenue, EBITDA, user growth, customer retention (must be objectively measurable)

**Risks for seller**:
- Buyer controls the business post-close (can starve investment, hurt performance)
- Earnout targets may be set too high
- Disputes over earnout calculation

**Risk mitigation**:
- Seller retains board seat or operational control during earnout period
- Independent auditor verifies earnout achievement
- Caps on buyer's ability to change business model

**Earnout as synthetic financing**: Seller effectively lends buyer money (earnout = deferred payment). Buyer gets to "finance" part of deal with seller's risk capital.

### Management Rollover (Equity Rollover)

**What**: Seller (usually founders/management) reinvests a portion of proceeds into the buyer's equity.

**Why**:
- Buyer wants skin in the game (alignment of incentives)
- Seller shows confidence in combined entity
- Reduces cash required at close

**Typical rollover %**: 10-30% of seller's equity

**Example**:
- Company sells for $100M
- Founder owns 50% ($50M)
- Founder rolls over 20% ($10M) into buyer's equity
- Founder receives $40M cash + $10M equity in buyer

**Rollover considerations**:
- What's the rollover valued at? (Same price as sale, or discounted?)
- Is rollover equity common or preferred?
- What are the vesting/lockup terms?

### Locked-Box vs Completion Accounts

Two methods for determining final purchase price.

**Locked-Box**:
- Purchase price is fixed based on a historical balance sheet date (the "locked box date")
- Buyer assumes all economic benefit/risk from locked box date onward
- No post-close adjustment (price is final)
- **Seller preference** (certainty, no post-close disputes)

**Completion Accounts**:
- Purchase price is estimated at signing, then adjusted post-close based on actual working capital, debt, cash at close
- Post-close adjustment: Buyer/seller reconcile final numbers (typically 60-90 days post-close)
- Disputes are common (what's "normal" working capital?)
- **Buyer preference** (true-up to actual financials)

**Example** (Completion Accounts):
- Agreed EV: $100M
- Assumed working capital: $10M
- Assumed debt: $5M
- **Purchase Price at close** = $100M + actual working capital - actual debt

If actual working capital is $12M and actual debt is $6M:  
**Final price** = $100M + $12M - $6M = $106M (buyer pays $1M more than expected)

## Due Diligence Workstreams

### Financial DD (QofE: Quality of Earnings)

**Goal**: Verify that reported EBITDA is accurate, recurring, and sustainable.

**What's reviewed**:
- Revenue recognition (are sales real? correctly timed?)
- One-time items (non-recurring expenses that inflate EBITDA)
- Working capital (AR aging, AP terms — is working capital "normal"?)
- CapEx (are CapEx levels sustainable or artificially low?)
- Customer concentration (is 50% of revenue from 1 customer? risk!)

**Red flags**:
- Revenue pulled forward (booked sales early to hit targets)
- Expenses deferred (CapEx classified as OpEx to inflate EBITDA)
- Related-party transactions (sales to sister companies)
- Working capital manipulation (delayed AP, aggressive AR collection)

**Outcome**: Adjusted EBITDA (often 10-20% lower than reported)

---

### Commercial DD

**Goal**: Validate market position, growth assumptions, competitive moat.

**What's reviewed**:
- Customer interviews (why do they buy from target? would they stay post-acquisition?)
- Market sizing (is TAM real or inflated?)
- Competitive landscape (who are the real competitors? how differentiated is target?)
- Growth drivers (are they sustainable or one-time?)

**Red flags**:
- Customer concentration (top 5 customers = 70% of revenue)
- High churn (customers leave after 1 year)
- Commoditized product (no pricing power)
- Market share declining (losing to competitors)

---

### Legal DD

**Goal**: Identify legal liabilities, contract risks, IP ownership.

**What's reviewed**:
- Contracts (customer, vendor, partnership agreements — are they assignable?)
- Litigation (pending/threatened lawsuits)
- IP (patents, trademarks — are they owned or licensed? any disputes?)
- Compliance (regulatory, data privacy, employment law)

**Red flags**:
- Key contracts have change-of-control clauses (customer can terminate post-acquisition)
- IP is licensed, not owned (buyer doesn't get full control)
- Ongoing litigation with material exposure
- Regulatory violations (fines, consent decrees)

---

### Operational / Tech DD

**Goal**: Assess operational capabilities, tech stack quality, org health.

**What's reviewed**:
- Tech stack (is it scalable? modern? or legacy spaghetti code?)
- Key person risk (is the CTO the only one who understands the system?)
- Org structure (are there enough people to scale?)
- Processes (are they documented or in people's heads?)

**Red flags**:
- Single-threaded systems (one person is irreplaceable)
- Tech debt (would require $5M+ to modernize)
- Key employee turnover (3 VPs left in the last year)
- No process documentation (everything is ad-hoc)

## Key Transaction Documents

### LOI (Letter of Intent)

**Purpose**: Outline key terms before full legal drafting. Usually non-binding except for exclusivity and confidentiality.

**Key sections**:
- **Purchase price**: $X million
- **Structure**: Stock purchase vs asset purchase
- **Payment terms**: Cash at close, earnout, rollover equity
- **Exclusivity**: 60 days (seller can't talk to other buyers)
- **Conditions**: Subject to satisfactory DD, financing, regulatory approval
- **Break-up fee**: $Y if seller walks after exclusivity (protects buyer's DD costs)
- **Expense allocation**: Who pays for DD, legal fees?

**Binding vs non-binding**:
- **Non-binding**: Valuation, structure, most terms (either party can walk)
- **Binding**: Exclusivity, confidentiality, expense reimbursement (if specified)

---

### SPA (Share Purchase Agreement)

**Purpose**: Definitive legal agreement governing the transaction.

**Key sections**:

**1. Purchase Price & Payment Mechanism**
- Base price, adjustments (working capital, debt, cash), earnout terms, escrow

**2. Representations & Warranties (Reps)**
- Seller "reps" that financials are accurate, no undisclosed liabilities, all contracts are valid, no pending litigation, etc.
- Buyer "reps" that it has financing, authority to complete the deal

**3. Indemnification**
- If reps are breached, seller indemnifies buyer (pays for losses)
- **Basket**: Minimum threshold before indemnity kicks in (e.g., $500K — buyer absorbs small losses)
- **Cap**: Maximum indemnity exposure (e.g., 10-20% of purchase price)
- **Survival period**: How long reps survive post-close (12-24 months typical, except tax/IP = longer)

**4. Conditions Precedent**
- What must happen before close? (Regulatory approval, financing, no material adverse change)

**5. Covenants**
- What seller must do/not do between signing and closing (e.g., run business in ordinary course, no major contracts without buyer consent)

**6. Closing Mechanics**
- How/when funds are wired, shares are transferred, resignations happen

---

### Reps & Warranties Insurance (RWI)

**What**: Insurance policy that covers breaches of seller reps.

**Why**: 
- Reduces escrow requirements (buyer doesn't need to hold back 10% of price for 18 months)
- Cleaner exit for seller (less post-close liability)
- Speeds up deal close (less negotiation over indemnity terms)

**Cost**: 3-6% of policy limit (e.g., $300K premium for $10M policy)

**Coverage**: Breaches of reps (but usually excludes known issues, fraud, certain taxes)

**Who pays**: Typically buyer, but sometimes split or seller pays (negotiated)

**Usage trend**: Increasingly common in deals >$50M (standard in PE deals)

## Capital Markets (Equity Raises)

### Term Sheet Mechanics

**Key terms in a VC term sheet**:

**1. Valuation**
- **Pre-money valuation**: Company value before new money comes in
- **Post-money valuation**: Pre-money + new investment
- Example: $20M pre-money + $5M raise = $25M post-money → Investor gets 20%

**2. Liquidation Preference**
- **1× non-participating**: Investor gets $5M back before common holders get anything (then converts to common and participates pro-rata)
- **1× participating**: Investor gets $5M back AND participates pro-rata with common (double-dips)
- **Participating with cap**: Participating up to 2-3× investment, then converts

**3. Anti-Dilution Protection**
- **Full ratchet**: If down-round, investor's price adjusts to new lower price (punitive to founders)
- **Weighted average**: Adjustment is blended (more common, less punitive)
- **No anti-dilution**: Investor takes the loss (rare except for late-stage/IPO)

**4. Board Composition**
- How many seats does investor get? (Typically: 2 founder seats, 2 investor seats, 1 independent)

**5. Pro-Rata Rights**
- Investor has right (but not obligation) to invest in future rounds to maintain ownership %

**6. Drag-Along / Tag-Along**
- **Drag-along**: Majority can force minority to sell in an M&A
- **Tag-along**: If founders sell their shares, minority investors can "tag along" and sell too

### SAFE vs Priced Round

**SAFE** (Simple Agreement for Future Equity):
- **Not debt**, not equity — it's a convertible instrument
- Converts to equity in next priced round
- **Valuation cap**: SAFE converts at the lesser of (cap, next round price)
- **Discount**: SAFE holders get X% discount to next round price (e.g., 20%)
- **No interest, no maturity date** (unlike convertible notes)

**When used**: Seed stage, fast fundraising (no lengthy valuation negotiation)

**Priced Round**:
- Set a valuation today, issue preferred stock
- Dilution is known immediately
- More legal complexity (full term sheet, stock purchase agreement)

**When used**: Series A and beyond (investors want clarity on ownership)

## What Kills Deals

**Top reasons deals fall apart**:

1. **Valuation gap too wide** (buyer won't pay seller's price, even after DD)
2. **QofE reveals problems** (EBITDA is overstated by 30%)
3. **Customer concentration** (80% of revenue from 2 customers who won't commit to stay)
4. **Key person dependency** (founder is irreplaceable, won't stay post-close)
5. **Regulatory issues** (antitrust concerns, requires lengthy approval)
6. **Financing falls through** (buyer can't secure debt/equity to fund deal)
7. **Management team conflict** (cultural misalignment surfaces in DD)
8. **Material adverse change** (market crash, pandemic, key customer churns during process)

**How to prevent**:
- **Set realistic expectations early** (don't over-promise valuation)
- **Clean up before going to market** (resolve legal/financial issues)
- **Diversify customer base** (de-risk concentration before selling)
- **Lock in key employees** (retention bonuses, earnouts tied to their stay)
