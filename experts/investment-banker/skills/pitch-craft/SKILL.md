# Pitch Craft

The art and structure of investment banking presentations — from pitchbooks to management presentations, IC memos, and board narratives.

---

## Investment banking documents

### The pitchbook

A pitchbook is a sell-side or buy-side marketing document. The goal is to win a mandate or recommend a transaction. Every section should answer: why this? why now? why us?

**Sell-side M&A pitchbook structure**:
1. Executive Summary — situation, recommendation, key financials, transaction rationale (1 page)
2. Company overview — business model, competitive position, financial summary (2–3 pages)
3. Transaction rationale — why now? what's the strategic logic?
4. Buyer universe — strategic buyers, financial buyers, special situation buyers
5. Valuation — DCF, comps, precedent transactions, football field summary
6. Process outline — timeline, buyer contact strategy, data room plan
7. Bank credentials — relevant transactions, team bios

**Common mistakes**:
- Too much information. A pitchbook should have a clear point of view, not every possible fact.
- Generic market sections. Cut anything that doesn't directly support the recommendation.
- Weak executive summary. If the first page doesn't compel reading, the document has failed.

### The IC memo (Investment Committee)

Internal document recommending a transaction to senior decision-makers.

**IC memo structure**:
1. Transaction overview — deal description, consideration, structure
2. Strategic rationale — why this target/seller? what's the value creation thesis?
3. Business quality assessment — moat, competitive dynamics, management, risks
4. Financial analysis — LBO or acquisition model summary, entry and exit assumptions, returns bridge
5. Risk factors and mitigants
6. Recommendation

IC memos are dense and factual. Unlike pitchbooks, they don't need to sell — they need to be thorough. Risks must be surfaced, not buried.

### The management presentation (company side)

The document a company uses to present itself to buyers, investors, or lenders.

**Structure**:
1. Investment highlights (the thesis in 6–8 bullets)
2. Company overview (what we do, how we make money)
3. Market opportunity (TAM/SAM, growth drivers)
4. Competitive positioning (moat, differentiation)
5. Products / services
6. Customers (logos, concentration, retention)
7. Financial overview (P&L summary, unit economics, growth trajectory)
8. Management team
9. Transaction details (if applicable)

## Slide construction

### The assertion-evidence structure

Every slide should have:
- **Title**: The assertion — what the audience should conclude after reading this slide. Not the topic. "Revenue growth is accelerating" not "Revenue Overview."
- **Body**: The evidence that supports the assertion.
- **One clear takeaway**: If the audience reads nothing else, what's the one thing they should get?

### Common slide types

**Table of contents / roadmap slide**: Shows structure, sets expectations. Don't skip it.

**Tombstone slide**: Completed transaction credentials. Always formal: Company / Transaction Type / Transaction Value / Date.

**Football field chart** (valuation summary): Horizontal bar chart showing valuation range from each methodology. DCF range, public comps range, precedent transactions range, 52-week trading range (if public). Each bar shows low–high; order methods by ascending mid-point.

**Bridge chart** (waterfall)**: Shows how one financial figure builds to another (revenue bridge, EBITDA bridge, enterprise value to equity value bridge). Essential for explaining value movement clearly.

**League table**: Rankings of banks by transaction volume or count. Know how to manipulate league table criteria to show your bank in the best light (standard practice, everyone does it).

## Narrative architecture

The most important skill in pitchcraft: building a logical flow that leads the audience to an inevitable conclusion.

**SCR structure** (Situation, Complication, Resolution): The classic McKinsey narrative structure. Start with the current state, introduce the tension/problem, present the solution.

**Pyramid principle**: State the conclusion first, then support it with evidence. This is the inverse of how most people think, but it's how sophisticated readers prefer to consume information — they want the answer, then the reasoning.

**The "So what?" test**: After every slide, ask: "So what?" If you can't immediately articulate why this slide matters to the investment decision, cut it or reframe it.

## Formatting standards

Investment banking has strict aesthetic conventions. Violating them signals inexperience.

- **Alignment**: everything must be pixel-perfect. Headers, body text, and charts aligned to invisible grid.
- **Font**: typically Times New Roman or Calibri; consistent throughout; no decorative fonts
- **Colors**: limited palette (dark blue, grey, red/green for positive/negative); no gratuitous color
- **No clipart, stock photography, or emoji** in formal banking documents
- **Slide numbers** always visible
- **Headers and footers**: client name, date, confidentiality legend on every page
- **Tables**: always formatted consistently; alternating row shading; clear labels
