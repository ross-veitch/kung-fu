# /weekly-plan — Structure the week's priorities and schedule

**Purpose**: Design the upcoming week's calendar and priorities to maximize the principal's effectiveness while protecting their time and energy.

**When to use**: Every Friday afternoon (planning next week) or Monday morning (adjusting the plan based on weekend developments).

---

## Phase 1: Gather inputs

Before designing the week, collect the necessary context:

### **1. Strategic priorities (from principal)**
What are the 2–3 most important outcomes for this week? Not tasks — outcomes.

Examples:
- "Finalize Q2 budget and get board approval"
- "Close the partnership deal with Company X"
- "Ship the product feature we've been building"

If there are more than 3, force prioritization. Everything else is secondary.

### **2. Fixed commitments**
What is already on the calendar that cannot move?
- Board meetings, investor calls, all-hands
- External commitments (conferences, client meetings)
- Personal commitments (family events, medical appointments)

### **3. Inbound requests**
What meetings, decisions, or tasks have been requested this week?
- New meeting requests received in the last 7 days
- Pending decisions that need the principal's input
- Follow-ups from last week's action items

### **4. Energy and capacity**
- How much travel is happening this week? (Travel days = reduced capacity)
- Are there any high-stress or high-stakes events? (Budget energy accordingly)
- What is the principal's current state? (Burned out → protect recovery time; energized → can take on more)

---

## Phase 2: Allocate time by category

A well-designed week balances four types of time:

### **1. Deep work (40–50% of available time)**
Uninterrupted blocks (≥2 hours) for strategic thinking, writing, decision-making, or complex problem-solving.

**Scheduling rules:**
- Morning blocks preferred (cognitive performance peaks before midday)
- No meetings before or immediately after (context-switching kills depth)
- Protect these blocks as aggressively as you would protect a board meeting

**What qualifies as deep work:**
- Writing strategy docs, board memos, or important communications
- Financial modeling, budget reviews, or complex analysis
- Product or technical design work
- Long-term strategic thinking

**What does not:**
- Responding to email (reactive)
- Quick check-ins (interruptible)
- Routine meetings (collaborative, not deep)

### **2. Collaboration time (30–40%)**
Scheduled meetings, 1:1s, working sessions, and team interactions.

**Scheduling rules:**
- Cluster meetings into blocks (morning or afternoon, not scattered)
- Leave 5–15 min buffer between meetings
- Stack related meetings together (all product meetings in one block, all people meetings in another)

**Types of meetings:**
- **1:1s with direct reports** (weekly, non-negotiable)
- **Decision meetings** (time-bound, specific outcome)
- **Working sessions** (collaborative creation)
- **External stakeholder meetings** (customers, partners, investors)

### **3. Communication time (15–20%)**
Email, Slack, quick calls, and ad-hoc coordination.

**Scheduling rules:**
- Batch into 2–3 scheduled windows per day (morning, midday, late afternoon)
- **Do not allow continuous communication throughout the day** (destroys focus)
- EA handles 80% of inbound; principal handles the remaining 20% that requires their voice

**What gets scheduled:**
- **9:00–9:30am**: Morning email triage
- **12:00–12:30pm**: Midday Slack catch-up and quick replies
- **4:30–5:00pm**: End-of-day inbox clearance and next-day preview

### **4. Recovery and margin (10–15%)**
Whitespace for thinking, walking, transitions, and the unexpected.

**Scheduling rules:**
- One full meeting-free day per week (or at minimum, one meeting-free half-day)
- Lunch is not a meeting slot (default: 30–60 min blocked for actual lunch)
- No back-to-back meetings for more than 3 hours straight

**What this time is for:**
- Decompressing after high-intensity meetings
- Thinking through decisions that surfaced during the week
- Catching up on reading, research, or learning
- Handling the inevitable urgent issue that arises mid-week

---

## Phase 3: Map the week

Using the inputs and time allocation principles, build the week day-by-day:

### **Monday: Launch and align**
- **Morning**: Deep work block (2h) — principal tackles highest-priority strategic task
- **Midday**: Team check-in or all-hands (if weekly cadence)
- **Afternoon**: 1:1s with key direct reports or decision meetings

**Goal**: Set the direction for the week, align the team, make any decisions that would block others.

### **Tuesday & Wednesday: Execution days**
- **Morning**: Deep work blocks (protected)
- **Afternoon**: Collaboration time (meetings, working sessions)
- **Late afternoon**: Communication window

**Goal**: Maximum productivity — execute on the week's priorities with minimal distraction.

### **Thursday: External and strategic**
- **Morning**: Deep work or external stakeholder meetings (investors, partners, customers)
- **Afternoon**: Strategic planning, review sessions, or high-stakes meetings
- **Late afternoon**: Wrap any open threads from earlier in the week

**Goal**: Engage with external stakeholders while there is still time in the week to act on what emerges.

### **Friday: Close and plan**
- **Morning**: Finish any incomplete priority work from the week
- **Midday**: Weekly review session (30 min with EA: what got done, what didn't, why)
- **Afternoon**: Plan next week (Friday 4–5pm or delegate to Monday morning)

**Goal**: Close the week cleanly, ensure nothing critical is dropped, and set up next week for success.

---

## Phase 4: Protect the plan

Once the week is designed, the EA's job is to **defend it against erosion**.

### **New meeting requests mid-week**
Default response: **"Can this wait until next week?"**

If yes → schedule for next week.

If no → ask: "What is the urgency?" and "What decision needs to be made?"

Then apply the priority test:
- Does this request align with one of the week's top 3 priorities? → **Consider fitting it in**
- Is this urgent but not important? → **Delegate to someone else**
- Is this neither urgent nor important? → **Decline or defer to next week**

### **Time thieves to watch for**
- **"Quick sync" requests** that turn into 30-minute discussions → Redirect to async (Loom, memo, Slack thread)
- **FYI meetings** where the principal is invited "just to be informed" → Decline and request notes instead
- **Recurring meetings that no longer serve their purpose** → Cancel or reduce frequency
- **Back-to-back meetings without breaks** → Shorten one meeting by 5–10 min to create breathing room

### **Energy management**
If a high-stress meeting or travel happens mid-week, adjust the rest of the week:
- Reduce meeting load immediately after (don't stack a 3-hour board meeting with back-to-back 1:1s afterward)
- Add recovery time (walk, offline thinking time, early end-of-day)
- Push non-urgent work to the following week

---

## Phase 5: End-of-week review (15 min)

Every Friday, review the week with the principal:

### **What got done?**
- Which of the top 3 priorities were completed?
- What were the key decisions made?
- What shipped, closed, or moved forward significantly?

### **What didn't get done, and why?**
- Was it deprioritized intentionally? (Good — priorities shifted)
- Was it blocked by something? (Bad — needs to be unblocked for next week)
- Was it ignored due to poor time management? (Fix the root cause)

### **What should change for next week?**
- Too many meetings? → Cancel or shorten recurring ones
- Not enough deep work? → Protect morning blocks more aggressively
- Energy crash mid-week? → Review travel/stress load and adjust pacing

### **Action items**
- What are next week's top 3 priorities?
- What prep work needs to happen over the weekend or Monday morning?
- Any upcoming high-stakes events that need extra preparation?

---

## Output format

Deliver the weekly plan as:

### **1. Calendar view** (visual)
The principal should be able to glance at the week and see:
- Deep work blocks (color-coded, e.g., blue)
- Meetings (grouped into clusters)
- Communication windows (e.g., green)
- Recovery/whitespace (blank or light gray)

### **2. Written summary** (1 page max)
```
Week of [Date]

Top 3 Priorities:
1. [Priority 1]
2. [Priority 2]
3. [Priority 3]

Key meetings:
- Monday 2pm: Board strategy session (2h) — pre-read sent Friday
- Wednesday 10am: Product roadmap review (1h)
- Thursday 3pm: Investor call with [Name]

Deep work blocks:
- Tuesday 9–11am: Draft Q2 strategy memo
- Wednesday 2–4pm: Financial model review
- Thursday 9–11am: Product spec finalization

Notes:
- No meetings before 9am this week (principal requested)
- Friday afternoon kept clear for weekly review + next-week planning
- Monday is heavy on alignment meetings; Tuesday-Wednesday are execution days

Potential conflicts:
- [Name] requested a meeting Thursday afternoon — currently holding for confirmation
```

### **3. Adjust as the week unfolds**
The plan is not rigid. The EA monitors throughout the week and makes real-time adjustments:
- Reschedule if something urgent arises
- Protect deep work blocks from encroachment
- Surface to the principal if the plan is breaking down ("You've had 6 hours of back-to-back meetings today — should we move tomorrow's session to next week?")

---

## Success metric

A well-planned week is one where, at Friday's review, the principal can say:

**"I made progress on what mattered most, I had enough time to think, and I didn't feel like the calendar controlled me."**

That is the standard. Everything else is iteration toward it.
