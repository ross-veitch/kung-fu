# Stakeholder Mapping

Every executive operates within a web of relationships — internal and external, formal and informal, transactional and strategic. Stakeholder mapping is the practice of making that web explicit, prioritizing attention intelligently, and ensuring that high-value relationships receive the time and care they deserve.

An EA who understands the stakeholder map is no longer just a scheduler — they become a relationship manager, a strategic gatekeeper, and a force multiplier for the principal's network.

## The stakeholder map: Three dimensions

Map every stakeholder along three axes:

### 1. **Power** (ability to affect the principal's goals)
- **High**: Board members, investors, key customers, regulators, direct reports with critical functions
- **Medium**: Vendors, partners, peer executives, influential advisors
- **Low**: Acquaintances, passive stakeholders, people outside decision-making loops

### 2. **Interest** (how engaged they are with the principal's work)
- **High**: Actively involved, frequent communication, strong opinions
- **Medium**: Occasionally engaged, reactive rather than proactive
- **Low**: Aware but passive, minimal involvement

### 3. **Relationship quality** (strength of trust and rapport)
- **Strong**: High trust, candid communication, history of collaboration
- **Developing**: Relationship exists but needs investment
- **Weak or strained**: Limited rapport, potential tension, or new contact

## The stakeholder matrix

Combine Power and Interest into a 2×2 matrix to determine engagement strategy:

| | **High Interest** | **Low Interest** |
|---|---|---|
| **High Power** | **Manage Closely** — Board, investors, key customers. Proactive communication, regular check-ins, never surprised. | **Keep Satisfied** — Regulators, passive board members. Inform regularly, respond quickly, but do not over-communicate. |
| **Low Power** | **Keep Informed** — Engaged team members, enthusiastic partners. Loop them in, appreciate their input, but do not let them consume disproportionate time. | **Monitor** — Peripheral contacts. Passive communication (newsletters, general updates), respond when engaged but do not proactively reach out. |

The mistake most executives make: treating everyone as "Manage Closely" and burning out. The art is allocating attention proportionally.

## Building the map

For each stakeholder, document:

1. **Name and role**
2. **Organization / context**
3. **Classification** (Power × Interest quadrant)
4. **Relationship quality** (Strong / Developing / Weak)
5. **Communication preferences** (email, Slack, phone, face-to-face; formality level; response time expectation)
6. **Last meaningful interaction** (date, topic)
7. **Next touch point** (scheduled or target date)
8. **Key topics / sensitivities** (what they care about, what to avoid)
9. **Gatekeeper / assistant** (if they have one — name, contact, relationship with your principal)

This can live in:
- **Notion database** (filterable, linked to calendar and tasks)
- **Airtable** (if you need richer views and automation)
- **CRM** (if the organization uses Salesforce, HubSpot, or similar)
- **Spreadsheet** (minimum viable version — functional but harder to maintain)

Update the map after every significant interaction. Stale maps are worse than no map — they create false confidence.

## Engagement cadence by quadrant

### Manage Closely (High Power × High Interest)
- **Frequency**: Weekly to monthly, depending on relationship
- **Mode**: Mix of scheduled check-ins (calendar blocks) and opportunistic touchpoints (quick messages, informal coffee)
- **Preparation**: EA prepares brief before every interaction — recent context, open threads, topics to cover
- **Follow-up**: Principal or EA sends recap within 24 hours if commitments were made

### Keep Satisfied (High Power × Low Interest)
- **Frequency**: Quarterly to semi-annually, unless something specific arises
- **Mode**: Formal updates, board materials, written summaries
- **Preparation**: EA ensures materials are polished and delivered on time
- **Follow-up**: Only if they engage — do not chase

### Keep Informed (Low Power × High Interest)
- **Frequency**: As topics arise, or via batch updates (monthly newsletter, team all-hands)
- **Mode**: Group communication preferred over 1:1 (scales better)
- **Preparation**: Minimal — these stakeholders are informed, not consulted
- **Follow-up**: Respond if they reach out, but do not proactively follow up

### Monitor (Low Power × Low Interest)
- **Frequency**: Passive only (they receive general updates if subscribed)
- **Mode**: No active outreach
- **Preparation**: None
- **Follow-up**: Only if they initiate

## The gatekeeper's role: Prioritizing access

Not everyone who wants the principal's time should get it. The EA's job is to protect the principal's time while maintaining relationships. This requires:

### Criteria for granting access
- **High Power stakeholders**: Almost always yes (schedule within the week if urgent, within the month otherwise)
- **Developing relationships with strategic value**: Yes, but schedule farther out (4–6 weeks)
- **Low strategic value requests**: Politely decline or delegate to someone else on the team

### Declining gracefully
Templates for common scenarios:

**No strategic fit:**
> "Thanks for reaching out. [Principal] is focused on [specific priority] right now and won't have bandwidth for this. I'd recommend connecting with [alternative person] who might be a better fit."

**Timing doesn't work:**
> "Unfortunately [Principal]'s calendar is fully committed through [date]. If this is time-sensitive, I can connect you with [delegate]. Otherwise, feel free to check back in [timeframe]."

**Redirect to delegate:**
> "[Principal] asked me to connect you with [Name], who leads [function] and will be the right person for this conversation."

The goal is not to say "no" — it is to redirect, defer, or delegate in a way that preserves the relationship.

## Managing upward and across

The stakeholder map is not just external — it includes internal relationships.

### Direct reports
- **Classification**: High Power (they control execution) × High Interest (deeply engaged)
- **Engagement**: Weekly 1:1s (non-negotiable), plus ad-hoc as needed
- **EA role**: Ensure 1:1s happen on schedule, prepare standing agenda, track follow-up items

### Peers (other executives, function heads)
- **Classification**: Medium Power (influence but not authority) × High Interest (operationally interdependent)
- **Engagement**: Monthly check-ins or as projects require
- **EA role**: Facilitate cross-functional coordination, ensure alignment on shared priorities

### Upward (CEO, board chair, investors)
- **Classification**: High Power × High Interest
- **Engagement**: Depends on org structure — weekly for direct manager, monthly+ for board/investors
- **EA role**: Obsessive preparation (materials ready 24h early), no surprises, proactive status updates

## Red flags: When the map is failing

**Symptom 1**: The principal is surprised by a stakeholder's reaction
- **Diagnosis**: Relationship quality was misclassified, or engagement cadence was too infrequent
- **Fix**: Increase touchpoint frequency, add a "temperature check" interaction

**Symptom 2**: High Power stakeholders are waiting days for responses
- **Diagnosis**: Inbox triage is not respecting the map — VIP filter is not working
- **Fix**: Audit email filters, ensure High Power stakeholders are flagged automatically

**Symptom 3**: Principal is over-committed and burned out
- **Diagnosis**: Too many relationships in "Manage Closely," or EA is not declining Low Power requests
- **Fix**: Re-classify stakeholders honestly (some may have moved down in Power/Interest), batch low-priority meetings

**Symptom 4**: Key relationships are decaying
- **Diagnosis**: No system for tracking "last contact" and "next touch point"
- **Fix**: Set calendar reminders for quarterly check-ins with High Power stakeholders

## Stakeholder mapping is continuous

Stakeholder maps are not static. Relationships evolve. A Low Power contact becomes High Power when they change roles. A High Interest stakeholder becomes Low Interest when a project ends. An external advisor becomes a board member.

**Review cadence**: Quarterly audit of the full map. Ask:
- Who has moved up or down in Power?
- Whose Interest level has shifted?
- Which relationships need repair or investment?
- Who is new and needs to be added?

Stakeholder mapping is not busywork — it is the operating system for the principal's most valuable asset: their network. When done well, the EA becomes the principal's Chief Relationship Officer.
