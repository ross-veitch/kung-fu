# Time Management for Executives

## The fundamental resource

Executives have two scarce resources: **time** and **attention**. They are not the same thing. You can be in a meeting (spending time) while mentally elsewhere (spending attention on something else). Great time management protects both.

The goal is not to do more things. It is to do the right things, fully present.

## The leverage hierarchy

Every task falls into one of four categories. Always know which you're dealing with:

| Category | Definition | Right response |
|----------|-----------|----------------|
| **Only I can do this** | Requires the principal's specific relationships, judgment, or authority | Do it, protect time for it |
| **Someone else should do this** | Could be done by a report, vendor, or tool — possibly better | Delegate or eliminate |
| **Should be done differently** | Right goal, wrong approach (too many meetings, wrong format) | Redesign |
| **Shouldn't be done** | No one should do this | Eliminate |

Most calendars are full of category 2–4 items masquerading as category 1. Audit ruthlessly.

## The ideal week framework

Design the week before it happens. A well-structured executive week has:

**Deep work blocks** (2–4 hour minimum): Strategic thinking, writing, complex problem-solving. These require no interruptions. Book them as recurring appointments before anything else. Typically morning, when cognitive energy is highest.

**Communication windows**: Batch email, Slack, and calls into 2–3 defined windows per day. Checking constantly destroys deep work without meaningful productivity gain.

**Meeting clusters**: If meetings must happen, cluster them. Three 1-hour meetings spread through the day destroy 6 hours. The same three meetings back-to-back cost 3.

**Buffer/processing time**: 30 minutes after each major meeting for notes, decisions, delegations. Without this, things fall through the gaps.

**Recovery**: Lunch without a screen. A 20-minute walk. These are not optional — they are cognitive maintenance.

## Meeting hygiene

Every meeting should have a clear answer to: *what decision are we making, or what is this for?*

**Before accepting**: Can this be an async message? A 10-minute call instead of an hour? If the meeting has no clear outcome, decline and propose an alternative.

**Meeting defaults to avoid**: 1-hour default for things that need 20 minutes. No agenda. Recurring meetings that have outlived their purpose. Back-to-back meetings with no buffer.

**Hard rules for the principal's calendar**:
- No meeting before [~~first-meeting-time] unless exceptional
- No meetings in deep work blocks unless the principal explicitly agrees
- Last meeting of day no later than [~~last-meeting-time]
- Never schedule travel days as working days

## Email and communication triage

The OHIO principle: **Only Handle It Once**. When you open an email, do one of: reply now, delete, delegate, defer to a specific time. Don't park it as "read but unresolved".

Priority tiers:
- **Urgent + important**: handle within hours. These are rare.
- **Important, not urgent**: scheduled response within 24 hours
- **Urgent, not important**: delegate or decline politely
- **Neither**: archive without guilt

## The weekly review

15 minutes, same time every week:
1. **Capture**: anything outstanding from the week that isn't in a trusted system
2. **Clarify**: what does "next action" mean for each open item?
3. **Organise**: check all projects, check calendar for next week
4. **Reflect**: what went well? What would I change?
5. **Engage**: confirm priorities for next week
