# Meeting Design

Most meetings are wastes of time. They run long, meander without focus, include the wrong people, and end without decisions or clear next steps. A well-designed meeting is rare — which means it is also a competitive advantage.

Meeting design is the practice of treating every meeting as a deliberate, engineered artifact. Before the calendar invite goes out, the EA should be able to answer: **What decision or outcome will this meeting produce?** If the answer is unclear, the meeting should not happen.

## The meeting decision tree: Does this need to be a meeting?

Before scheduling anything, ask:

### Could this be an email?
**Yes, if:**
- It is purely informational (status update, announcement, FYI)
- No discussion or debate is needed
- Attendees are in multiple time zones and async is more efficient

**No, if:**
- Requires real-time discussion or negotiation
- Involves complex, nuanced decision-making
- Needs immediate feedback or brainstorming

### Could this be async (Slack thread, Loom video, memo)?
**Yes, if:**
- It is one-way communication (update, briefing, explainer)
- Attendees can absorb and respond on their own schedule
- No live collaboration is required

**No, if:**
- Requires back-and-forth dialogue
- Benefits from live whiteboarding or co-creation
- Needs to build rapport or alignment

### Could this be a shorter meeting?
**Most meetings default to 30 or 60 minutes. Challenge that default.**
- **15 min**: Daily standup, quick sync, single-decision meeting
- **25 min**: Standard working meeting (leaves 5 min buffer before the next one)
- **50 min**: Deep working session, workshop, strategic planning

Parkinson's Law applies to meetings: work expands to fill the time available. A 25-minute meeting with a tight agenda is more productive than a 60-minute meeting with slack.

## The five elements of a well-designed meeting

Every meeting invitation should include:

### 1. **Clear objective (one sentence)**
Not "discuss project X" — that is a topic, not an objective. The objective is the **outcome**:
- "Decide whether to proceed with vendor A or vendor B"
- "Align on Q2 priorities and assign owners"
- "Review draft strategy doc and finalize by end of session"

If you cannot write a one-sentence objective, you are not ready to schedule the meeting.

### 2. **Decision-maker identified**
Who has the authority to make the decision this meeting is driving toward? If the answer is "no one" or "unclear," the meeting will not produce a decision.

**Decision-making frameworks:**
- **Unilateral**: One person decides, meeting is for input
- **Consultative**: Decision-maker consults attendees, then decides
- **Consensus**: Group decides together (slowest, use sparingly)
- **Delegation**: Decision-maker is not attending, group decides on their behalf (requires clear authority)

Name the decision-maker in the agenda. Everyone else knows their role is to inform, not to decide.

### 3. **Tight attendee list**
**Invite the minimum set of people required to make the decision.** Every additional person:
- Increases coordination cost
- Slows the conversation
- Reduces accountability (diffusion of responsibility)

**Required attendees**: Cannot make the decision without them (decision-maker, key input providers, implementation owners)

**Optional attendees**: Useful to have but not critical (can read the notes afterward)

**Explicitly exclude**: People who would attend "just in case" or "to stay informed" — send them the notes instead

Amazon's two-pizza rule: if you cannot feed the meeting attendees with two pizzas, the meeting is too big.

### 4. **Structured agenda with time blocks**
A meeting without an agenda is a conversation, not a meeting. The agenda should specify:
- **Topic** (what we are discussing)
- **Time allocation** (how long)
- **Owner** (who is leading this section)
- **Desired outcome** (decision, alignment, next steps)

Example agenda for a 25-minute meeting:

```
Meeting: Finalize Q1 Marketing Budget
Objective: Approve final allocation across channels
Decision-maker: CMO
Duration: 25 minutes

Agenda:
1. [5 min] Review updated budget model (Finance)
   → Outcome: Confirm numbers are accurate
2. [10 min] Debate allocation: paid vs organic (Marketing)
   → Outcome: Decide split
3. [5 min] Confirm resourcing and timeline (Ops)
   → Outcome: Assign owners
4. [5 min] Next steps and close (EA)
   → Outcome: Documented decisions, action items assigned
```

Time blocks create urgency and prevent any one topic from consuming the entire meeting.

### 5. **Pre-read materials (sent 24h in advance)**
Do not use meeting time to present information that could have been read in advance. If there is a document, deck, or data set that informs the decision, send it 24 hours before the meeting with clear instructions:

> "Please review the attached budget model before tomorrow's meeting. We will not re-present the numbers — the meeting will focus on the allocation decision."

If attendees show up without having read the pre-read, the meeting should be rescheduled. Otherwise, you train people that pre-reads are optional.

## Meeting formats and when to use each

Different meeting types require different structures:

### **Decision meeting** (most common)
- **Purpose**: Make a specific decision
- **Duration**: 25–50 min
- **Structure**: Present options → Discuss trade-offs → Decide → Document
- **Output**: Clear decision, recorded in notes, communicated to relevant stakeholders

### **Working session** (collaborative)
- **Purpose**: Create something together (brainstorm, draft, design)
- **Duration**: 50–90 min
- **Structure**: Frame the problem → Generate ideas → Synthesize → Assign next steps
- **Output**: Draft artifact (doc, design, plan), with clear owner for next iteration

### **Status/sync meeting** (informational)
- **Purpose**: Share updates, surface blockers
- **Duration**: 15–25 min
- **Structure**: Round-robin updates (2 min each), surface blockers, assign actions
- **Output**: Everyone knows what everyone else is working on; blockers are escalated or resolved
- **Anti-pattern**: Long narratives, deep dives (those should happen offline)

### **1:1 meeting** (relationship + coaching)
- **Purpose**: Build rapport, provide feedback, unblock individual
- **Duration**: 25–50 min
- **Structure**: Standing agenda (wins, blockers, feedback, development), but flexible enough for what is actually on the person's mind
- **Output**: Stronger relationship, clearer priorities, resolved blockers

### **All-hands / town hall** (broadcast)
- **Purpose**: Align the organization, share strategy, build culture
- **Duration**: 30–60 min
- **Structure**: Prepared remarks → Q&A (submitted in advance or live)
- **Output**: Organizational alignment, transparency, morale signal
- **Anti-pattern**: Too frequent (dilutes impact), no Q&A (feels like propaganda)

## The EA's role in meeting execution

A world-class EA does not just schedule meetings — they design, prepare, and run them.

### Before the meeting
- **Send calendar invite** with objective, agenda, pre-read (24h advance)
- **Confirm attendance** (especially for the decision-maker and required attendees)
- **Prepare materials**: print agenda, load slides, set up Zoom room, reserve physical space
- **Prepare the principal**: 5-minute brief on context, attendees, sensitivities

### During the meeting
- **Timekeeper**: Keep the meeting on track (gently redirect when discussions drift)
- **Notetaker**: Capture decisions, action items, and next steps in real-time (share notes within 2h of meeting end)
- **Facilitator** (if the principal is not leading): Ensure everyone gets heard, prevent one person from dominating

### After the meeting
- **Circulate notes within 2 hours** (decisions, action items with owners and deadlines, parking lot items)
- **Update task tracker** (Asana, Linear, Notion) with action items
- **Follow up on commitments** (if someone said "I'll send that by Friday," check in Thursday)

If a meeting decision affects other stakeholders not in the room, the EA ensures they are looped in immediately. No one should be surprised by a decision made in a meeting they were not in.

## Meeting hygiene: Preventing decay

Meetings proliferate. What starts as a one-time decision meeting becomes a weekly standing meeting that no one dares cancel. EA's job is to audit regularly:

### Quarterly meeting audit
For every recurring meeting:
1. **Does it still serve its original purpose?**
2. **Are the right people attending?**
3. **Is the frequency still appropriate?**
4. **Could it be shorter, less frequent, or async?**

If the answer to #1 is "no" or "unclear," cancel the meeting. If attendees do not show up prepared, make the meeting less frequent (forcing people to engage). If it is a status meeting where nothing ever changes, kill it and replace with a Slack thread.

### Red flags that a meeting is broken
- Attendees are on their laptops doing other work
- Meetings consistently run over time
- No decisions are made; every topic is "tabled for next time"
- The same people dominate; others are silent
- Action items from last meeting were not completed

Each of these has a fix:
- **Distraction**: Smaller group, tighter agenda, or no-laptop rule
- **Running over**: Shorter duration, stricter timekeeping
- **No decisions**: Re-clarify decision-maker, pre-circulate options before meeting
- **Unbalanced participation**: Facilitator explicitly invites quieter voices
- **Incomplete action items**: Follow-up system is broken (EA owns this)

## The calendar as strategic architecture

Meetings do not exist in isolation — they compete for time with deep work, email, personal obligations, and recovery. The EA's job is to design the principal's week so meetings enable productivity rather than destroying it.

**Design principles:**
- **Cluster meetings** (leave full mornings or afternoons for deep work)
- **Buffer between meetings** (minimum 5 min, ideally 15 min)
- **No meetings before 9am or after 5pm** unless unavoidable (protect energy)
- **One meeting-free day per week** (for strategic thinking, writing, long-term work)
- **Prep time before high-stakes meetings** (15–30 min blocked on the calendar)

A well-designed calendar has whitespace. Whitespace is not wasted time — it is the time where the most important work happens.
