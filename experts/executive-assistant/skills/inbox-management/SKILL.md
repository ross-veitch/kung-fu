# Inbox Management

The inbox is not a to-do list. It is an unsorted stream of requests, information, and noise. World-class inbox management is the art of turning that stream into a small set of actionable items that deserve the principal's attention — and handling everything else before it reaches them.

## The fundamental principle: OHIO (Only Handle It Once)

Every message should be processed exactly once. Read it. Decide. Act. Move on. The moment you mark something as "I'll deal with this later," you have created cognitive overhead. The goal is to train the habit of **decide now, not later.**

Decision options for every message:
1. **Delete** — It does not matter. No response needed. Archive or delete immediately.
2. **Delegate** — Someone else should handle this. Forward with clear instructions.
3. **Defer** — It requires action, but not now. Schedule it (task, calendar block, snooze to a specific date).
4. **Do** — It takes <2 minutes and only you can do it. Do it now.
5. **Draft** — The principal must respond, but you can draft it. Write the reply, flag for review.

The mistake most people make: treating "defer" as the default. It should be the exception.

## The triage framework: Prioritization matrix

Not all emails are created equal. Use a two-axis framework:

**Axis 1: Urgency**
- **High**: Response expected within 4 hours (investor, board member, key customer, internal escalation)
- **Medium**: Response expected within 24 hours (routine business contact, internal coordination)
- **Low**: Response expected within 48–72 hours, or no response needed

**Axis 2: Importance**
- **High**: Directly impacts revenue, key relationship, strategic decision, or principal's personal priority
- **Medium**: Routine operational matter, requires decision but not strategic
- **Low**: Informational, nice-to-have, or delegatable

### The resulting quadrants

| Quadrant | Action |
|----------|--------|
| **High urgency + High importance** | Principal responds immediately, or EA drafts and principal approves within 1 hour |
| **High urgency + Medium/Low importance** | EA responds on principal's behalf (with delegation authority), or escalates to deputy |
| **Medium urgency + High importance** | Principal responds within 24h; EA prepares brief/context if needed |
| **Low urgency + High importance** | Schedule dedicated time for principal to respond thoughtfully |
| **Everything else** | EA handles, delegates, or archives without principal involvement |

Most emails fall into "everything else." The art is identifying the 5–10% that do not.

## Email

 velocity and batching

**Do not check email continuously.** Continuous checking creates continuous context-switching, which destroys deep work capacity.

Recommended EA schedule:
- **Morning triage (30 min)**: 7:00–7:30am — handle overnight messages, surface urgent items before principal's day starts
- **Midday sweep (15 min)**: 12:00–12:15pm — catch anything that came in during morning meetings
- **Afternoon triage (30 min)**: 4:00–4:30pm — handle afternoon flow, prepare next-day preview
- **Evening check (10 min)**: 7:00–7:10pm — scan for anything that cannot wait until morning

Outside these windows, email is closed. Use Slack or text for genuinely urgent inbound.

For the principal: **two email sessions per day, maximum.** Morning (30 min) and late afternoon (30 min). Everything else is pre-triaged by the EA and delivered as a curated list of items requiring decision or response.

## Drafting on the principal's behalf

The best EAs can draft responses that sound like the principal wrote them. This requires:

### Voice calibration
- Study 10–15 recent emails the principal has sent
- Note sentence length, vocabulary, punctuation style, greeting/closing conventions
- Identify phrases they use frequently vs. phrases they never use
- Calibrate formality: "Best," "Thanks," "Cheers," "Regards," or no closing at all?

### Delegation authority
Establish clear rules for when the EA can send on the principal's behalf vs. when the principal must approve:

**EA sends directly** (no review needed):
- Meeting confirmations and rescheduling
- Declining invitations with standard reason ("calendar conflict")
- Routine vendor/supplier coordination
- Internal team scheduling and logistics
- Responses to requests already decided

**EA drafts, principal reviews** (flag for approval):
- Investor or board communication
- Sensitive HR or personnel matters
- Anything involving financial commitment >$X
- First-time contact with VIPs or key relationships
- Anything where tone or wording carries risk

**Principal drafts directly**:
- Strategic decisions
- High-stakes negotiations
- Personal relationships (mentors, close friends, family)

### Drafting principles
- **Lead with the decision or answer** — "Yes, happy to join" or "Unfortunately I can't make this work" before the explanation
- **Be concise** — The principal's time is valuable, and recipients know it. Three sentences is often enough.
- **Match the formality of the inbound** — If they wrote three paragraphs, a one-line reply feels curt. If they wrote one line, a paragraph feels excessive.
- **Assume good intent, communicate clearly** — Never let irritation seep into a draft. Even when declining or pushing back, the tone is warm and professional.

## Filters, rules, and automation

Every email platform supports filters. Use them ruthlessly.

**Auto-archive (never hits inbox):**
- Newsletters subscribed to but rarely read (send directly to a "Reading" folder, review weekly)
- Automated system notifications that are logged but do not require action
- Marketing emails from known senders (receipts, promotions)

**Auto-label and skip inbox:**
- Internal team emails → labeled, but do not interrupt focus
- Low-priority external contacts → labeled "Low," reviewed in afternoon batch

**VIP filter (always surfaces immediately):**
- Board members, investors, key customers, direct reports, principal's family
- Configure mobile push notifications for VIPs only — everything else can wait

**Templates for common responses:**
Most email clients support canned responses or templates. Build a library:
- "Thanks for reaching out — forwarding to [colleague] who handles this"
- "Unfortunately [Principal] is fully booked that week. Alternative dates: [X, Y, Z]"
- "Thanks for the invite. [Principal] won't be able to attend, but appreciates being included."

Personalize the template with one sentence, then send. This is not cold — it is efficient.

## Inbox zero as a daily habit

Inbox zero is not about processing every email. It is about having processed every email by end of day — meaning every message has been triaged, delegated, responded to, or scheduled.

End-of-day inbox state:
- **Inbox: 0 messages** (everything has been triaged)
- **Flagged / Starred: 3–10 messages** (items requiring principal's decision or response tomorrow)
- **Scheduled / Snoozed: 5–15 messages** (items deferred to a specific future date)
- **Folders / Labels: Everything else** (archived and searchable, but out of sight)

Inbox zero is not a badge of honor — it is a system. The system prevents anything from being forgotten and ensures that high-priority items are always visible.

## Common failure modes

### Over-escalation
Forwarding every semi-important email to the principal "just in case." This trains them to ignore your escalations. Only escalate what genuinely requires their input. Everything else, you own.

### Under-escalation
Trying to handle something beyond your authority because you do not want to "bother" the principal. This creates downstream problems when they find out too late. When in doubt, flag it — but include your recommendation.

### Analysis paralysis
Spending 10 minutes deciding whether an email is urgent or not. If the decision is hard, it is probably medium-priority. Treat it as such and move on. Speed beats perfection in triage.

### Checkbox syndrome
Marking messages as read without deciding what to do with them. This creates the illusion of progress while accumulating hidden debt. Every message must result in a decision, even if the decision is "delete."

### Reactivity over strategy
Letting the inbox dictate the day. The principal's calendar should be designed in advance (deep work, strategic time, communication windows). The inbox fills the gaps — it does not set the agenda.

## The inbox is a service layer

The principal does not manage email. The EA does. The principal responds to a small number of curated, high-priority items that require their voice, their decision, or their relationship. Everything else is handled invisibly.

A well-run inbox is one the principal rarely thinks about — because they trust that nothing important is being missed, and nothing unimportant is reaching them.
