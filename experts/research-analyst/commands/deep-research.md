# /deep-research

A rigorous research pass delivered as an opinionated memo. Not "here's everything I found" — "here's what the evidence supports and why."

---

## Step 1: Frame the question and form a hypothesis

**Before researching, write down:**
1. The precise question (not the topic — the question)
2. A tentative answer (hypothesis): what do you expect to find?
3. What evidence would confirm it? What would refute it?
4. What's the key uncertainty — the thing that most affects the answer?

This makes research directional. Without a hypothesis, research is browsing.

**From USER.md / PLAYBOOK.md:** Any prior research or known positions on this topic; relevant context (e.g., is this research for a board decision, a competitor analysis, or personal curiosity?)

---

## Step 2: Map the landscape (breadth pass)

**Goal: know the territory before going deep.**
- What are the main schools of thought / positions on this?
- Who are the key players, thinkers, or institutions?
- Where is genuine disagreement vs. where is there rough consensus?
- What are the contested questions (empirical uncertainties) vs. value disputes (people weight things differently)?

Time allocation: ~30% of research time. Don't go deep yet.

---

## Step 3: Deep pass on what matters

**Go deep on the areas the breadth pass identified as most important or most contested.**
- Primary sources > secondary > tertiary
- Evaluate each source: Who produced this? What incentives? What methodology? What would it take for this to be wrong?
- Specifically seek out the strongest counter-argument to your hypothesis — find the best version of the opposing view

Source quality tiers:
| Tier | Examples | Weight |
|---|---|---|
| Primary | Original research, primary data, first-hand accounts, official filings | Highest |
| Secondary | Academic synthesis, quality journalism with named sources, analyst reports | High |
| Tertiary | Trade summaries, Wikipedia, secondary commentary | Use for leads, verify before citing |

---

## Step 4: Synthesise — not aggregate

**The question is not "what did various sources say?" but "what does the evidence support?"**

- State your conclusion first (bottom line up front)
- For each key finding: what is the evidence, and how confident are you?
- Name the disagreements: where experts disagree, describe what the disagreement is actually about (the underlying assumption or value difference)
- Be willing to say "I don't know" rather than presenting false certainty

---

## Output (McKinsey memo format)

```
## Research Memo: [Topic / Question]
**Date**: [Date] | **Depth**: Light / Standard / Deep | **Time spent**: [Estimate]

---

### Bottom Line
[The answer, in 1–3 sentences. Committed. Not hedged into uselessness.]

---

### Key findings

1. **[Finding]** — Confidence: HIGH / MEDIUM / LOW
   Evidence: [Specific source(s) and what they show]

2. **[Finding]** — Confidence: [level]
   Evidence: [Source(s)]

3. [Continue for 3–6 key findings]

---

### Where experts disagree

**Dispute**: [What the disagreement is]
**Position A**: [Who holds it, what evidence supports it]
**Position B**: [Counter-position and its evidence]
**My assessment**: [Which position is better supported and why]

---

### Important caveats

- [Limitation 1: what the research doesn't resolve or can't answer]
- [Limitation 2: key assumption that if wrong would change the conclusion]

---

### Background (for those who want depth)

[Full analysis — can be longer. Methodology, source evaluation, alternative framings, nuance that didn't fit in the key findings section.]

---

### Sources

| Source | Type | Confidence | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| [Source] | Primary / Secondary / Tertiary | HIGH / MED / LOW | [Any bias or limitation note] |
```

---

## Edge cases

**Insufficient reliable sources:** State it. "Available sources on this topic are thin / one-sided / primarily from actors with conflicted interests." Don't fabricate confidence.

**Rapidly changing topic:** Flag the shelf life of the findings. "This analysis reflects the state of the evidence as of [date]. Key aspects may have changed."

**Commissioned research vs. independent research:** If the available research was primarily produced by parties with a stake in the conclusion (industry bodies, advocacy groups), weight it accordingly and say so.
