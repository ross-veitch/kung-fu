# /briefing-note — Write a structured briefing note on a topic

**Purpose**: Produce a concise, decision-oriented briefing note that gives the reader everything they need to understand a topic and act on it — in under 5 minutes of reading time.

**When to use**: When a decision-maker needs to be brought up to speed on a topic quickly, before a meeting, investment decision, strategic discussion, or external engagement.

---

## Phase 1: Scope the briefing

### Define the reader and their need
1. **Who is reading this?** (CEO, board member, investor, team lead)
2. **What decision does this inform?** (Investment, partnership, strategy pivot, competitive response)
3. **How much do they already know?** (Expert = skip background; Novice = include context)
4. **Time budget**: Most briefing notes should be readable in 3–5 minutes (~800–1,200 words)

### Define the scope
- What is included: [specific topic boundaries]
- What is excluded: [explicitly state what you are not covering]
- Time horizon: [historical context period + forward-looking period]

---

## Phase 2: Research and synthesize

### Research protocol (time-boxed)
- **Quick scan (15 min)**: Identify the 5–8 most important sources on this topic
- **Deep read (30 min)**: Extract key facts, data points, and expert positions
- **Synthesis (15 min)**: Form a view — what does the evidence say? What is the recommendation?

### Source requirements
- Minimum 3 independent sources
- At least 1 primary source (data, filing, official document)
- At least 1 contrarian or critical perspective
- All claims must be attributable to a specific source

---

## Phase 3: Write the briefing note

Use the following structure. Every section is mandatory.

### 1. BLUF (Bottom Line Up Front) — 2–3 sentences
State the conclusion first. The reader should know your view after the first paragraph.

> "Company X is a credible acquisition target at the proposed valuation. The strategic rationale is strong (geographic expansion into MENA), but due diligence should focus on [specific risk]. We recommend proceeding to detailed DD with a target close by [date]."

### 2. Background — 3–5 sentences
Only the context necessary to understand the BLUF. Do not write a history of the topic. Include only what the reader needs to follow the analysis.

### 3. Key findings — 3–5 bullet points
The most important facts, data points, or insights from the research. Each bullet should be one insight, with source attribution.

Format:
- **[Finding]** — [Source, date]
- **[Finding]** — [Source, date]

### 4. Analysis — 2–3 paragraphs
Your synthesis of the findings. This is where you:
- Connect the dots between the findings
- Address the key question or decision
- Present the strongest counterargument and explain why you still hold your view
- Flag uncertainties and their potential impact

### 5. Risks and uncertainties — 3–5 bullet points
What could go wrong? What don't we know?
- **[Risk]** — Likelihood: [High/Medium/Low] — Impact: [High/Medium/Low]

### 6. Recommendation — 1–3 sentences
Specific, actionable recommendation tied to the decision context.

### 7. Sources — numbered list
All sources cited, with URLs or document references.

---

## Phase 4: Quality check

Before delivering, verify:

- [ ] BLUF answers the reader's question in the first paragraph
- [ ] No section exceeds its target length
- [ ] Every factual claim has a source
- [ ] Counterarguments are addressed, not ignored
- [ ] Recommendation is specific and actionable
- [ ] Total length: 800–1,200 words (1 page single-spaced, 2 pages double-spaced)
- [ ] No jargon unexplained (if the reader is non-expert)
- [ ] Confidence level is stated for uncertain claims
