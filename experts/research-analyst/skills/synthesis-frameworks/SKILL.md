# Synthesis Frameworks

Synthesis is the core analytical skill — the ability to take heterogeneous, often contradictory evidence and produce a coherent, defensible view. It is fundamentally different from aggregation. Aggregation says "five sources say X, three say Y." Synthesis says "the disagreement turns on assumption Z, and the evidence favors X because..."

## The synthesis process

### Step 1: Map the landscape of claims

Before synthesizing, catalogue what you have found:

- **What are the main claims or positions?** Group them into 2–4 schools of thought.
- **What evidence supports each?** List the evidence basis for each claim (data, expert opinion, logical argument, case study).
- **Where do they agree?** Points of consensus are usually reliable — multiple independent sources reaching the same conclusion from different angles.
- **Where do they disagree?** This is where synthesis adds value. Identify the exact point of divergence.

### Step 2: Diagnose the disagreement

Disagreements between credible sources usually stem from one of five causes:

**1. Different data**: Sources are looking at different datasets (different time periods, geographies, samples). Resolution: identify which dataset is more relevant to the question at hand.

**2. Different definitions**: Sources define key terms differently ("active users," "market size," "profitability"). Resolution: standardize definitions, recompute if possible.

**3. Different assumptions**: Sources agree on the data but make different assumptions about causation, trend continuation, or structural factors. Resolution: make assumptions explicit and test which is better supported.

**4. Different frameworks**: Sources use different analytical lenses (neoclassical vs behavioral economics, market-driven vs technology-driven). Resolution: evaluate which framework better fits the specific context.

**5. Different incentives**: Sources have different motivations (bullish analyst vs bearish short-seller). Resolution: weight by incentive alignment — who benefits from being wrong?

Diagnosing the root cause of disagreement is often more valuable than resolving it. The diagnosis itself is insight.

### Step 3: Weigh the evidence

Not all evidence is equal. Apply weights based on:

- **Source quality**: Primary > secondary > tertiary (see source-evaluation skill)
- **Recency**: More recent data gets more weight (unless the question is structural/historical)
- **Independence**: Evidence from independent sources that converges is much stronger than dependent sources echoing each other
- **Methodology**: Rigorous methodology (large sample, controlled experiment, transparent methods) > anecdotal or opaque
- **Consistency**: Evidence that is consistent across multiple studies, time periods, and geographies is more trustworthy

### Step 4: Form a view

Based on weighted evidence, commit to a position:

- **Strong conviction**: "The evidence clearly supports X. Counterarguments are weak because [specific reason]."
- **Moderate conviction**: "The balance of evidence favors X, though Y is plausible if [specific condition] holds."
- **Low conviction**: "The evidence is genuinely ambiguous. The key unknown is [specific factor]. When that becomes clear, the answer will be [conditional]."

**Never hedge everything into mush.** "It depends" is not analysis. Say what you think, qualify appropriately, and explain what would change your mind.

### Step 5: Structure the output

The synthesis must be structured for the reader's decision, not the researcher's process.

## Output frameworks

### The Pyramid Principle (Minto)

The most widely used framework for structured analytical communication:

**Structure:**
1. **Situation**: What is the context? (Known, agreed-upon facts)
2. **Complication**: What has changed? What is the tension? (The reason this analysis is needed)
3. **Question**: What does the reader need to know? (Implicit or explicit)
4. **Answer**: Your synthesized view (stated upfront, not at the end)
5. **Supporting arguments**: 2–4 MECE (mutually exclusive, collectively exhaustive) reasons that support the answer
6. **Evidence**: Data and sources that support each argument

**Key rule**: Answer first, then justify. Never build up to the conclusion — state it immediately and spend the rest of the memo defending it.

### The SCQA framework (situation, complication, question, answer)

A lighter version of the Pyramid, useful for shorter memos and briefings:

- **S**: "Company X is the market leader in travel metasearch in MENA"
- **C**: "A new competitor has entered with a differentiated product and aggressive pricing"
- **Q**: "Should we respond, and if so, how?"
- **A**: "We should respond with [specific action] because [reason], supported by [evidence]"

### The opposing arguments framework

When the question is genuinely contentious, structure as a structured debate:

1. **Thesis**: The strongest version of position A
2. **Antithesis**: The strongest version of position B (not a strawman — the actual best argument)
3. **What the disagreement really turns on**: The specific assumption, data point, or value judgment that separates A from B
4. **Assessment**: Which position is better supported, why, and with what confidence

This framework is particularly useful for strategic decisions, investment theses, and policy questions.

### The confidence-weighted summary

When the evidence is mixed and certainty is low:

| Claim | Evidence | Confidence | Notes |
|-------|----------|------------|-------|
| Market is growing 15%/year | 3 reports, consistent data | High | Agree across sources |
| Company X has 40% market share | 1 analyst report | Medium | Cannot independently verify |
| AI will disrupt this market in 2 years | Expert opinion, no data | Low | Plausible but speculative |

This makes the evidentiary basis transparent and lets the reader calibrate their own confidence.

## Synthesis quality markers

A well-synthesized analysis has these properties:

**1. It has a clear point of view.** The reader knows what the analyst thinks after the first paragraph.

**2. It distinguishes facts from inferences.** "Revenue was $50M" (fact) vs. "Revenue will grow 20%" (inference). These are different objects and should be labeled as such.

**3. It addresses counterarguments.** If someone could reasonably disagree, the memo acknowledges why and explains why the analyst's view is still preferred.

**4. It states what would change the view.** "If churn exceeds 8%, this thesis breaks." This is the hallmark of rigorous thinking — knowing the conditions under which you are wrong.

**5. It is appropriately scoped.** The analysis covers what it claims to cover and explicitly states what is out of scope.

**6. It leads to a decision.** A memo that ends with "more research is needed" has failed. If more research is genuinely needed, specify exactly what information is missing and how to get it.

## Common synthesis failures

### The literature review masquerading as synthesis
"Author A says X. Author B says Y. Author C says Z." This is aggregation, not synthesis. It lists perspectives without evaluating them, weighing evidence, or forming a view.

**Fix**: After listing perspectives, add the paragraph that starts with "The disagreement turns on..." and ends with "I believe the evidence favors..."

### The false balance
Presenting two positions as equally credible when the evidence clearly favors one. This is intellectual cowardice — the researcher avoids committing to a view by pretending the question is harder than it is.

**Fix**: If one side has stronger evidence, say so. "Both sides have merit" is only true when both sides actually have merit.

### The kitchen sink
Including every finding from the research because "it might be relevant." The reader drowns in information and cannot find the insight.

**Fix**: Apply the 80/20 rule. 20% of the findings contain 80% of the insight. Cut the rest. If the reader wants more detail, they can ask.

### The conclusion-first bias
Starting with a conclusion and selecting only evidence that supports it. The researcher confuses advocacy with analysis.

**Fix**: Write down the hypothesis before researching. Track evidence for and against. If the evidence goes against the hypothesis, update the hypothesis — do not ignore the evidence.

### The precision trap
Presenting precise numbers when the underlying estimates are rough. "The TAM is $4.73 billion" implies a level of certainty that does not exist when the underlying methodology is a top-down estimate with ±30% error.

**Fix**: Use appropriate precision. "The TAM is approximately $4–5 billion" is more honest and equally useful.

## The meta-framework: Think in bets

Treat every analytical conclusion as a bet with a probability. "I am 80% confident that X is true" is more useful than "X is true" or "X might be true." Calibrated probability forces you to think about what you actually know versus what you are assuming.

**High conviction (>80%)**: Strong evidence, multiple independent sources, consistent logic. Recommend action.
**Moderate conviction (50–80%)**: Good evidence but gaps. Recommend conditional action with monitoring.
**Low conviction (<50%)**: Insufficient evidence. Recommend gathering specific additional information before acting.

This is not hedging — it is honest communication of uncertainty. The best analysts are well-calibrated: when they say "80% confident," they are right about 80% of the time.
