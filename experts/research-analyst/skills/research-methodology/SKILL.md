# Research Methodology

## Start with a hypothesis

Before starting research: form a tentative answer to the question. Write it down.

This isn't because you expect to be right. It's because a hypothesis tells you:
- What evidence would confirm it
- What evidence would refute it
- What you can stop searching once you've found it

Research without a hypothesis produces comprehensive but inconclusive outputs. Research with a hypothesis produces testable findings.

Update the hypothesis as you learn. A hypothesis that survives contact with the evidence is a finding. One that gets overturned is also a finding — often a more valuable one.

## The two-pass approach

**Pass 1: Map the landscape (30–40% of time)**

Goal: understand the terrain before going deep.
- Who are the main players / schools of thought?
- What is genuinely contested vs what is settled?
- Where is the best primary evidence?
- What is being actively argued about right now?

Output: a mental map of the topic with the key questions identified.

**Pass 2: Go deep on what matters (60–70% of time)**

Now that you know where the signal is: dig. Primary sources. Most-cited papers. Expert writing. Contradictory evidence.

Resist the pull to continue mapping forever. At some point additional breadth has diminishing returns. Switch to depth.

## Source hierarchy

| Tier | Examples | Trust level |
|------|---------|-------------|
| Primary | Original research, SEC filings, court documents, government data, direct interviews | High on facts; requires context interpretation |
| Secondary | Academic review articles, quality journalism, analyst reports | Moderate; verify key claims against primary |
| Tertiary | Wikipedia, encyclopedias, aggregators | Useful for orientation, never cite for substance |
| Opinion/Commentary | Substack, newsletters, blog posts | Valuable for perspective, not evidence |

**Evaluate every source for**:
1. Incentives: who funded this? Who benefits from this conclusion being true?
2. Methodology: how was this data collected? What are the sample size and selection criteria?
3. Date: when was this? Does it pre-date key developments that would change the finding?
4. Independence: is this based on original work, or citing something else citing something else?

## The synthesis move

Synthesis is not aggregation. Aggregation: "Expert A says X. Expert B says Y. Expert C says Z." Synthesis: "The disagreement between X and Y turns on assumption Q. The evidence for Q from Expert C suggests Z is closer to right, but only if context K holds."

The synthesis move requires:
1. Identifying what the disagreement is *really* about (often not what it appears to be)
2. Evaluating the evidence bearing on that specific point
3. Forming a view — not "it's complicated" but "on balance, the evidence points to X because Y"

A memo that concludes "more research is needed" on a time-sensitive question is not a memo — it's a request for delay.

## Memo structure

The correct output for most research is a memo, not a slide deck or a brain dump.

**Standard structure**:
1. **Recommendation / Bottom Line** (1 paragraph): what is the answer to the question, and what should the reader do?
2. **Key findings** (3–5 bullet points): the evidence that most supports the recommendation
3. **Important caveats** (2–3 bullet points): where uncertainty is highest, what could change the conclusion
4. **Background** (as much as needed): the full analysis for those who want to go deeper

Write the structure in this order even if you draft from the bottom up. The reader sees it top-down.

## Knowing when to stop

Research has diminishing returns. You have enough when:
- Additional sources are repeating information you already have
- You can anticipate the counterarguments and you have responses to them
- The person who needs the output would reach the same conclusion from what you have

Perfectionism in research is procrastination in disguise. Know when you're done.
