# MENA Business Culture

## Relationship-First Culture (Wasta)

**Wasta** = connections, influence, who you know

**How it works**:
- Business is done through trusted relationships, not cold outreach
- Warm introductions are everything (mutual friend, family connection, previous business partner)
- Trust is built over months, not meetings
- Personal relationships come before business transactions

**Practical implications**:
- Don't cold-email a CEO in Riyadh and expect a response
- Ask: "Who do you know who can introduce me?"
- Invest in relationship-building before asking for the deal
- Expect 6-12 months from first meeting to deal close

---

## Decision-Making Hierarchy

**Top-down** in most MENA markets (especially GCC):
- CEO/founder makes decisions (not committees)
- Mid-level managers have limited authority (need approval from top)
- Consensus is less common than in Western companies
- Family businesses: patriarch makes final call

**Meeting dynamics**:
- Pitch to the decision-maker directly (don't waste time with mid-level)
- Respect hierarchy (don't bypass people)
- Decisions happen after the meeting (rarely in the meeting itself)

---

## Meeting Norms

**Tea and relationship-building first** (15-30 minutes):
- Meetings start with small talk (family, health, general topics)
- Jumping straight to business is rude
- This is not wasted time — it's relationship-building

**Dress code**:
- Men: Suit and tie (or business casual in UAE)
- Women: Conservative (cover shoulders, knees; headscarf optional except in Saudi)

**Language**:
- English is widely spoken in business (GCC, Jordan)
- Arabic is appreciated (even basic greetings show respect)
- Bring Arabic materials if available (business cards, pitch decks)

**Time**:
- Expect delays (meetings may start 15-30 min late)
- "Inshallah" (God willing) = "we'll try but no guarantees"
- Deadlines are flexible (Western urgency is not always shared)

## Ramadan Business Patterns

**Ramadan** = 9th month of Islamic calendar, month of fasting from sunrise to sunset

**Business impact**:
- Work hours shortened (typically 9am-2pm)
- Energy is lower (people are fasting)
- Meetings are harder to schedule
- Business slows dramatically
- Evening after Iftar (breaking fast) is family time

**Strategy**:
- Avoid launching major initiatives during Ramadan
- Plan around Ramadan (it shifts ~11 days earlier each year on Gregorian calendar)
- If you must meet, early morning (9-11am) is best

## Arabic vs English Business Communication

**Email**:
- English is standard for business
- Opening with "السلام عليكم" (Assalamu Alaikum — peace be upon you) is polite
- Expect slower response times than Western markets (2-5 days is normal)

**Verbal communication**:
- Indirectness is polite (direct "no" is rude)
- "Yes" sometimes means "maybe" (read between the lines)
- "We'll think about it" often means "no" (but politely)
- Ask clarifying questions to understand true intent

**Negotiation**:
- Expect negotiation on everything (price, terms, timelines)
- First offer is rarely accepted (leave room to negotiate)
- Patience is valued (don't rush)

## Localization Requirements

**Language**:
- Arabic localization matters (especially in Saudi)
- RTL (right-to-left) UI design (not just translated text)
- Formal Arabic (Modern Standard Arabic) for business, Dialectal Arabic for marketing

**Calendar**:
- Hijri calendar (Islamic lunar calendar) alongside Gregorian
- Ramadan dates vary each year (plan ahead)

**Prayer times**:
- 5 daily prayers (Fajr, Dhuhr, Asr, Maghrib, Isha)
- Businesses close briefly for prayer (especially in Saudi)
- Apps should display prayer times (relevant for travel/booking apps)

**Payment**:
- Cash is still dominant (Egypt, some parts of Saudi)
- Local payment methods: mada (Saudi), KNET (Kuwait), mada vs Visa/Mastercard
- BNPL (Buy Now Pay Later) is huge in UAE (Tabby, Tamara)

**Cultural sensitivity**:
- Avoid pork/alcohol in imagery (haram in Islam)
- Modest imagery (no revealing clothing, especially for women)
- Family-friendly content (conservative values)

## Women in Business

**Varies by market**:
- **UAE**: Women in leadership roles (especially Dubai), Western norms mostly apply
- **Saudi Arabia**: Rapid change post-2017 (women can drive, travel, work without male guardian), but still conservative compared to West
- **Egypt**: Mixed (women in business common in urban areas, less so in rural)

**Best practices**:
- Don't assume gender norms from one MENA country apply to another
- In Saudi: meetings are often gender-segregated (especially government/conservative companies)
- In UAE: mixed meetings are normal

## Common Mistakes Foreign Companies Make

1. **Treating MENA as one culture**: Saudi culture ≠ UAE culture ≠ Egyptian culture
2. **Rushing relationships**: Expecting a deal after one meeting (relationships take months)
3. **Ignoring hierarchy**: Pitching to mid-level managers who can't decide
4. **Cold outreach**: No warm introduction = low response rate
5. **Impatience with timelines**: Expecting Western-speed decisions (MENA is slower)
6. **Ignoring Ramadan**: Launching during Ramadan = bad timing
7. **Not localizing Arabic**: English-only product in Saudi = missed opportunity
8. **Underestimating payment complexity**: Assuming everyone uses Visa/Mastercard (cash is still king in Egypt)
