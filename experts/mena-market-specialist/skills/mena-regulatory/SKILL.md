# MENA Regulatory Environment

Regulatory frameworks, licensing, and compliance requirements across key MENA markets — what you need to know before operating.

---

## Saudi Arabia

**Company formation**: Foreign companies can establish through MISA (Ministry of Investment) — formerly SAGIA. Options: LLC (most common), branch office, or regional HQ (now mandatory for government contracts — companies must have regional HQ in Saudi by 2024+). Free zones exist but are less developed than UAE.

**Saudization (Nitaqat)**: The most significant operational requirement. Companies are classified by sector and size into bands (Platinum, Green, Yellow, Red) based on the ratio of Saudi nationals employed. Failing Nitaqat blocks visa issuance and can lead to penalties. Minimum Saudi hiring percentages vary by sector (typically 30–70%).

**Data localization**: Saudi PDPL (Personal Data Protection Law, effective September 2023) requires data processing consent and has provisions around cross-border data transfer. Cloud hosting must comply with NCA (National Cybersecurity Authority) and CITC regulations. Financial data has stricter localization requirements.

**Licensing**: Sector-specific licenses required. Tourism: Ministry of Tourism license. E-commerce: Ministry of Commerce registration. Fintech: SAMA (Saudi Arabian Monetary Authority) sandbox or license. Telecom: CITC.

**Tax**: 15% VAT (since July 2020). 20% corporate income tax on foreign-owned entities (Saudi/GCC-owned entities pay 2.5% Zakat instead). Withholding tax on payments to non-residents.

## UAE

**Free zones vs mainland**: The fundamental structural decision. UAE has 40+ free zones, each with different rules.

| Feature | Free zone | Mainland |
|---------|-----------|----------|
| Foreign ownership | 100% | 100% (since June 2021 for most sectors) |
| Corporate tax | Subject to UAE CT (9%) | Subject to UAE CT (9%) |
| Trade with local market | Limited (through agent/distributor) | Direct |
| Visa sponsorship | Through free zone authority | Through company |
| Regulatory body | Free zone authority | DED (Department of Economic Development) |

**Key free zones**: DIFC (financial services — common law jurisdiction, own courts), ADGM (Abu Dhabi financial free zone), DMCC (commodities and general trading), Dubai Internet City/Media City (tech and media), JAFZA (logistics), Masdar City (clean tech).

**Corporate tax**: 9% on taxable profits above AED 375,000 (effective June 2023). Free zone entities can qualify for 0% rate on qualifying income if conditions are met. This was a major change — UAE was previously tax-free.

**Data protection**: UAE Federal Decree-Law No. 45/2021 (PDPL). DIFC and ADGM have their own data protection regulations modelled on GDPR. Cross-border data transfer requires adequacy assessment or contractual safeguards.

## Egypt

**Company formation**: General Authority for Investment and Free Zones (GAFI) handles foreign investment. Options: LLC, joint stock company, branch office, representative office. Free zones exist (public and private) with tax incentives.

**FX controls**: The most significant operational challenge. Egypt has a managed float currency; periodic devaluations create uncertainty. Repatriating profits requires central bank approval in practice. Businesses must manage FX exposure actively.

**Payment infrastructure**: Cash-dominant economy (70%+ of transactions). Card penetration growing but still low. Mobile money (Vodafone Cash, Orange Money) growing rapidly. Fawry is the dominant electronic bill payment network.

**Tax**: 22.5% corporate tax (general). VAT 14%. Various withholding taxes on dividends and payments to non-residents.

**Labor**: Generous labor protections for employees. Termination is difficult and expensive. Social insurance contributions required. Hiring is relatively cheap (average tech salary significantly lower than GCC).

---

## Jordan

**Company formation**: Companies Control Department (CCD) handles registration. Aqaba Special Economic Zone (ASEZA) offers significant tax incentives: 5% flat income tax, 0% customs on imports, 5% social services tax.

**IT/BPO sector**: Jordan has positioned itself as a regional tech hub. Educated workforce (strong universities), lower costs than Gulf, and proximity to Saudi/UAE. Many regional tech companies have Jordan development centres.

**Tax**: 20% standard corporate tax (higher for banks, telecom). ASEZA: 5%. Income tax for individuals: progressive up to 25%.

## GCC-wide considerations

**VAT**: All GCC states have implemented or plan to implement VAT. Saudi: 15%, UAE: 5%, Bahrain: 10%, Oman: 5%. Kuwait and Qatar still pending implementation.

**Data protection**: Evolving rapidly. Saudi PDPL, UAE PDPL, Bahrain PDPA, Qatar Data Protection Law. Trend is toward GDPR-influenced frameworks but with local variations (especially around government data access).

**Payment regulations**: Each country has its own fintech licensing framework. Saudi (SAMA fintech sandbox), UAE (CBUAE, DIFC Innovation Testing License), Bahrain (CBB fintech sandbox — the most progressive in the region).

**Employment and visas**: Work permits required for all non-citizens. GCC nationals have freedom of movement for work across GCC states. Sponsorship (kafala) system still exists in modified forms — employer sponsors employee's visa. Reform is ongoing but uneven.

## Common mistakes by foreign companies

1. **Assuming "MENA" is one market**: Regulations differ dramatically country by country and even city by city (DIFC vs Dubai mainland have different legal systems).
2. **Underestimating Saudization costs**: Hiring Saudi nationals at competitive wages significantly increases operational costs vs. expat hires.
3. **Ignoring local partnership dynamics**: While 100% foreign ownership is now technically possible in most markets, having a well-connected local partner remains practically essential for government-adjacent business.
4. **FX exposure in Egypt**: Not hedging or managing EGP exposure has bankrupted regional operations of multinational companies.
5. **Speed assumptions**: Government approvals move at their own pace. Building in 2–3x the expected timeline for licensing, visas, and regulatory approvals is standard practice.
