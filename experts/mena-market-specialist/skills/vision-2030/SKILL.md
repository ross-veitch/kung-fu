# Vision 2030

Saudi Arabia's national transformation programme — what it means for business, investment, and market entry decisions.

---

## What Vision 2030 is

Launched in 2016 by Crown Prince Mohammed bin Salman (MBS), Vision 2030 is Saudi Arabia's plan to diversify its economy away from oil dependency. The programme targets three pillars: a vibrant society, a thriving economy, and an ambitious nation. It represents the most significant economic transformation in the Middle East.

**Scale**: $500B+ in committed investment across sectors. This is not a government white paper — it's backed by real capital deployment from the Public Investment Fund (PIF, now ~$930B AUM), private sector mandates, and foreign direct investment incentives.

---

## GIGA projects

The flagship mega-developments that define the ambition:

**NEOM** ($500B): A 26,500 km² smart city zone in Tabuk Province, northwest Saudi Arabia. Includes The Line (170km linear city, now reportedly scaled back in initial phase), Trojena (mountain tourism and 2029 Asian Winter Games), Sindalah (luxury island resort), and Oxagon (industrial city). Current status: significant construction underway, but timelines have slipped from original 2025 targets. Key risk: execution at scale.

**Red Sea Global** (formerly The Red Sea Development Company): Luxury coastal tourism across 50+ islands on the Red Sea coast. Targeting 1M visitors/year by 2030. Phase 1 (16 hotels) opening progressively. This is Saudi's most credible tourism play — the natural assets are genuinely world-class.

**Qiddiya**: Entertainment mega-destination near Riyadh. Theme parks (including Six Flags), motorsport (Formula E circuit), sports venues, and cultural attractions. Targeting 17M visitors/year. Positioned as the "Capital of Entertainment" — filling a gap in domestic leisure infrastructure.

**Diriyah Gate**: Heritage-focused mixed-use development at the UNESCO World Heritage site of At-Turaif (original seat of the Saudi state). Hotels, retail, cultural venues. High strategic importance as it reinforces Saudi national identity.

**ROSHN**: Government-backed residential developer building entire communities. Addressing Saudi's housing shortage (60% home ownership target vs ~47% at launch).

## Tourism transformation

The single most relevant sector for travel businesses. Saudi issued ~27.5M tourist visas in 2023 (up from effectively zero pre-2019). Target: 150M visits/year by 2030 (domestic + international).

**Key developments**:
- e-Visa launched 2019 — citizens of 49 countries can get visa on arrival or online
- Saudi Tourism Authority (STA) — massive marketing budgets, aggressive PR campaigns
- Hajj and Umrah modernisation — target 30M Umrah visitors/year (from ~19M)
- Entertainment: concerts, sporting events (F1, boxing, football), cultural festivals — all previously prohibited
- Women's travel rights expanded: women can travel without male guardian consent (since 2019)

**Reality check**: Infrastructure is still catching up. Hotel supply outside Riyadh/Jeddah/Makkah/Madinah is limited. Service quality is inconsistent. Many GIGA projects are behind schedule. But the direction is unmistakable and the capital commitment is real.

## PIF (Public Investment Fund)

The engine of Vision 2030. PIF is simultaneously a sovereign wealth fund and a domestic development vehicle.

**AUM**: ~$930B (as of 2024). Target: $2T by 2030.
**Role**: Lead investor in all GIGA projects. Also makes major international investments (SoftBank Vision Fund, Lucid Motors, Newcastle United, LIV Golf, gaming companies).
**Domestic mandates**: PIF has created 90+ portfolio companies to drive Vision 2030 sectors (e.g., ROSHN, Cruise Saudi, Amaala, stc Group restructuring).
**For businesses**: PIF is often the counterparty for large deals in Saudi. Understanding PIF's priorities and investment thesis is essential for any significant commercial engagement.

## What this means for business decisions

1. **Market timing**: Saudi is in a window of aggressive expansion. Companies entering now get first-mover advantages in a market that will be much more competitive in 3–5 years.
2. **Government as customer**: Many opportunities are government-funded or government-adjacent. Understanding procurement processes and relationship dynamics is essential.
3. **Saudization (Nitaqat)**: Any company operating in Saudi must comply with local hiring quotas. This affects cost structure and operational planning significantly.
4. **Regulatory modernisation**: Saudi is rapidly updating regulations to attract FDI — but implementation is uneven. What's announced in Riyadh doesn't always match reality in regional offices.
5. **Long-term commitment**: Saudi partners and government entities favour companies that demonstrate long-term commitment to the market — not those looking for quick wins.
