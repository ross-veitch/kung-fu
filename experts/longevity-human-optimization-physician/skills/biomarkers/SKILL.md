# Biomarker Interpretation

## Optimal vs normal ranges

**Critical distinction**: lab reference ranges are built on population distributions, not optimality. "Normal" means you are in the middle 95% of the tested population — which includes people who are sick, sedentary, and metabolically compromised. For longevity optimisation, we care about *optimal* ranges, not just "not flagged".

The key question for every biomarker: *what range predicts the best long-term outcomes in large prospective studies?*

## Core longevity biomarker panel

### Metabolic health
| Marker | Standard normal | Optimal | What it tells you |
|--------|----------------|---------|-------------------|
| Fasting glucose | 70–99 mg/dL | 70–85 mg/dL | Insulin sensitivity, diabetes risk |
| HbA1c | < 5.7% | < 5.3% | 3-month average glucose |
| Fasting insulin | < 25 µIU/mL | < 6–8 µIU/mL | Insulin resistance (most labs don't flag mild resistance) |
| HOMA-IR | < 2.0 | < 1.0 | Insulin resistance index (glucose × insulin / 405) |
| Triglycerides | < 150 mg/dL | < 80 mg/dL | Metabolic health, dietary carbohydrate load |
| HDL cholesterol | > 40 (M) / > 50 (F) mg/dL | > 60 mg/dL | Reverse cholesterol transport |

### Cardiovascular risk (advanced lipid panel)
| Marker | What it tells you |
|--------|-------------------|
| LDL-P (particle count) | More predictive than LDL-C alone; high particle count at normal LDL-C = risk |
| ApoB | Directly measures atherogenic particle burden; the most important cardiovascular biomarker |
| Lp(a) | Genetic cardiovascular risk factor; elevated Lp(a) is independent of diet/lifestyle |
| hsCRP | Systemic inflammation; > 1.0 mg/L = elevated risk regardless of lipid values |
| Homocysteine | Vascular damage marker; elevated by B12/folate deficiency and MTHFR mutations |

### Hormonal health
| Marker | Notes |
|--------|-------|
| Total testosterone | Reference range is broad; symptoms matter as much as numbers |
| Free testosterone | More clinically relevant than total (accounts for SHBG binding) |
| SHBG | High SHBG reduces free testosterone; affected by thyroid, insulin resistance |
| DHEA-S | Adrenal health, declines with age; proxy for biological aging |
| Cortisol (AM) | Stress load, adrenal function; best taken at 8am |
| TSH, Free T4, Free T3 | Full thyroid panel; TSH alone misses many functional issues |
| Vitamin D (25-OH) | Optimal 50–80 ng/mL; most people are below 40 ng/mL |

### Inflammatory and organ function
| Marker | Optimal | Notes |
|--------|---------|-------|
| hsCRP | < 0.5 mg/L | Systemic inflammation |
| Ferritin | 30–100 ng/mL | High ferritin = inflammation (not just iron stores) |
| ALT/AST | Lower in optimal range | Liver stress; elevated by alcohol, fatty liver, statins |
| eGFR (kidney function) | > 90 mL/min/1.73m² | Declining trend matters |
| Uric acid | < 5.5 mg/dL | Gout risk, metabolic health, fructose marker |

## How to read a trend, not just a value

A single lab value is a snapshot. What matters is:
1. **Direction**: is it moving toward or away from optimal?
2. **Rate of change**: slow drift is different from rapid change
3. **Clinical context**: the same number means different things in different contexts (e.g., low ferritin with symptoms = deficiency; low ferritin with no symptoms and confirmed vegan diet = dietary)
4. **Correlation**: multiple markers often move together; if ApoB is high and triglycerides are high and HDL is low, that is a pattern (metabolic syndrome cluster) not three separate problems

## What to test and when

**Minimum annual panel** (everyone):
CBC with differential, comprehensive metabolic panel, lipid panel (standard), HbA1c, fasting insulin, TSH, Vitamin D, hsCRP, ferritin, uric acid

**Extended longevity panel** (add):
ApoB, LDL-P, Lp(a), DHEA-S, free testosterone (M) / estradiol (F), homocysteine, free T3/T4

**Advanced / periodic**:
DEXA scan (body composition + bone density), VO2 max test, continuous glucose monitor (CGM, 2-week wear), coronary calcium score (over 40)
