# Talent Management

## Performance Management Frameworks

### Performance review calibration (forced curve vs no curve)

**Calibration** = managers meet to align on performance ratings across teams before finalizing reviews.

**Why calibrate?**
- Eliminates manager bias (some managers rate everyone high, others rate everyone low)
- Ensures fairness across teams
- Forces honest conversations about who is truly high-performing

**Forced curve (stack ranking)**:
- Example: Top 20% = Exceeds, Middle 70% = Meets, Bottom 10% = Below
- **Pros**: Forces differentiation, identifies clear under/over-performers
- **Cons**: Demotivating if curve is rigid; encourages competition over collaboration
- **When to use**: Large orgs (500+ people), sales orgs, when talent density is low

**No curve (manager discretion)**:
- Managers rate based on performance, not quota
- **Pros**: More flexible, less political, better for high-performing teams
- **Cons**: Grade inflation (everyone is "exceeds"), harder to identify underperformers
- **When to use**: Startups, high-talent-density teams, creative/knowledge work

**Hybrid approach (recommended)**:
- Calibrate without a forced curve, but flag outliers (manager who rates 80% as "exceeds")
- Use data to guide, not mandate: "This team has 60% exceeds rating — is that defensible?"

### 9-Box Grid (Talent Matrix)

Maps **Performance** (x-axis) vs **Potential** (y-axis) to identify talent segments.

```

Potential│  (inconsistent     │  (strong performer  │  (top performer +
        │   performer, high  │   + ready for more) │   ready for next level)
        │   growth)          │                     │
────────┼────────────────────┼─────────────────────┼─────────────────────
Medium  │  4. Enigma         │  5. Core Contributor│  6. High Performer
Potential│  (underperforming  │  (solid, steady,    │  (great at role, 
        │   but fixable)     │   meets expectations│   likely topped out)
        │                    │   reliably)         │
────────┼────────────────────┼─────────────────────┼─────────────────────
Low     │  1. Poor Fit       │  2. Effective       │  3. Solid Performer
Potential│  (wrong role,      │  (meets standards   │  (good at job,
        │   should exit)     │   but limited growth│   no promotion path)
        │                    │   path)             │
        Low                  Medium                High
                          Performance →
```

### Talent actions by box

| Box | Action | Retention Risk |
|---|---|---|
| **9. Star** | Fast-track promotion, give stretch projects, retain at all costs | High (external offers) |
| **8. High Potential** | Invest in development, prepare for promotion in 12-18 months | Medium |
| **7. Potential Gem** | Coach on performance consistency, high upside if fixed | Medium |
| **6. High Performer** | Reward well but don't over-promote (topped out at current level) | Low |
| **5. Core Contributor** | Backbone of the org; recognize their steady value | Low |
| **4. Enigma** | 90-day improvement plan; if no progress, exit | Medium (they may leave) |
| **3. Solid Performer** | Keep in role; don't promote; compensate fairly | Low |
| **2. Effective** | Keep or exit depending on business need | Low |
| **1. Poor Fit** | Exit within 90 days (PIP or direct termination) | N/A (you want them to leave) |

**Rule**: Invest 70% of development resources in boxes 7, 8, 9. Don't over-invest in boxes 1-3 (low ROI).

## High-Performer Retention

### Why high performers leave

1. **Compensation not competitive** (they get external offers)
2. **No growth path** (topped out, no promotion in sight)
3. **Manager relationship broken** (bad boss is #1 reason for quitting)
4. **Bored / unchallenged** (doing the same work for too long)
5. **Mission drift** (company strategy changed and they don't believe in it anymore)

### Retention strategies by cause

| Cause | Retention Fix |
|---|---|
| Compensation gap | Equity refresh, off-cycle raise, promotion |
| No growth path | Create new role, give stretch project, lateral move to new team |
| Bad manager | Move to different team or get manager coaching |
| Bored | Rotate to new project, give ownership of strategic initiative |
| Mission drift | Honest conversation (you can't fake mission fit) |

**Warning signs** (high performer is likely looking):
- Disengaged in meetings (used to be vocal, now quiet)
- Interview requests on LinkedIn spike
- Suddenly takes PTO (interview trips)
- Asks about "what's next for me here?" (testing if there's a path)

**Proactive retention (before they're looking)**:
- Quarterly career conversations (not just annual review)
- Equity refreshes every 12-18 months for stars
- Visibility with leadership (make sure execs know who they are)
- Protect from burnout (don't overload stars just because they can handle it)

## Compensation Strategy

### Market-rate comp vs value-based comp

**Market-rate comp** = what competitors pay for the role (benchmark via Radford, Mercer, Payscale, Levels.fyi)  
**Value-based comp** = what the role is worth to your business (strategic value, revenue impact, replaceability)

**Example**:  
- Market rate for Senior Engineer in Singapore: $120K-140K  
- But your top Senior Engineer built your core platform → value to business is $200K+  
- **Decision**: Pay above market ($160K) to retain them

**When to pay above market**:
- Critical role (hard to replace)
- High performer (top 10%)
- Retention risk (external offers)
- Strategic role (builds future capability)

**When to pay at market**:
- Core contributors (solid but not stars)
- Easily replaceable roles
- Roles with deep talent bench

---

### Comp bands (salary ranges by level)

Comp bands define min/max salary for each level/role.

**Example comp band:**

| Level | Role | Min | Mid | Max |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| IC3 | Senior Engineer | $100K | $120K | $140K |
| IC4 | Staff Engineer | $140K | $165K | $190K |
| IC5 | Principal Engineer | $180K | $210K | $240K |

**Band width**: Typically 40-50% spread (max = min × 1.4 to 1.5)

**Where people land in band**:
- **New hire**: 50th-75th percentile (leave room for growth)
- **Solid performer**: 60th-80th percentile
- **Top performer**: 80th-100th percentile
- **Topped out**: At band max → must promote to get more salary

**Merit increase vs promotion increase**:
- **Merit increase** (annual raise): 3-8% for solid performers
- **Promotion increase**: 10-20% (bigger jump because role changed)

---

### Equity refresh programs

**Problem**: Early employees' equity vests out → retention risk increases  
**Solution**: Grant new equity (refresh) to keep them golden-handcuffed

**Typical refresh cadence**: Every 12-18 months for high performers

**Refresh sizing**:
- **Star performers (top 10%)**: 50-70% of original grant
- **High performers (top 30%)**: 30-50% of original grant
- **Core performers**: 10-30% of original grant

**Vesting schedule**: Usually 4 years with 1-year cliff (same as initial grant)

## Multi-Jurisdiction Employment Law (Basics)

**Disclaimer**: This is not legal advice. Always involve employment counsel for specific situations.

### Singapore (Ministry of Manpower — MOM)

**Employment Pass (EP) requirements**:
- Min salary: SGD $5,000/month (higher for older candidates)
- COMPASS points system (degree, salary, firm diversity, local PMET benchmarks)
- Renewal: Must maintain qualifying salary + pass COMPASS scoring

**Termination notice**:
- Governed by employment contract (typically 1-3 months)
- Retrenchment: Must notify MOM, provide retrenchment benefits (typically 2 weeks to 1 month per year of service)
- No at-will employment (must have cause or give notice + pay)

**Key compliance**:
- CPF (Central Provident Fund): Employer contributes 17% of salary for Singaporeans/PRs
- Fair Consideration Framework: Must advertise jobs on MyCareersFuture before hiring foreigners

---

### UAE (Dubai / Abu Dhabi — DIFC vs Mainland)

**Two legal systems**:
- **DIFC (Dubai International Financial Centre)**: Common law, more employee-friendly
- **Mainland UAE**: Local labor law, more employer-friendly

**Contract types**:
- **Limited term contract**: Fixed end date (1-3 years). Termination before end date = compensation penalty.
- **Unlimited term contract**: No fixed end date. Easier to terminate with notice.

**Termination notice**:
- DIFC: 30-90 days (depends on contract)
- Mainland: 30 days (can be extended to 90 days by mutual agreement)

**End-of-Service Gratuity** (severance):
- 21 days of basic salary per year of service (years 1-5)
- 30 days of basic salary per year of service (years 6+)
- Capped at 2 years of salary
- **Only paid if employee resigns after 1+ year OR is terminated (not if they resign <1 year)**

**Key compliance**:
- Visa tied to employer (employee must find new sponsor or leave UAE within 30 days of termination)
- No income tax (but 5% VAT on goods/services)

---

### Saudi Arabia (Ministry of Human Resources and Social Development — MHRSD)

**Nitaqat system** (Saudization quotas):
- Companies must hire a % of Saudi nationals (varies by industry + company size)
- Non-compliance = penalties (can't hire foreigners, can't renew visas)

**Contract types**:
- **Indefinite contract**: Standard (like at-will but with notice)
- **Fixed-term contract**: Max 4 years; must convert to indefinite after that

**Termination notice**:
- 60 days for indefinite contracts
- 30 days for fixed-term contracts
- End-of-service award: Half-month salary per year (first 5 years), one month per year (after 5 years)

**Key compliance**:
- Work week: Sunday-Thursday (Friday-Saturday weekend)
- Women in workforce: Rapid growth post-Vision 2030; equal pay laws in place

---

### India (Shops and Establishments Act / Contract Labor Act)

**Contract types**:
- **Permanent employment**: Rare (strong labor protections, hard to terminate)
- **Fixed-term contract**: Most common for white-collar (1-3 years, renewable)
- **Contract labor**: Via staffing agencies (common for lower-skilled roles)

**Termination notice**:
- Governed by employment contract (typically 30-90 days)
- Severance: Not mandatory unless contract specifies OR under retrenchment law (for permanent employees only)

**Provident Fund (PF)**:
- Employer + employee each contribute 12% of basic salary
- Mandatory for companies with 20+ employees

**Key compliance**:
- State-specific laws (employment law varies by state)
- Retrenchment (layoff): If >100 employees, must get government approval (Industrial Disputes Act)
- Gratuity: Paid after 5 years of service (15 days salary per year of service)

## Performance Improvement Plans (PIPs)

### What PIPs are (honest answer)

**In theory**: A tool to help underperformers improve  
**In practice**: Documentation for termination (80-90% of people on PIPs exit)

**When PIPs actually help** (10-20% of cases):
- Role mismatch (good person, wrong seat → move them)
- Skill gap (trainable, and they're motivated to close gap)
- Manager failed to give feedback earlier (surprised by poor review)

**When PIPs are just exit paperwork** (80-90% of cases):
- Capability mismatch (not smart/skilled enough for the role)
- Attitude problem (don't care, not coachable)
- Trust broken (manager has given up on them)

---

### How to run a PIP (if you must)

**Duration**: 30-90 days (longer = false hope)

**Structure**:
1. **Specific performance gaps** (not "you need to improve communication" — too vague)
   - Good: "You missed 3 out of 5 project deadlines in Q2. You must hit 100% of deadlines in Q3."
2. **Observable behaviors / outcomes required**
   - "Complete X deliverable by Y date"
   - "Receive positive feedback from 3 out of 4 stakeholders"
3. **Support provided** (training, coaching, resources)
4. **Check-ins** (weekly 1:1s with manager)
5. **Success criteria** (what "pass" looks like)

**Consequence if they fail**: "If these gaps are not closed by [date], your employment will be terminated."

---

### Termination conversation script

**Setting**: Private room, bring HR, have tissues available

**Script**:
1. **Be direct** (first sentence): "I've made the decision to end your employment, effective [date]."
2. **State reason** (brief, factual): "Despite the PIP, the performance gaps have not closed."
3. **Logistics** (what happens next): "Your last day is [date]. You'll receive [severance details]. HR will walk you through offboarding."
4. **Don't debate**: If they argue, repeat: "The decision is final. I understand this is difficult."
5. **End quickly** (10-15 minutes max): Don't drag it out.

**What NOT to say**:
- "This was a hard decision" (makes it about you, not them)
- "You're a great person" (mixed messages)
- "It's not you, it's us" (confusing — then why are they being fired?)

**After termination**:
- Collect laptop/badge immediately (IT offboards same day)
- Announce internally (same day, brief email: "[Name] is no longer with the company. [Manager] will handle [their work].")

## Succession Planning

**Succession plan** = who can step into a critical role if the incumbent leaves?

### Roles that need succession plans

- C-suite (CEO, CFO, CTO, CPO)
- VP/Director level (leads of critical functions)
- Critical ICs (e.g., engineer who owns core platform, top salesperson)

### Succession readiness levels

**Ready now** (0-6 months): Can step into role immediately with minimal ramp  
**Ready soon** (6-18 months): Needs development but on track  
**Ready later** (18-36 months): High potential but needs significant growth  
**No successor identified**: Retention risk + hiring risk

### Succession planning process

1. **Identify critical roles** (where would we be screwed if this person left tomorrow?)
2. **Assess readiness** (who could step in? how ready are they?)
3. **Develop successors** (give them stretch projects, exposure to leadership, coaching)
4. **Review quarterly** (people's readiness changes)

**Red flag**: If >50% of critical roles have "no successor identified" → talent pipeline problem.
