# Performance Management

How to run performance review cycles, deliver feedback, manage underperformers, and build calibration processes that surface honest assessments.

---

## Performance review cycles

### Cadence options

| Cadence | Best for | Tradeoffs |
|---------|----------|-----------|
| Annual | Traditional orgs, regulated industries | Too infrequent for fast-moving teams; recency bias dominates |
| Bi-annual (H1/H2) | Most tech companies | Good balance; aligns with planning cycles |
| Quarterly | High-growth startups, sales orgs | More signal, more overhead; works if lightweight |
| Continuous (no formal cycle) | Netflix-style culture | Requires very high management maturity |

Most companies land on **bi-annual reviews with quarterly check-ins** — formal feedback twice a year, lightweight goal-setting and pulse checks quarterly.

## Feedback frameworks

### SBI (Situation-Behavior-Impact)

The most widely used structured feedback model. Specificity eliminates defensiveness.

```
Situation: "In yesterday's product review meeting..."
Behavior: "...you interrupted the engineer three times before they finished presenting."
Impact: "...the team didn't hear the technical constraints, and we made a decision without full information."
```

**Why SBI works**: It separates observation from judgment. You're describing what happened, not what kind of person they are.

### Radical Candor (Kim Scott)

Two dimensions: **Care Personally** × **Challenge Directly**. The matrix:

| | Low Challenge | High Challenge |
|---|---|---|
| **High Care** | Ruinous Empathy (sugarcoating) | Radical Candor ✓ |
| **Low Care** | Manipulative Insincerity (passive-aggressive) | Obnoxious Aggression (brutal) |

Most managers default to Ruinous Empathy — they care about the person but avoid difficult feedback. The skill is learning to challenge directly *because* you care.

## Calibration

The process of aligning performance ratings across managers to ensure consistency and fairness.

**How it works**: Managers present their ratings to a calibration group (typically skip-level manager + HR + peer managers). Each rating is discussed, especially top and bottom ratings. The group adjusts ratings to ensure consistency across teams.

**Why it matters**: Without calibration, rating distributions reflect manager generosity, not employee performance. Some managers are harsh, some are generous. Calibration corrects for this.

**Best practices**:
- Require written evidence for all top and bottom ratings before calibration
- Force-rank within calibration groups (uncomfortable but effective)
- Use the full rating scale — if 90% of ratings are "meets expectations," the system isn't working
- Document calibration decisions — these create the paper trail for promotion and termination decisions

## Performance Improvement Plans (PIPs)

A PIP is a formal, documented process for an employee who is underperforming. In practice, PIPs serve two purposes:

1. **Genuine improvement** (~20% of cases): The employee didn't know they were underperforming, the PIP provides clarity, and they improve.
2. **Documentation for exit** (~80% of cases): The manager has already decided the person needs to leave, and the PIP creates the legal documentation trail.

**PIP structure**:
- **Clear gap statement**: What is expected vs. what is happening (specific, measurable)
- **Improvement plan**: What the employee needs to do differently (specific actions, not vague goals)
- **Support offered**: Training, mentoring, reduced scope, 1:1 frequency increase
- **Timeline**: Typically 30–60 days (30 days is standard for clear-cut cases)
- **Success criteria**: Measurable outcomes that constitute "passing" the PIP
- **Consequence**: Explicit statement that failure to meet criteria may result in termination

**Legal considerations**: The PIP document becomes a legal artifact. Write it as if a lawyer will read it. Be factual, not emotional. Document all check-in conversations during the PIP period.

## Managing underperformers out

When the decision is to exit an employee:

1. **Document**: Performance issues, feedback given, PIP results, all meetings (dates, attendees, what was discussed)
2. **Involve legal early**: Especially for employees in protected categories, employees in jurisdictions with strong labor protections (most of MENA, Europe, Singapore), or employees who have raised complaints
3. **Prepare the separation agreement**: Severance, benefits continuation, equity treatment, reference agreement, non-disparagement
4. **Deliver with dignity**: Private meeting, clear and brief explanation, HR present, logistics handled (access revocation, equipment return, final pay timing)
5. **Communicate to team**: Brief, respectful, forward-looking. Don't over-explain. "X has moved on, we wish them well, here's how their work is covered."
