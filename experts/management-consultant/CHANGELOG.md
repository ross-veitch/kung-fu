## 1. Key Insights

**1. OpenAI's strategic position is weaker than its brand suggests — and the incumbents are the real story.**
Benedict Evans (Feb 19) makes a pointed observation: OpenAI lacks proprietary technology advantage, has thin engagement (usage only a couple times weekly), and faces incumbents who have matched the core capability *and* have superior distribution. The strategic implication isn't "OpenAI is doomed" — it's that **distribution moats are reasserting themselves faster than the disruption cycle completed**. For clients asking "who wins in AI," the honest answer is increasingly "whoever already has the customer relationship." This updates my prior that foundation model providers would hold durable leverage.

**2. LLMs may dissolve the network effect barrier in search/recommendations — a genuinely structural shift.**
Evans (Feb 23) raises the possibility that LLMs enable building competitive search and recommendation systems *without* a massive user base. If true, this is a direct attack on one of the most durable 7 Powers advantages (network economies) in the digital economy. This is early-stage thinking, not settled, but it's the kind of structural question that belongs in any AI strategy engagement.

**3. Apple is playing a different strategic game than everyone else in AI — and it may be the right one.**
Evans (Feb 2026) frames Apple's positioning as treating LLMs as commodity infrastructure rather than platform or product. This is textbook **counter-positioning** (Helmer): Apple doesn't need to win the model race; it needs to prevent the model race from commoditizing *its* layer. Worth tracking whether this holds as on-device capability improves.

**4. Hotel software is differentiating within the AI disruption narrative, not just surviving it.**
PhocusWire (March 2) via HotelTechReport argues hotel software is an *unlikely winner* in the AI reset — specific applications are proving resilient or enhanced rather than displaced. This is a useful corrective to the generic "AI disrupts everything" framing I'd apply to hospitality tech clients. The nuance: **workflow-embedded software with high switching costs weathers AI resets better than standalone tools**.

**5. Indonesia's travel market is a concrete emerging-market AI adoption data point, not just a forecast.**
PhocusWire (March 2): 51% of Indonesians already using AI for travel research, 35% for personalized recommendations. A $17B market projection with *demonstrated* AI behavior change (not projected) is a stronger signal than most emerging market forecasts. This is benchmarkable data for clients assessing Southeast Asia digital strategy.

**6. Accessible tourism is attracting serious capital — Wheel the World's $11M Series A signals a maturing niche.**
PhocusWire (Feb 25): $3.5M fresh capital in the round suggests this isn't purely a social-impact play. Investors are pricing a real market. For hospitality clients, this is an early signal that **accessibility as a product feature is transitioning from compliance to competitive differentiation**.

## 2. What's Changing (Early Signals, Not Headlines Yet)

**The AI hiring shift in travel is about *role redefinition*, not headcount reduction — and that's the harder problem.**
PhocusWire (Feb 24) frames AI's impact on travel hiring as a mindset shift, not a layoff story. The harder consulting problem emerging here is: *what does a travel company's org design look like when AI handles the research/recommendation layer?* No one has a clean answer yet. Watch for the first major OTA to publish a restructured job architecture — that will set the benchmark.

**OpenAI's platform dilemma is a preview of every enterprise AI vendor's problem.**
Evans' framing — OpenAI must either invent everything or partner broadly — is actually the strategic trap facing every enterprise AI point solution right now. Clients building on top of foundation models face the same "platform or feature?" question. This is moving from theoretical to urgent as incumbents (Microsoft, Google, Salesforce) accelerate embedding.

**The a16z post (March 1, referenced in 7 Powers summary) applying Helmer's framework to AI disrupting application software is worth tracking directly.** The 7 Powers source flagged it but it fell outside the two-week window. The intersection of a rigorous competitive moat framework with AI disruption of SaaS is exactly the analytical territory clients are asking about. This is a gap in my current reading.

## 3. Ideas → Skills/Commands

**Update AI strategy diagnostic command:**
Add a "distribution moat stress test" step. Current framing asks "what's the AI capability advantage?" — Evans' OpenAI analysis suggests the better question is "who controls the customer relationship and switching cost stack?" Revise the command to force explicit assessment of incumbent distribution advantages before evaluating AI-native challengers.

**Add a "7 Powers × AI disruption" overlay to competitive analysis templates:**
The a16z framing (referenced in sources) and Evans' network effects point both suggest that different Powers are being disrupted at different rates by AI. Network economies (search/recommendations) are under attack; switching costs (hotel software) are holding. Build a simple matrix: [Power type] × [AI disruption risk: high/medium/low] × [evidence]. This would sharpen client conversations immediately.

**Emerging markets digital strategy — add Indonesia as a concrete benchmark case:**
Current domain knowledge files likely treat Southeast Asia as a single block. The Indonesia data (51% AI research adoption) is specific enough to use as a calibration point. Add it as a named benchmark alongside more commonly cited markets.

**Revise the "who wins in AI" framing in client-facing materials:**
Replace "foundation model providers vs. application layer" binary with a three-way: (1) infrastructure/commodity layer, (2) distribution incumbents embedding AI, (3) new-experience inventors (Evans' third category — things not yet invented). The third category is underweighted in most consulting frameworks right now.

## 5. Questions Raised

**1. If LLMs dissolve network effect barriers in search/recommendations (Evans, Feb 23), which of Helmer's other six Powers are similarly vulnerable — and which are actually *strengthened* by AI?**
My hypothesis: switching costs and process power are strengthened (AI embeds deeper into workflows); network economies and cornered resources are most vulnerable. Needs stress-testing against real cases.

**2. OpenAI's engagement data (usage only a couple times weekly) — is this a product problem or a category problem?**
If it's a category problem (AI assistants are inherently episodic tools), then the entire "AI as daily driver" thesis underlying many enterprise AI investments is weaker than assumed. Worth investigating whether enterprise usage patterns differ materially from consumer.

**3. What does the hotel software "AI resilience" thesis actually rest on — switching costs, workflow integration, or data moats?**
The PhocusWire/HotelTechReport piece argues hotel software wins in the AI reset, but the mechanism matters enormously for advising clients. If it's switching costs, that's durable. If it's just current integration complexity, it's temporary.

**4. Is the Indonesia AI adoption data (51%) self-reported survey data or behavioral data?**
The number is striking enough that the methodology matters before using it in client work. Survey-reported AI usage consistently overstates actual behavior change. Need to check the primary source before citing.
