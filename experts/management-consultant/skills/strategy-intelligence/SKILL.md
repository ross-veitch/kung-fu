# Strategy Intelligence

## Problem structuring

### The decision statement

The single most important move in strategy work. Most "strategy problems" arrive as one of:
- A vague anxiety ("our growth is slowing")
- A solution in search of a problem ("should we expand to Saudi Arabia?")
- A symptom without a cause ("our NPS is declining")

Convert all of these to a decision statement before any analysis:
> *"Should [actor] do [action], given [context], decided by [date]?"*

If you can't write this sentence, you don't have a strategy problem — you have a research project. Stop and get the decision statement.

### MECE issue trees

**Mutually exclusive**: branches don't overlap (if hotel economics and flight economics are separate branches, OTA package economics belongs in one, not both)
**Collectively exhaustive**: together they cover the full decision space (if branches cover market attractiveness, competitive position, and financial viability — does that cover everything that could change the answer? Check.)

**Common templates** (adapt, don't use verbatim):

*Should we enter market X?*
- Market attractiveness (size, growth, margin pool)
- Competitive position (can we win vs incumbents and new entrants?)
- Strategic fit (does this compound our existing advantages or dilute them?)
- Execution feasibility (do we have or can we build required capabilities?)
- Financial return (is the NPV positive under realistic scenarios?)

*Why is growth declining?*
- Acquisition decline (fewer new customers) → channel efficiency / market saturation / competitive pressure
- Activation decline (customers not converting) → product, onboarding, pricing
- Retention decline (customers churning) → product, support, competitive alternatives, price elasticity

## Hypothesis design

**Principle**: form a point of view first. Research to test it, not to form it.

A good hypothesis is:
- Stated as an assertion, not a question
- Specific enough to be falsifiable
- Decision-relevant: if true, it changes the recommendation

**Ranking hypotheses**:
- Impact if true: High / Medium / Low (what does it do to the decision?)
- Ease of validation: Easy / Medium / Hard (can we test it in days, weeks, or months?)

Prioritise: High impact + Easy to validate → resolve first. High impact + Hard to validate → use judgment or bound the uncertainty via scenario analysis.

## Competitive strategy

### 7 Powers (Helmer) — the only moat framework worth using

1. **Scale economies**: unit cost falls as volume grows; competitors face permanent cost disadvantage at smaller scale. *Example: AWS infrastructure, Booking.com marketing machine.*
2. **Network effects**: product becomes more valuable as users/participants grow. *Example: Airbnb supply density, LinkedIn profiles.* Can be same-side (user-user) or cross-side (supply-demand). Cross-side is more common and more durable in marketplaces.
3. **Switching costs**: costs (time, data, workflow) of switching to alternative are high enough to deter rational customers. *Example: Salesforce CRM data migration, ERP systems.*
4. **Counter-positioning**: new business model that incumbents can't copy without damaging their existing business. *Example: Netflix vs Blockbuster (streaming vs late fees), Expedia disrupting offline travel agents.*
5. **Branding**: customer pays premium beyond functional quality because of what the brand represents. Requires sustained investment; takes years to build; destroyed quickly by poor quality or scandal. *Example: American Express, Four Seasons.*
6. **Cornered resource**: exclusive access to a scarce, valuable asset. *Example: Booking.com hotel density, Amadeus GDS data.*
7. **Process power**: organisational process that is genuinely hard to replicate, embedded through years of iteration. *Example: Toyota production system, Amazon supply chain.*

**Test**: which specific power does this strategy build toward? If the answer is "none," the strategy creates only temporary advantage.

## Scenario modeling

### Structure

Every scenario model needs:
1. **Named scenarios** (not Base/Bear/Bull — name them after the worldview they represent)
2. **Key assumption per scenario**: the 1–2 assumptions that define the scenario
3. **Financial outputs**: revenue, margin, cash at 12/24/36 months
4. **Strategic implications**: what decisions does each scenario require?
5. **Trigger signal**: what observable event would confirm we're in this scenario?

### Common scenario families

**Demand scenarios**: market grows faster/slower than expected; recession hits; category disruption
**Competitive scenarios**: major player enters; incumbent responds aggressively; consolidation
**Regulatory scenarios**: intervention on pricing/data/content; new licensing requirement
**Technology scenarios**: new capability changes cost structure or customer behavior; AI disrupts distribution

### Sensitivity analysis

For each scenario, identify the 2–3 inputs that most affect the outcome. Run sensitivity table:
- Revenue sensitivity to 10% CAC increase
- Margin sensitivity to 5pp take-rate compression
- Cash sensitivity to 3-month delay in product launch

## Capital allocation

### Expected value framework

For each initiative under consideration:

**EV = Σ (probability × outcome) across scenarios**

Inputs:
- What is the upside outcome (magnitude + probability)?
- What is the base case (magnitude + probability)?
- What is the downside (magnitude + probability)?
- What is the NPV of expected cash flows at cost of capital?

**Reversibility adjustment**: subtract a premium for irreversible bets (can't unwind). Add an option value for reversible ones (can cut losses early).

**Portfolio logic**: don't evaluate initiatives in isolation. Evaluate the portfolio:
- Correlation: initiatives that all fail in the same scenario provide less diversification than they appear
- Sequencing: some initiatives create the capabilities needed for others (sequence matters)
- Capacity: total initiative portfolio vs execution bandwidth (most companies are 2–3× overcommitted)

## Executive synthesis

### The Minto Pyramid

Board-ready narrative always goes **top-down**:
1. **Conclusion / Recommendation** (first sentence)
2. **3 supporting arguments** (why this conclusion is correct)
3. **Evidence** beneath each argument

Never build up to the conclusion. Busy executives read the first sentence and decide whether to keep reading.

### Compression test

If you can't explain the recommendation in three sentences, you don't understand it yet. Keep compressing until the structure is clear, then expand with evidence for the audience that needs it.

### Board language

- Replace "we believe" with "the data shows" or "our analysis indicates"
- Replace "significant" with a number
- Replace "opportunity" with a specific market, customer, and revenue estimate
- Replace "we need to move fast" with "our window is X months because Y"
- Every ask from the board should be specific: "we need $Xm approved by [date] to [do Y], which enables [Z outcome]"
