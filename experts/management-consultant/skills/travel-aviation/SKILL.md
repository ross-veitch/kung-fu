# Travel & Aviation Economics

## OTA economics

### Revenue model mechanics

**Agency model**: OTA earns commission on completed booking. Revenue = GMV × commission rate. Commission: hotel 15–25%, flight 3–7%, car 10–15%. Risk: none (no inventory ownership). Margin pressure: direct booking push from suppliers; Google Hotel Ads disintermediation.

**Merchant model**: OTA buys inventory at net rate and sells at retail. Spread = revenue. Higher margin potential but inventory and cancellation risk. More common in hotel (packaging, opaque) than flight.

**Key OTA P&L ratios** (indicative, large-scale player):
- Take rate (net revenue / GMV): hotel 10–15%, flight 3–5%, overall blended 8–12%
- Marketing as % of revenue: 40–60% (dominates cost structure; Google dependency is existential)
- Contribution margin after marketing: 30–50%
- EBITDA margin: 20–35% at scale (booking.com/Expedia level)
- Customer acquisition cost (hotel): $20–80 depending on channel
- Repeat booking rate (mature players): 50–65% of bookings from repeat customers

### The Google dependency problem
Search (Google Hotel Ads, Google Flights) drives 40–60% of OTA acquisition for many players. CPC inflation has compressed margins industry-wide. Players building loyalty/direct as structural hedge: Booking's Genius program, Expedia One Key, IHG/Hilton member rates.

**Counter-positions available**:
- Loyalty programs that shift demand to direct
- Niche/vertical focus that reduces Google competition (eg: luxury, niche destinations)
- Supply-side exclusives (rates/inventory not available on Google)
- App-first strategy (reduces Google dependency; loyalty loop in app)

## Aviation economics

### Core airline metrics

**Load factor** = seats filled / seats available. Industry average ~82–85% (post-pandemic recovery). At ~75–80%, most legacy carriers cover cash costs. Below 70% → acute pressure on yield.

**Yield** = revenue per RPK (revenue passenger kilometre). Moves inversely with supply growth. When load factors are high, airlines can yield-manage aggressively (last-seat pricing). When LF is low, they fill seats with discounts → yield collapses.

**RASK** (Revenue per Available Seat Kilometre) = total revenue / ASK. Combines load factor and yield into one metric. Best top-line efficiency measure across airlines of different scale.

**CASK** (Cost per Available Seat Kilometre) = total costs / ASK. Key cost drivers: fuel (25–30%), labour (25–30%), distribution (5–10%), maintenance, airport fees. Low-cost carriers achieve CASK 30–40% below legacy by: point-to-point routing, single fleet type, ancillary-heavy revenue mix, no GDS distribution.

**Ancillary revenue**: LCCs derive 40–55% of total revenue from ancillaries (bags, seat selection, meals, priority boarding). Legacy carriers typically 15–25%. Ancillary growth is structural — no sign of reversal.

### Distribution economics (airline)

**GDS** (Amadeus, Sabre, Travelport): Full content, complex itineraries, corporate booking tools. Cost to airline: $7–12 per segment. Declining share (was 60%+ of OTA bookings; now 40–50%) due to NDC.

**NDC** (New Distribution Capability): IATA standard for direct API distribution. Allows airlines to offer dynamic pricing, ancillaries, and bundles. Cost to airline: $1–3 per segment vs GDS. Adoption: 30–50% of bookings on NDC-capable airlines by 2025, rising.

**Direct channel** (airline.com + app): Lowest distribution cost. Strategic priority for all major carriers. Friction: not available in corporate booking tools without NDC integration. Growing via loyalty-driven demand.

**Metasearch** (Google Flights, Kayak, Skyscanner): CPC model. Good for price-transparent commodities (point-to-point LCC); weaker for complex itineraries. OTAs competing directly here.

## Marketplace liquidity

### Supply-demand balance

Travel marketplaces (hotel OTA, airline metasearch, experiences) face classic two-sided market dynamics:

**Supply-side constraints**:
- Hotel: inventory is geographic (can't ship a hotel room); local supply depth determines LF
- Flight: slot constraints at major hubs; seasonal capacity decisions locked months ahead
- Experiences: highly fragmented, low digitization, long-tail content challenge

**Demand-side constraints**:
- Seasonality (2–5x peak vs trough volume on leisure routes)
- Price elasticity (flight much higher than hotel in most segments)
- Intent signal lag: search → book window varies 3–90 days by segment

**Liquidity thresholds**: A destination needs minimum viable supply (typically 50–100 bookable options) before demand-side investment (marketing) pays off. Reverse is also true: supply won't list if demand isn't there. Cold start requires subsidy to one side.

### Take-rate design
- Too high: suppliers build direct; incentivize disintermediation
- Too low: insufficient margin to fund demand-side marketing; marketplace starves
- Optimal: match-fund marketing spend from take rate; sustainable at scale
- Dynamic pricing on take rate is emerging (lower rate for new suppliers; higher for exclusive content)

## Destination ecosystem strategy

**Airlift development**: Demand before capacity. Airlines won't add routes unless load factor forecasts support it. Approach: (1) demonstrate latent demand via booking data/waiting lists, (2) reduce airline cost/risk via DMO incentives (airport fee waivers, minimum revenue guarantees), (3) target airlines where the route fills a network gap (6th freedom, banking hub).

**Capacity sequencing**: Start with a carrier willing to test. Prove the route with 1–2 flights/week. Once load factors demonstrate viability, negotiate for daily service. Secondary carriers follow primary.

**Tourism infrastructure timing**: Infrastructure (hotels, transport) has long build cycles (3–7 years). Airlift decisions have 12–18 month lead times. Destination governments must sequence: target investment → airlift commitment → infrastructure → marketing → volume. Doing marketing before airlift is wasted spend.
