# Lifecycle Marketing

The discipline of communicating with users across their journey — from acquisition through activation, retention, monetisation, and referral — using the right message, channel, and timing.

---

## The lifecycle model

Every user moves through stages. The goal of lifecycle marketing is to accelerate movement through the right stages and prevent drop-off at critical points.

```
Acquisition → Activation → Engagement → Retention → Monetisation → Referral
```

Each stage has different goals, messaging strategies, and channels.

---

## Activation

The most leveraged stage. A user who activates (reaches first value moment) is dramatically more likely to retain.

**First value moment (FVM)**: Define it precisely. For Spotify: playing first song. For Airbnb: booking first trip. For a B2B SaaS: first core action. Your onboarding flow should be a relentless march toward FVM.

**Onboarding best practices**:
- Progressive disclosure: don't show everything at once. Surface features contextually at the moment of relevance.
- Value before friction: delay account creation, permission requests, and payment until after FVM where possible.
- Jobs-to-be-done framing: ask what the user is trying to accomplish (not what features they want). Then guide them there.
- Time-to-value (TTV): minimise it. Every extra step reduces conversion.

**Channels**: In-app tooltips and checklists, welcome email series (D0/D1/D3/D7), push notifications (if opted in), in-app messages (Intercom, Braze), SMS for transactional moments.

---

## Retention

Habit formation and value expansion. Retained users are the foundation of sustainable unit economics.

**Retention metrics**:
- D1 / D7 / D30 / D90 retention curves — what % of users are still active?
- L28 (28-day active) — the current standard for mobile app health
- Monthly active users (MAU) vs daily active users (DAU) — DAU/MAU ratio indicates stickiness (0.5+ is excellent; 0.1–0.2 is typical)

**Retention mechanics**:
- Content/value loops: users who return find fresh value each visit (social feeds, marketplace inventory, content)
- Variable reward schedules (Hooked model): unpredictable rewards (likes, deals) drive habit loops more effectively than predictable ones
- Investment / sunk cost: user-generated content, saved searches, booking history, points balances — the more a user puts in, the more they're locked in
- Push notifications: high-leverage but high-risk; over-notification drives churn; personalised and triggered notifications dramatically outperform broadcast
- Email engagement programmes: weekly digests, price drop alerts, re-engagement for dormant users

**Churn prevention**: Identify early warning signals (declining session frequency, reduced depth of engagement). Trigger intervention (offer, feature highlight, support outreach) before churn, not after.

---

## Monetisation

Converting engagement into revenue. The best lifecycle marketers increase monetisation without degrading the user experience.

**Upsell and cross-sell**: Timed relative to activation and demonstrated value. Upsell after user has experienced FVM and established a habit. Contextual triggers (booking a flight → upsell hotel) outperform generic campaigns.

**Pricing experiments**: Lifecycle is one of the key channels for testing pricing. A/B test price points, discount levels, and bundle offers — measure not just conversion but LTV impact.

---

## Win-back

Re-engaging users who have churned or become dormant.

**Segments**: Distinguish lapsing (at risk) from lapsed (churned). Different interventions apply. Lapsing users respond to re-engagement; fully churned users need a compelling reason to return.

**Win-back sequence**: Typically 3 emails over 2–3 weeks. First: value reminder or "we miss you." Second: social proof or new features. Third: explicit incentive (discount/offer). Sunset after no response.

**Channel**: Email is the primary channel for win-back (push notifications may not reach churned users who uninstalled; email is more persistent). SMS for high-LTV segments.

---

## Referral programme design

The best referral programmes are growth loops: each new user brings more users.

**Classic two-sided referral**: Sender and receiver both receive value (e.g., Dropbox: 500MB for you, 500MB for them). Incentive must be directly tied to the core product value.

**Key design decisions**: incentive type (credit vs cash vs product benefit — product benefit tends to attract higher-quality users), one-sided vs two-sided, share mechanic (link, code, social), referral virality coefficient (K-factor = % who refer × how many they refer).

**Anti-gaming measures**: Restrict same-device redemption, minimum usage threshold before incentive is awarded, fraud signals.
