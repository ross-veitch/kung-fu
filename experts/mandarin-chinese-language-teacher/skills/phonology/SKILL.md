# Mandarin Phonology: Tones, Pinyin, and Pronunciation

## Why tones are non-negotiable

Mandarin is a tonal language: the same syllable, spoken with a different tone, is a completely different word. This is not optional or advanced — it is the foundation. Skipping tones early creates habits that are very difficult to fix later.

The four tones of standard Mandarin (Putonghua), plus the neutral tone:

| Tone | Number | Pinyin mark | Description | Mnemonic |
|------|--------|------------|-------------|----------|
| First | 1 | ā | High, flat, level | Holding a musical note |
| Second | 2 | á | Rising (like a question in English) | "Really?" |
| Third | 3 | ǎ | Dipping (low, dips down then rises) | Hesitation "well..." |
| Fourth | 4 | à | Falling sharply (decisive, emphatic) | "Stop!" |
| Neutral | 0 | a | Short, light, unstressed | Varies by preceding syllable |

**Classic minimal pair**: 妈(mā) = mother | 马(mǎ) = horse | 骂(mà) = to scold. Getting the tone wrong doesn't produce an accent — it produces a different word.

## The pinyin system

Pinyin is the official romanisation system for Mandarin. It is the standard for input methods, textbooks, and pronunciation notation. Every learner must master it.

**Pinyin is not intuitive for English speakers.** Key differences:

| Pinyin | English approximation | Common mistake |
|--------|----------------------|----------------|
| x | Between 's' and 'sh' (front of mouth) | Pronounced as 'sh' |
| q | Like 'ch' but front of mouth | Pronounced as 'ch' or 'k' |
| zh | Like 'j' but retroflex (tongue back) | Confused with 'j' |
| c | Like 'ts' in "cats" | Pronounced as 'k' or 's' |
| z | Like 'dz' in "adze" | Pronounced as 'z' |
| r | Nothing in English — retroflex, between 'r' and 'zh' | Most difficult for English speakers |
| ui | Actually pronounced 'uei' | Pronounced as English 'oo-ee' |
| iu | Actually pronounced 'iou' | Compressed vowel |

**Finals that catch learners**:
- `-an` vs `-ang`: "an" has a shorter, fronter vowel; "ang" is backed and open
- `-en` vs `-eng`: similar distinction, common source of errors
- `-in` vs `-ing`: "-ing" in Mandarin has no final 'g' sound — it's a nasalised vowel

## Tone sandhi (tone changes)

Tones don't always stay the same in connected speech:

**Third tone + third tone**: the first third tone becomes a rising (second) tone.
- 你好 (nǐ hǎo) is pronounced "ní hǎo" in practice, not "nǐ hǎo"

**一 (yī, "one") tone sandhi**:
- Before 4th tone: changes to 2nd tone (一样 yíyàng)
- Before 1st, 2nd, 3rd tones: changes to 4th tone (一天 yìtiān)

**不 (bù, "not") tone sandhi**:
- Before 4th tone: changes to 2nd tone (不是 búshì)

## Listening discrimination: building a tone ear

Most adult learners can produce tones correctly in isolation but struggle to hear and produce them at natural speech speed. Remedies:

1. **Minimal pair drills**: listen to pairs of words that differ only by tone (mā/má/mǎ/mà) and identify which you hear. Do this before producing.

2. **Shadowing**: listen to native speech and simultaneously repeat what you hear — including prosody, tone, rhythm. Don't translate or think about meaning.

3. **Record yourself**: compare your production to a native speaker. Tones that sound correct to you often don't match on recording.

4. **Spaced repetition with audio**: use Anki cards with audio rather than text-only cards. Train your ear, not just your eyes.

## Simplified vs Traditional characters

| System | Used in | Characters | Notes |
|--------|---------|-----------|-------|
| Simplified | Mainland China, Singapore | ~2,500 common characters | Reduced stroke counts, some merged characters |
| Traditional | Taiwan, Hong Kong, Macau, diaspora | ~5,000+ common characters | Preserves historical forms |

**For Putonghua learners**: Simplified is the standard for mainland China business and most international Mandarin contexts. Start with Simplified unless Traditional is a specific goal.

**Can you learn both?** Yes, but learn one first. Simplified → Traditional is generally easier than the reverse. At advanced level, reading both is expected for serious learners.
