# /editorial-brief — Brief on a topic for coverage planning

**Purpose**: Produce a structured editorial brief that maps the landscape of a topic — what is known, what the key angles are, which sources to monitor, and what coverage would add value.

**When to use**: When planning coverage of a developing story, emerging trend, or topic area — before writing, to ensure the coverage is well-directed and not redundant with what already exists.

---

## Phase 1: Define the editorial question

Before researching, frame what we are trying to understand:

1. **What is the topic?** (Specific: "Impact of EU AI Act on travel tech companies" not vague: "AI regulation")
2. **Why now?** (What triggered interest — a new development, an upcoming event, a pattern emerging?)
3. **Who is the audience?** (This determines depth, angle, and technical level)
4. **What coverage already exists?** (Quick scan: has this been well-covered? Is our angle fresh?)

The editorial question should be one sentence: "What should [audience] understand about [topic] given [context]?"

---

## Phase 2: Landscape scan

Map the information landscape in 30–60 minutes:

### Current state of reporting
- **Who has covered this?** (List major outlets and their angle)
- **What is the consensus narrative?** (What does most coverage say?)
- **Where are the gaps?** (What has not been said? What angle is missing?)
- **What are the contrarian views?** (Who disagrees with the consensus, and why?)

### Key actors and stakeholders
- **Decision-makers**: Who has power to change the situation?
- **Affected parties**: Who wins, who loses?
- **Experts**: Who has credible domain knowledge? (Academics, practitioners, analysts)
- **Commentators**: Who is shaping public opinion? (Media voices, influencers, think tanks)

### Timeline and trajectory
- **How did we get here?** (Key milestones, 3–5 bullet points)
- **Where is this heading?** (Likely scenarios in the next 1–3 months)
- **What are the inflection points?** (Events or decisions that could change the trajectory — elections, regulatory deadlines, earnings releases)

---

## Phase 3: Identify the angle

An editorial brief is not a research dump — it recommends a specific angle for coverage.

### Angle selection criteria
- **Fresh**: Not already well-covered by other outlets
- **Relevant**: Directly matters to the audience
- **Defensible**: Supported by evidence, not just opinion
- **Specific**: Narrow enough to cover well in one piece

### Common angle types
- **The explainer**: "Here's what's actually happening and why it matters" (best when the topic is complex and poorly understood)
- **The contrarian**: "The consensus narrative is wrong, here's why" (best when there is genuine evidence against the prevailing view)
- **The impact analysis**: "Here's how this affects [specific audience]" (best for niche audiences)
- **The prediction**: "Here's what happens next" (best when the trajectory is unclear and analysis adds value)
- **The investigation**: "Here's what nobody is talking about" (best when there is a genuine information gap)

### Red flags for bad angles
- "Everything you need to know about X" — too broad, no editorial point of view
- Restating what wire services have already reported — no added value
- Pure opinion without evidence — editorializing, not journalism
- Too inside-baseball for the audience — interesting to experts, irrelevant to readers

---

## Phase 4: Source plan

Identify the sources needed to execute the coverage:

### Primary sources (highest value)
- **People to interview**: Name, role, why their perspective matters, contact method
- **Documents to obtain**: Filings, reports, datasets, leaked materials
- **Data to analyze**: Public databases, company filings, government statistics

### Secondary sources (context and background)
- **Previous reporting**: Key articles that provide foundation (cite, don't replicate)
- **Academic research**: Papers or reports that provide analytical framework
- **Industry reports**: Market research, analyst notes, trade association data

### Sources to monitor (for developing stories)
- **RSS feeds**: Specific outlets covering this beat
- **Social media**: Key voices to follow for real-time updates
- **Government/regulatory**: Official channels for policy announcements
- **Company communications**: Investor relations pages, press releases, earnings calls

---

## Phase 5: Coverage recommendation

Synthesize the brief into a specific recommendation:

### Format options
- **News brief** (500 words): Fast turnaround, headline + context + implications
- **Analysis piece** (1,000–2,000 words): Deeper treatment, multiple sources, structured argument
- **Explainer** (1,500–2,500 words): Educational, assumes reader has no background
- **Series**: Multiple pieces over time, tracking a developing story
- **Digest inclusion**: Not worth a standalone piece, but relevant for the next scheduled briefing

### Timing
- **Immediate**: Story is time-sensitive, publish within hours
- **This week**: Story is developing, aim for next 3–5 days
- **When ready**: Not time-sensitive, quality over speed
- **Hold**: Story is not ready — missing key information, wait for next development

```
- Interview: [Name, role, contact]

Estimated effort: [Hours to produce]
```
