# Editorial Judgment

## The core question: significance vs noise

Every story that crosses your desk requires one question before anything else: *will this matter in six months?*

News that passes: geopolitical shifts that restructure alliances, technology transitions that change competitive dynamics, regulatory changes with industry-wide impact, corporate actions that reflect structural trends.

News that fails: press releases dressed as news, viral social media stories without independent verification, incremental updates to ongoing stories that add no new information, opinion pieces misframed as reporting.

The editor's job is not to be comprehensive. It is to be right about what matters.

## The significance framework

Score each potential story across three axes:

**1. Impact magnitude**: If this development plays out fully, how many people/businesses/dollars are affected?
- Local/niche → general/systemic (1–5 scale)

**2. Novelty**: Is this genuinely new information, or is it a restatement of what is already known?
- Restatement/follow-up → first reporting of a structural change (1–5 scale)

**3. Actionability**: Does knowing this change what the reader should do or think?
- Background colour → decision-relevant (1–5 scale)

Total ≥ 10 out of 15: include. 7–9: include if space allows. < 7: skip.

## Source evaluation hierarchy

Not all sources are equal. Know the hierarchy:

**Tier 1**: Primary sources — company filings, government documents, court records, official press releases (note: these reflect the issuer's view, not truth)

**Tier 2**: Wire services (Reuters, AP, Bloomberg) — fast, fact-focused, minimal editorialising. Strong verification standards.

**Tier 3**: Specialist trade press (Skift, PhocusWire, TechCrunch, FT) — domain expertise, deeper analysis, slower than wire.

**Tier 4**: Generalist press (NYT, Guardian, WSJ) — broader reach, variable depth on specialised topics.

**Tier 5**: Opinion/commentary — valuable for framing, worthless as news sources.

**Red flags**: Single-source stories, outlets with financial relationships with the companies they cover, "exclusively reports" from publications with a track record of later corrections.

## Verification standard

- **One source**: a lead to investigate, not a story to run
- **Two independent sources**: runnable, with sourcing noted
- **Primary document**: highest confidence

When running a single-source story because it's time-sensitive: clearly mark the sourcing ("According to [outlet], which has not been independently confirmed").

## Regional calibration

The same story has different significance depending on geography and readership. Develop a mental model of your reader's context:

- **Oil price movements**: globally significant, especially acute in MENA economies
- **US interest rate decisions**: global capital flows, EM currency impact
- **China economic data**: supply chain, commodities, APAC travel demand
- **MENA political developments**: regional stability, investment climate, aviation route economics
- **Aviation/hospitality news**: direct relevance to travel industry professionals

A story about European aviation consolidation is a 3/10 for a Singapore-based executive and a 7/10 for someone running a MENA travel business.

## Briefing construction

A good briefing is:
- **Scannable**: headline → source → date → link. Nothing more needed unless the reader wants depth.
- **Ordered by significance**: most important first. Don't bury the lead.
- **Sectioned**: group by beat (geopolitics / tech / industry / regional). Readers often want one section only.
- **Dated clearly**: a story from 3 days ago is not "today's news". All items should include publication date.
- **Honest about gaps**: if a beat had no significant news this period, say so. Don't pad with low-signal stories.
